| Under Discussion: A Dearth of Democracy<br/>This piece is part of an Under Discussion series. To read more about this week’s topic and see more pieces from this series visit Under Discussion: Democracy’s Demands.<br/>At what is arguably the most powerful level of our government, there is the least democracy. The words “government of the people, by the people, and for the people” rings largely hollow, when seen in the light of the possibilities of democracy. This observation is not countered by the pedantic point that the United States was not conceived of as a democracy, but instead as a republic. Even conceding this point, as a democracy of indirect representation the United States is not particularly democratic.<br/>The federal government is more removed from the direct control of the people than are the governments of the various states. This is by design. Originally, the federal government was conceived of more as the forum in which the states themselves, rather than the citizens thereof, advocated for their interests. Members of the US Senate were chosen by the legislatures of the states they represented, rather than directly by the people of those states. The President of the United States was chosen by electors, which in most states were not selected by popular vote but by the legislature of that state. Moreover, the number of electors allocated to a given state was based on that state’s number of representatives in US Congress. Hence, all of the federal government, except the US House of Representatives, constituted an at least doubly-indirect democracy.<br/>To demonstrate this point, it helps to spell-out different mechanisms of democracy. We’ll talk about two mechanisms of indirect democracy, and two of direct democracy. The first mechanism of indirect democracy is what most people in the US likely think of when they talk about a democratic process — election. To state the obvious, election is the mechanism by which we pick someone to act as our representative in some role. As we’ll see later, elections can be conducted in manners that lead to a more or less representative outcome. The corresponding mechanism to election is recall, by which people vote to remove someone from a role for which they were earlier elected. A recall election is followed by, and sometimes combined with, a special election to fill the seat vacated by the official who was recalled. Retention elections, in which voters determine whether a judge remains in office, are used in several states and are a kind of hybrid election-recall mechanism.<br/>The mechanisms of direct democracy are powers which people in indirect democracies vest in elected officials: initiative and referendum. Initiative is the power to propose and enact laws: i.e., legislative power. Ballot initiatives are the relatively rare occasions in which voters exert some direct control over what laws are enacted. The power of referendum is to initiative what recall is to election: it is the means by which enacted laws are repealed. In practice, the terms ‘initiative’ and ‘referendum’ are both used primarily to refer to legislative proposals that are voted on directly by voters. There are other, non-legislative powers that in theory could be assigned to voters at large. However, we’ll stipulate for the purposes of this discussion that it is a bad idea to give executive (enforcing the law) and judicial (interpreting the law) powers to the general electorate.<br/>At the federal level, there is no popular initiative or referendum. All federal legislation is promulgated, amended, or repealed by the initiative of US Congress. Nor are any federal officials subject to recall. (Federal judges are not subject to retention elections, as they serve during “good behavior.”) Hence three of the four mechanisms of democracy — and both of the mechanisms of direct democracy — are unavailable at the federal level. This leaves only election. The federal government is less removed from popular control than it was originally. US Senators have been elected by popular vote since the 17th Amendment to the US Constitution, ratified in 1913. Further, every state apportions its electoral votes for president on the basis of popular vote. However, discontent with the federal government has led to numerous calls to reform these entities or the way they’re elected. In some cases, people even call for the abolition of the electoral college or US Senate. These institutions and the way their members are selected do not necessarily represent the varied interests of US citizens and their diverse values. Many reformers look to parliamentary countries (e.g., New Zealand) that make use of systems like proportional representation and ranked-choice voting for inspiration. Some US states make limited use of some mix of these enhanced democratic mechanisms. But by-and-large, the various states of the US recapitulate the limited and indirect forms of democracy found at the federal level.<br/>As the United States prepares for a presidential election marked by extreme polarization and cautious attention from the rest of the world, there will be a lot of talk about the US system of government. The candidates will tout the US as a great democracy that will flourish under their leadership, and will warn of the degradation democracy faces at the hands of their opponent. It is important to keep in mind when evaluating all this rhetoric how little democracy the US has ever had, especially at the federal level. | Diskusní téma: Demokracie<br/>Tento článek je součástí série Under Discussion. Více se o tématu tohoto týdne dočtete na stránce Under Discussion: Democracy's Demands.<br/>Na tom, co je pravděpodobně nejmocnější úrovní naší vlády, je nejméně demokracie. Slova "vláda lidí, lidí a pro lidi" zní do značné míry prázdné, když je vidět ve světle možností demokracie. Tento postřeh není v rozporu s pedantským bodem, že Spojené státy nebyly chápány jako demokracie, ale spíše jako republika. I když to připouštíme, jako demokracie nepřímého zastoupení nejsou Spojené státy nijak zvlášť demokratické.<br/>Federální vláda je více vzdálena přímé kontrole lidí než vlády různých států. Původně byla federální vláda koncipována spíše jako fórum, ve kterém státy samy, spíše než jejich občané, obhajovaly své zájmy. Členové Senátu USA byli voleni zákonodárci států, které zastupovali, spíše než přímo lidmi těchto států. Prezident Spojených států byl vybrán voliči, které ve většině států nebyly vybrány lidovým hlasováním, ale legislativou tohoto státu. Navíc počet voličů přidělených danému státu byl založen na počtu zástupců tohoto státu v americkém Kongresu. Proto veškerá federální vláda, s výjimkou americké Sněmovny reprezentantů, představovala přinejmenším dvojnásob nepřímou demokracii.<br/>Abychom tento bod demonstrovali, pomáhá vyhláskovat různé mechanismy demokracie. Budeme hovořit o dvou mechanismech nepřímé demokracie a dvou o přímé demokracii. Prvním mechanismem nepřímé demokracie je to, co si většina lidí v USA pravděpodobně myslí, když mluví o demokratickém procesu - volbách. Abych uvedl zřejmé, volby jsou mechanismem, kterým si vybereme někoho, kdo bude v nějaké roli naším zástupcem. Jak uvidíme později, volby mohou být vedeny způsobem, který vede k více či méně reprezentativnímu výsledku. Odpovídajícím mechanismem voleb je odvolání, kdy lidé hlasují, aby odstranili někoho z role, pro kterou byli dříve zvoleni. Po odvolání volby je následován, a někdy v kombinaci s, zvláštní volby zaplnit místo uvolnil úředníka, který byl odvolán. Retenční volby, v nichž voliči určují, zda soudce zůstává ve funkci, jsou používány v několika státech a jsou jakýmsi hybridním mechanismem pro odvolání voleb.<br/>Mechanismy přímé demokracie jsou pravomoci, které lidé v nepřímých demokraciích vkládají do volených úředníků: iniciativa a referendum. Iniciativa je pravomoc navrhovat a přijímat zákony: tj. legislativní moc. Volební iniciativy jsou relativně vzácnými příležitostmi, kdy voliči mají určitou přímou kontrolu nad tím, jaké zákony jsou uzákoněny. Sílou referenda je iniciovat to, co je odvoláním na volby: je to prostředek, kterým jsou uzákoněné zákony zrušeny. V praxi se termíny "iniciativa" a "referendum" používají především k odkazu na legislativní návrhy, o nichž hlasují přímo voliči. Existují i jiné, nelegislativní pravomoci, které by teoreticky mohly být přiděleny voličům obecně. Pro účely této diskuse však stanovíme, že je špatný nápad dát obecným voličům výkonné (vynucování práva) a soudní (interpretace práva).<br/>Na federální úrovni neexistuje žádná lidová iniciativa nebo referendum. Všechny federální právní předpisy jsou vyhlášeny, pozměněny nebo zrušeny iniciativou amerického Kongresu. Ani federální úředníci nejsou předmětem odvolání. (Federální soudci nepodléhají retenčním volbám, protože slouží během "dobrého chování.") Proto jsou tři ze čtyř mechanismů demokracie - a oba mechanismy přímé demokracie - na federální úrovni nedostupné. To ponechává pouze volby. Federální vláda je méně odstraněna z lidové kontroly, než tomu bylo původně. Američtí senátoři byli zvoleni lidovým hlasováním od 17. dodatku k americké ústavě, ratifikované v roce 1913. Navíc každý stát rozděluje své volební hlasy pro prezidenta na základě lidového hlasování. Nespokojenost s federální vládou však vedla k četným výzvám k reformě těchto subjektů nebo způsobu, jakým jsou zvoleni. V některých případech lidé dokonce vyzývají ke zrušení volebního kolegia nebo amerického senátu. Tyto instituce a způsob, jakým jsou jejich členové vybíráni, nemusí nutně představovat různorodé zájmy amerických občanů a jejich rozmanité hodnoty. Mnoho reformátorů se dívá na parlamentní země (např. Nový Zéland), které využívají systémy, jako je poměrné zastoupení a hlasování s hlasovacím právem. Některé státy USA využívají jen omezeně některé z těchto posílených demokratických mechanismů. Ale jednotlivé státy USA rekapitulují omezené a nepřímé formy demokracie, které se nacházejí na federální úrovni.<br/>Jak se Spojené státy připravují na prezidentské volby poznamenané extrémní polarizací a opatrnou pozorností zbytku světa, bude se hodně mluvit o americkém systému vlády. Kandidáti budou vychvalovat USA jako velkou demokracii, která bude pod jejich vedením vzkvétat, a budou varovat před degradací, které demokracie čelí v rukou jejich oponentů. Při hodnocení všech těchto rétoriek je důležité mít na paměti, jak málo demokracie USA kdy měly, zejména na federální úrovni. |
| - Share this article<br/>- Subscribe to our newsletter<br/>City compost in India – from waste to healthy soil<br/>In India, over 377 million people live in almost 8,000 cities or towns. They generate 62 million tons of municipal solid waste annually, according to the country’s government. More than 80 per cent is disposed indiscriminately without treatment at dump yards in an unhygienic manner. In the Indian countryside, ecological sustainability of agriculture has been at risk due to an excessive use of chemical fertilisers and monoculture since the ‘Green Revolution’ led to a degradation of land. Carbon concentration in India has decreased to a minimum. This causes crucial problems as carbon improves soil health, releases nutrients and protects plants against harmful substances. More than half of India’s land is already reported to be degraded.<br/>From the countryside to the city and back<br/>One clever solution to both problems is city compost. Organic waste is collected in the cities, recycled and processed to compost and finally used as organic matter by farmers complementing the traditional farmyard manure. Thereby, carbon nutrient in the waste gets back to the soil to enhance agricultural production. The two Deutsche Gesellschaft für Internationale Zusammenarbeit (GIZ) projects “Green Innovation Centre for the Agriculture and Food Sector” and “Soil Protection and Rehabilitation for Food Security (ProSoil)” support the implementation. Both projects are part of the special initiative “One World – No Hunger” led by the German Federal Ministry for Economic Cooperation and Development (BMZ). GIZ develops business models such as the city compost model reaching the farmers and improving their soils, builds capacities for quality assurance of compost and conducts pilot interventions for innovative products made of waste.<br/>Some of the compost is further processed into value-added products such as phosphate rich organic manure (PROM), to which nutrients have been added. Its quality is certified by HARIT – an initiative of the State of Maharashtra. Farmers’ groups buy such products in bulks to reduce their costs and distribute them to their farming members. The application of city compost is embedded in a full innovative package of practices developed by GIZ that includes mechanisation, drip irrigation and other technologies. This cycle creates business for urban producers and farmers’ groups while simultaneously increasing productivity and soil health on Indian farms since the compost from the urban waste stream provides better results than conventional manure.<br/>City compost is an innovative approach that closes the loop of carbon and nutrient flow from soil back into soil. At the same time, Maharashtra makes use of the 22,300 tons of municipal solid waste emerging daily, up to 45 per cent of which is organic. A single average city in Maharashtra can therefore provide nutrient and carbon for about 14,000 hectares of land annually.<br/>“The new compost allows our potatoes to withstand more moisture stress during the dry spell, as it increases the water holding capacity of the soil. Compared to our conventional farm yard manure it is also better decomposed and does not lead to any fungal diseases, as the compost is treated with fungicide,” says farmer Vilas Darekar Papalwadi.<br/>Challenges – prices and upscaling<br/>While farmers produce their own farmyard manure at a cost of 1,600 Rupees (Rs.) per ton (20 euros) or buy it at Rs. 2,350 per ton (30 euros), city compost costs about Rs. 2,300 per ton including transport. One option to bridge this gap was found by the Indian government – the State Ministry of Chemicals and Fertilizers subsidises the production of city compost, so it can be an affordable organic matter complementary to farm yard manure. GIZ not only supports facilitation with government agencies for subsidies but also links farmers’ groups and urban producers directly. The response is positive: “Urban compost provides a great alternative as a soil fertility enhancer. After experimentation with a few lead farmers, we see a growing demand for this compost. This year, we are procuring 160 tons of compost from the city Nashik,” maintains Somnath Gorakh Palve, Director of a farmers’ group.<br/>Favourable policies and cooperation schemes<br/>Since April 2018, GIZ has been carrying out the implementation of the city compost project and is now entering in the phase of standardisation for further upscaling. The initiative is in line with India’s political priorities, such as the nation-wide campaign Clean India for sanitation or the goal of the government to double farmer’s income by 2022. The winter is a time of peace and rest, but we all know we still need to stay physically active. Yet, as the outdoor conditions change with the season, various factors come into play that can affect our balance and our ability to prevent a fall.<br/>The days grow shorter. Lower light means a higher risk for falls. The weather grows colder. Ice and snow mean a higher risk for falls.<br/>While these factors apply to everyone, older adults are at a higher risk for falls than younger adults and are more likely to have serious injuries. Something that may be a sprain or strain in a younger adult could be much more serious in an older adult. Sometimes this means a bone fracture in the extremities or spine, which can become very serious very quickly. Additionally, falls can lead to traumatic brain injuries and disability.<br/>Falls are common in those 65 years old or greater with about 33% having at least one fall per year. According to the National Institutes of Health, more than 1.5 million older adults go to the emergency room each year due to fall-related injuries. But, while falls, and resulting injuries, are common, they are not inevitable. There are steps we can all take to prevent falls, even in the winter.<br/>Top 3 Tips for Preventing Falls<br/>There are three main components that we use for balance: eyesight, feeling in our feet (somatosensory), and inner ear (equilibrium). Knowing this, there are some simple things you can do to keep yourself from falling.<br/>Get routine, annual eye exams to ensure your eyewear prescription is current and correct. If you’re wearing the wrong Rx, you increase your chance of falling dramatically. Also, talk to your optometrist about moving away from progressive lenses or bi/trifocals if you currently wear then and have a balance issue. Instead, use specific glasses for going about town and another set for when you’re reading a book or checking your text messages.<br/>Have your feet checked by your Podiatrist, Physical Therapist, or Doctor. Healthy feet are vital for mobility and balance. Sensory information in your feet is important so you can sense the ground underneath you and react appropriately. If you suffer from neuropathy (weakness, numbness, and pain from nerve damage, commonly caused by diabetes, but also sometimes resulting from injuries, infections, or exposure to toxins) regularly checking your feet for signs of injury or infection can prevent more serious problems.<br/>To improve your sense of equilibrium you must work on your inner ear. This means balance exercises! Start with ankle sways — stand with your feet shoulder width apart and shift your weight side to side only moving at the ankles. The shoulders and hips should move together as one unit. Do this for 1 minute. Then, try shifting weight forward and backward. Only go forward toward your toes until your heels feel light, then shift backward to your heels (slowly!) until your toes feel light. Do this for 1 minute as well.<br/>In addition to following these tips, you should exercise regularly to improve your strength, especially in your legs! Routine exercise, even strengthening alone, has been shown to have a significant impact on fall risk. You can stay fit, and impact muscle strength with simple, at-home moves to keep your legs strong.<br/>3 Simple Strengthening Moves to Reduce Fall Risk<br/>The Chair Rise exercise is a perfect place to start increasing leg strength to reduce your fall risk. From a seated position with your feet about shoulder width apart, reach your arms out and lean forward from your hips. As your bottom lifts off the chair, and without letting your knees collapse inward, move into a standing position. Reverse the movement back into the starting position. Aim for 3 sets of 10 repetitions.<br/>Next, while holding onto your countertop for support, try heel raises to stretch and strengthen foot and calf muscles, key to improving balance. Slowly raise up onto your toes, then lower. Start with 10 repetitions, rest, then do 10 more! When you’re ready for a challenge, try one leg at a time.<br/>To round out your strengthening routine, try sidestepping. Stand with a countertop or railing in front of you. Take a step out to the side while staying upright and without leaning your trunk, then bring the trailing leg to shoulder width stance and repeat! Do 20 steps with the right leg leading, and 20 with the left leg leading.<br/>As the peace and beauty of the winter settles upon us, make a commitment to you safety by following the above tips and incorporating these balance exercises into your daily routine.

The level of obstruction vary from slight obstruction to full obstruction. Usually, vowels make no or very little obstruction, while consonants make high or complete obstruction. The way of air obstruction in speech sound is manner of articulation.<br/>Vowel letters do not make any clear obstructions, but on the other hand, constant letters make clear obstructions. Both, active and passive articulators form a structure of complete closure so that no air can pass through lips (mouth) or nasal cavity (nose). Usually it refers to a sound when a complete closure in the vocal tract is suddenly released; (as in the word [book]). Thus the sound stop occurs in two steps; the obstruction of air and sudden release of that air.<br/>Examples of stop are [p, b, t, d, k, and g].<br/>When we push air through a narrow gap to create a hissing noise, we make a fricative consonant. Sibilant fricatives are strong and clearly audible such as [s, ʃ]. Strident fricatives are weak and less audible such as [θ, f].<br/>Approximant includes constant sounds that are created by very less obstruction to the airflow. BBC English has four approximant sounds. Usually we divide approximants into two groups:<br/>(1) Semivowels with very less obstruction such as [w] in ‘wet’ and [j] in ‘yet’.<br/>(2) Liquids sounds which have comparatively identifiable constriction of the airflow but not like fricative noise such as [l] in ‘lead’ and [r] in ‘read’.<br/>The manner of articulation in affricate is similar to plosive but it does not remain stop for a long time. Unlike plosive, the air is released slowly in affricate.<br/>This articulation occurs when a fricative is followed by a plosive with the same place of articulation. This manner of articulation is known as nasal.

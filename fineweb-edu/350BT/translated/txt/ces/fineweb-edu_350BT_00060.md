| When Hurricane Harvey swept across southeastern Texas in 2017, 1.67 million customers lost power — and that doesn’t even make the top-10 list of the biggest power outages in U.S. history.<br/>The potential effects of widespread power outages are well known: disabled communications and transportation, contaminated water and nonfunctioning medical equipment, to name just a few. These are not only threats to personal security, but to national security as well. A weakened grid leaves us vulnerable to both economic and political disruption — something our adversaries can exploit. Keeping the nation safe means, in part, keeping the lights on — and we need to ensure that we invest in the scientific research to make that possible.<br/>Researchers at Los Alamos National Laboratory in New Mexico, which is run by a partnership that includes Texas A&M University, are working with the government and utility companies to harden our power grids and reduce widespread outages.<br/>Using advanced mathematics, physics, engineering and artificial intelligence, and leveraging rapidly expanding data available from smart devices, we are developing computer models to better understand the threats from extreme events and reduce the risks.<br/>Beginning with data about a specific region’s power grid, such as the status of switches and how much electric load they carry, our researchers are working to understand which lines are likely to go down, why and the impact on the surrounding area’s power supply. These data help to identify which specific lines or substations are the most important to protect.<br/>Doing this not only gets the power back on quicker, but it helps utilities prioritize rolling out periodic upgrades, such as reinforcing or hardening utility poles, creating water-blocking barriers around substations or increasing security measures. This mass is due to a rotating steel framework, nine magnets for directing the proton beam, the vacuum tube in which the protons are bundled, devices for radiation diagnostics, and a 1.5-metre-wide cable carrier, among other things. Proto musí být portál nebo jeho ozařovací hlava otočena kolem pacienta. Obrovské rotující těleso (3) portálu váží 220 tun a je široké až 10,5 metru. Tato hmotnost je způsobena rotující ocelovou konstrukcí, devíti magnety pro řízení protonového paprsku, vakuovou trubicí, ve které jsou protony svázány, zařízeními pro radiační diagnostiku a kabelovým nosičem o šířce 1,5 metru.

| Writing Poetry of the Spirit<br/>by Violet Nesdoly<br/>This is our twenty-first column in the "Poet's Classroom" series.<br/>“I am convinced the kind of experience—the kind of knowledge—one gets from poetry cannot be duplicated elsewhere. The spiritual life wants articulation—it wants embodiment in language." Edward Hirsch1<br/>Though poetry may not be popular in our culture or the modern church, poetry and the textbook of our faith—the Bible—are no strangers. Whole books of the Bible are made up of poems (Psalms, Song of Solomon). Many Bible writers of prose inserted large chunks of poetry into their narratives, essays or speeches (the Pentateuch, the Major and Minor Prophets). Jesus' teachings and stories are full of poetic devices (metaphor, allegory, symbol, parallelism). Even Paul, the Bible's premiere logician, was skilled in using poetic language (he described the Christian life as a race, the church as a body, the prepared Christian as clothed in armor). If you enjoy reading poetry about faith, you are in good company.<br/>Hirsch's statement can be applied to reading poetry, but how much more does it fit with writing it! In this article we're going to look at ways writing poetry helps us articulate our spiritual life and in this way benefits us as Christian writers. We will talk about how it does that in two areas: by challenging us to find and express truth and by helping us become modern-day disciples in the transformation of our imaginations.<br/>1. Poetry challenges writers to find and express truth<br/>Jill Baumgaertner paraphrasing Kenneth Koch's ideas from his book, Making Your Own Days, says: "What poets look for is the truth of experience and the means of expressing it…Poetry forces the writer to pay attention and find what is true."2<br/>What kinds of truth does poetic expression about God and our life with Him express? It often differs from truths about God that we read in factual statements from theology books because it is non-literal. "Poetry evokes a language that moves beyond the literal and consequently, a mode of thinking that moves beyond the literal," says Edward Hirsch.3<br/>Robert Frost explains it this way: "There are many other things I have found myself saying about poetry but the chiefest of these is that it is metaphor, saying one thing and meaning another, saying one thing in terms of another, the pleasure of ulteriority."4<br/>Below are some poems that communicate truths about God and the Christian life in this non-literal, slant, ulterior way. As you read each one, ask yourself:<br/>– What truth does this poem embody?<br/>– Does the fact that the piece is a poem (as opposed to prose) help communicate that truth or blur it for you?<br/>"Dead Word Walking" by James Beard<br/>"By a Far Star's Account" by Cindy L. Beebe<br/>Derailed" by Stephanie Roberts<br/>"Crucified, Risen, Still Wounded" by Sandra Savage<br/>One of the most powerful truths poetry communicates better than any theological treatise is that the Gospel is beyond our understanding. As Louis A. Markos says in a Christianity Today article: "Poetry with its desire to incarnate transcendent truths in material images while maintaining (via metaphors, symbols, allusions, etc.) a vital sense of play and interchange between the two, comes much closer than science, logic, or systematic theology to capturing the mystery inherent in the incarnation."5<br/>Many of our favorite images of God possess that poetic mystery. He is called a refuge and fortress, given motherly attributes, pictured as a bird, called a shepherd, the Word, and letters of the Greek alphabet. Though these images of God are disparate and not meant to be taken literally, they begin to help us grasp the truth of who He is.<br/>Read a poetic Bible passage, for example John 15:1-8. As you're reading ask yourself:<br/>– What is the truth here?<br/>– What poetic devices does the writer use? (metaphor, allegory, parallelism, hyperbole, rhyme, rhythm, the placement of the words on the page etc.)<br/>– What aspects of truth (emotional, factual, relational) come across as a result of the writer using poetic devices.<br/>Below is George Herbert's take on the theme we find in John 15.<br/>I BLESSE thee, Lord, because I G R O W<br/>Among thy trees, which in a R O W<br/>To thee both fruit and order O W<br/>What open force, or hidden C H A R M<br/>Can blast my fruit, or bring me H A R M<br/>While the inclosure is thine A R M?<br/>Inclose me still for fear I S T A R T.<br/>Be to me rather sharp and T A R T,<br/>Than let me want thy hand and A R T.<br/>When thou dost greater judgements S P A R E,<br/>And with thy knife but prune and P A R E,<br/>Ev'n fruitful trees more fruitfull A R E.<br/>Such sharpness shows the sweetest F R E N D:<br/>Such cuttings rather heal than R E N D:<br/>And such beginnings touch their E N D.<br/>I love Herbert's poem not only for the truth it expresses but for other things that are present just because it is a poem. The perfect rhyme at the end of each line gives it a sense of inevitability or completion. The trimming of the words is not only clever and playful, but illustrates the idea of pruning. (Notice the "OW" at the end of stanza one. I know it's meant to be pronounced "owe" but reading it as "ow"—an expression of pain—brings home another truth about all that cutting; it hurts!) The way the poet draws attention to the last word of each line by putting it in capitals also gives the reader a physical picture of something being trimmed.<br/>2. Poetry helps writers become modern-day disciples by transforming the imagination<br/>A handout given me by a professor in a poetry class I took years ago expresses our human dilemma well: "Poetry potentially has an important function in our discipleship…this function centers in the role that the imagination plays in our lives.… Our imaginations are fallen and they are nourished and shaped by the culture of a fallen world. The images of life and its meaning given us by all sorts of media harden and form a rigid shell about what a man is, a woman is, what is success, what is sex—at "habitual" set of images governs what we conceive real or possible.…The process of sanctification is, among other things, a process of dissolving, of smashing those layers of hardened and rigid imagination."6<br/>The writer goes on to explain how we smash those layers of hardened and rigid imagination by replacing them with the images from God's Word. Writing poetry can help with that as we challenge ourselves to plumb the depths of Bible images with our imaginations and communicate our understanding in poetry.<br/>The Bible is replete with interesting metaphors and word pictures. Set yourself the task of noticing them by marking them in your Bible. I did that for a while, underlining each image that seemed to have poem potential with an orange pencil crayon. Looking back through that Bible, here are some of the phrases I underlined: "double-minded man" (James 1:8), "Mercy triumphs over judgment" (James 2:13), "as long as I live in the tent of this body" (1 Peter 1:13), "speak from the viewpoint of the world" (1 John 4:5). Now I haven't, to this point, written a poem about each one of these. But can't you see interesting pictures in each of these fragments? Can you understand how giving them some thought would stretch the imagination along spiritual lines and on spiritual subjects?<br/>One poem that did arise from that exercise is based on the phrase "build yourself up" from Jude 20. In it I attempted to show how building oneself up spiritually was similar to a physical workout.<br/>Build Yourselves Up<br/>But you, beloved, build yourselves up in your most holy faith–Jude 20<br/>Warm up with worship<br/>hands raised, spirit stretching<br/>to the Almighty.<br/>Increase the rhythm of the heart<br/>with the jumping-jacks of praise.<br/>Hop onto the treadmill of the Word<br/>read it, study it,<br/>meditate on it, memorize it.<br/>Then it's down on the floor<br/>for push-ups of confession<br/>abdominal crunches of petition<br/>and, firmly grasping others' weighty burdens,<br/>bench presses of intercession—set after set.<br/>Up on your feet again for step-ups of listening<br/>then cool down walking in place, silent.<br/>End with a song of thanksgiving<br/>that pours from a well-toned heart.<br/>Now go out to meet the day<br/>your spirit radiating contentment and joy<br/>flexible and strong from its workout<br/>with faith, hope and love.<br/>© 2004 by Violet Nesdoly<br/>• Combine Bible study with factual research to explore Bible themes. | Cosmic ray astrophysics, an active area at the cutting edge of basic research,<br/>investigates particles (or rays) which arrive at the earth's surface from<br/>sources beyond our sun (or even beyond our galaxy). Project GRAND is an<br/>extensive air shower array which studies these rays in two energy bands<br/>depending on the trigger selected: from 30 to 300 GeV (GeV is giga, or billion<br/>electron volts, eV), and from 100 to 100 000 TeV (TeV is teva, or trillion eV).<br/>Gamma Ray Astrophysics at Notre Dame<br/>The original goal in the construction of this array was to study stellar point<br/>sources of gamma rays. This study requires: good angular resolution, good<br/>particle identification, and adequate area together with sufficient running<br/>time to gather statistics. The idea of using tracking detectors provides<br/>superior angular resolution and simultaneous particle identification; this<br/>project has pioneered the use of tracking detectors in cosmic ray research. PWC<br/>detectors also allow the array to be used for additional studies. including a<br/>new primary goal: measuring the cosmic rays in the energy region from 30 to 300<br/>The GRAND array is located in a 100 m x 100 m field adjacent to the Notre Dame<br/>campus at 222 m elevation, 86o W and 42o N. The 64 stations are<br/>arranged in an 8 x 8 grid with 14 m separation between stations. The<br/>electronics trailer at the center of the field is a former astronaut debriefing<br/>and isolation chamber, and was obtained from NASA government surplus. The<br/>southwest quadrant was the first constructed; these stations were placed 0.6 m<br/>into the ground for natural cooling in the summer. The cooling works well, but<br/>moisture problems suggested the placement of the remainder of the array above<br/>ground with added insulation.<br/>Each station is enclosed by a 2.4 m x 2.4 m x 0.9 m high hut. Inside are eight<br/>planes of proportional wire chambers (PWCs) and a 50 mm thick steel absorber<br/>plate. The chambers have been surveyed to a precision of 0.1 deg. The<br/>temperature of the huts is controlled with heaters to keep them > 17 deg C in<br/>the winter; dehumidifiers control the humidity < 55%. The eight planes are<br/>arranged in xy pairs; the pairs are separated vertically by 200 mm and are 1.2<br/>sq-m in area. The top three pairs measure the xz and yz angle of the track; the<br/>pair below the 50 mm steel absorber plate are used for muon identification.<br/>Muons are distinguished from electrons by demanding that a muon track be<br/>single hits in each of the 3 pairs above the steel plus the pair below, all of<br/>which are aligned. This simple algorithm is 96% accurate for muons. Muon<br/>identification is performed for each track.<br/>IV. Construction design utilizes<br/>mass production techniques to economically manufacture precision detectors. The<br/>cells in each plane are 14 mm wide and 1100 mm long separated 9.5 mm from the<br/>high voltage planes. The PWCs are filled with a gas mixture of 80% argon and<br/>20% carbon dioxide and operate at 2600 V.<br/>V. Electronics and Data Acquisition System<br/>The huts contain 8 PWC planes. A plane has 80 cells; each cell has an amplifier,<br/>shift register memory, and summer whose outputs are used for coincidence input<br/>to form a self-trigger for the station. A station trigger causes the status of<br/>its 640 cells to be stored in local shift register memory. The trigger is<br/>comprised of an x-OR-y signal from each pair of planes; the top 3 PWC pair of<br/>signals are placed in 3-fold coincidence. This 3-fold coincidence records over<br/>99% of the cosmic ray tracks and introduces < 1% noise.<br/>The trailer receives a timing signal from each hut which triggered on a track.<br/>A coincidence circuit is set for N simultaneous huts, where N can be set to 1,<br/>2, 3, etc. A coincidence causes a train of clock pulses to be sent to all the<br/>stations which clocks the data serially from their shift register memory to the<br/>trailer. The data from all the stations are read out to the online computer in<br/>the trailer in a total of 70 microseconds. The time of the trigger, accurate to<br/>1 millisecond, is recorded along with the rest of the trigger's data.<br/>The data acquisition system "computer" records the data.

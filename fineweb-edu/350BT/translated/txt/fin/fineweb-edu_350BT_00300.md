| In addition to a collection of the world's most extraordinary geysers and hot springs, Yellowstone is home to a large variety of wildlife including grizzly bears,<br/>bison, elk and wolves. The wolf is a major predator that had been missing from the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem for decades until its restoration in 1995.<br/>The gray wolf (Canis lupus) was present in Yellowstone when the park was established in 1872. Predator control, including poisoning, was practiced<br/>here in the late 1800s and early 1900s. Between 1914 and 1926, at least 136 wolves were killed in the park; by the 1940s, wolf packs were rarely reported. An<br/>intensive survey in 1978 found no evidence of a wolf population in Yellowstone, although an occasional wolf probably wandered into the area. During the 1980s,<br/>wolves began to reestablish breeding packs in northwestern Montana; 50-60 wolves inhabited Montana in 1994.<br/>In late 1994/early 1995, and again in 1996, USFWS and Canadian wildlife biologists captured wolves in Canada and relocated and released them in both<br/>Yellowstone and central Idaho. In mid-January 1995, 14 wolves were temporarily penned in Yellowstone; the first 8 wolves on January 12 and the second 6 on<br/>January 19, 1995. Wolves from one social group were together in each release pen. On January 23, 1996, 11 more wolves were brought to Yellowstone for the second<br/>year of wolf restoration. Four days later they were joined by another 6 wolves.<br/>Since their release, wolves have been doing well. At the end of 2006, at least 136 wolves in 13 packs occupied Yellowstone National Park. Preliminary<br/>data from studies indicate that wolf recovery will likely lead to greater biodiversity throughout the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem (GYE). Wolves have preyed<br/>primarily on elk and these carcasses have provided food to a wide variety of other animals, especially scavenging species. They are increasingly preying on<br/>bison, especially in late winter. Grizzly bears have usurped wolf kills almost at will, contrary to predictions and observations from other areas where the<br/>two species occur. Wolf kills, then, provide an important resource for bears in low food years. Aggression toward coyotes has decreased the number of<br/>coyotes inside wolf territories, which may benefit other smaller predators, rodents, and birds of prey.<br/>So far, data suggests wolves are contributing to decreased numbers of elk calves surviving to adulthood and decreased survival of adult elk. Wolves may<br/>also be affecting where and how elk use the habitat. Some of these effects were predictable, but were based on research in relatively simple systems of one to<br/>two predator and prey species. Such is not the case in Yellowstone, where four other large predators (black and grizzly bears, coyotes, cougars) prey on<br/>elk—and people hunt the elk outside the park. Thus, interactions of wolves with elk and other ungulates has created a new degree of complexity that<br/>makes it difficult to project long-term population trends. The effect of wolf recovery on the dynamics of northern Yellowstone elk cannot be generalized to<br/>other elk populations in the GYE. The effects depend on a complex of factors including elk densities, abundance of other predators, presence of alternative<br/>ungulate prey, winter severity, and—outside the park—land ownership, human harvest, livestock depredations, and human-caused wolf deaths.<br/>The biological requirement for removing the wolf from the endangered species list has been achieved: Three years of 30 breeding pairs across the three<br/>recovery areas. The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS) has approved the wolf management plans of Idaho and Montana. As a result, day-to-day wolf management<br/>has been transferred to these states. USFWS has proposed delisting the wolves in Idaho and Montana, and in Yellowstone and Grand Teton national parks. Delisting<br/>is not proposed for the state of Wyoming because of disagreements over state wolf management if delisting were to occur.

Typically, they are identical to the Streptococcus pyogenes type II CRISPR systems, except that a single guide-RNA is used in place of the complementary crRNAs and tracrRNAs of the natural CRISPR system, and the Cas9 protein is codon-optimized for the cells intended to be transfected with the CRISPR/Cas9 system (Jinek et al. 2012). For example, CRISPR/Cas9 expression plasmids used to edit the genome of human cells have codons optimized for human cells, and thus are called humanized Cas9 (hCas9) (Mali et al. Feb 2013).<br/>As the guide RNA sequence determines the cut site of the Cas9 nuclease with high specificity, the re-targeting of the CRISPR/Cas9 system to new sequences is far easier than re-targeting other engineered nucleases such as TALENs (transcription activator-like effector nuclease) or ZFNs (zinc finger nuclease). In fact, the cut site of a guide RNA-Cas9 nuclease complex can be reprogrammed to target just about any site that occurs immediately before a PAM sequence (5′-NGG-3′) (Cong et al. 2013). In addition, the efficiency of the nuclease is comparable or greater than that of TALENs or ZFNs, which is remarkable given the high specificity of the nuclease (Mali et al. Feb 2013). To understand how this high specificity and high efficiency are possible, check out our mechanism page.<br/>Mutations in the active sites of both nuclease domains results in a non-catalytic Cas9. | The Brookings Institution recently released a study examining the increasing level of income inequality in cities and metro areas across the country, with Boston topping the list.<br/>Conducted by Alan Berube and Natalie Holmes, the study was released on Jan. 14. Alongside Boston, the study also ranked New York and San Francisco among the top ten cities for inequality. While causes of income inequality are varying and multifaceted, Berube shed some light on the possible forces behind Boston’s imbalance.<br/>“Inequality tends to reflect, more than anything else, the economic structure of a city or metropolitan area,” Berube said in an email. “That is, the types of industries and jobs a place has produce the incomes its workers earn, and thus the disparities between its rich and poor residents.”<br/>With some of its major industries including finance, scientific research and development and engineering, it’s no wonder Boston and the surrounding area employ large numbers of high-earning employees. According to William Dickens, professor of economics and social policy at Northeastern University, one of the main causes of income inequality is changing technology. More jobs become available for highly skilled workers, while unskilled workers lack the same opportunities. As more companies and businesses that require skilled workers move into an area, the demand for those workers drives up already high wages while driving down low wages.<br/>This divide between skilled and unskilled workers is an issue that the city of Boston is actively engaged in tackling. Trinh Nguyen, director of the city’s Office of Workforce Development, explained several of the initiatives aimed at closing the income gap in both the short and long term.<br/>“We see our department in helping the city make resources and programs as accessible to Boston residents as possible,” Nguyen said. “Economic growth is about inclusion and the aspiration to be part of that growth. You can only aspire to be part of that growth if you think you have a shot.”<br/>Some of these efforts include collaborations with Boston Public Schools to encourage college application and completion, along with support from initiatives like Success Boston, aimed at providing students with the resources necessary to graduate from college.<br/>Nguyen also explained the city’s redistribution of funding for job-training programs to create a more holistic career-building continuum. These programs focus on building a career path, so Boston’s citizens can gain the necessary skills for quality jobs while also receiving income support and connections for post-secondary education so they can continue advancing their careers.<br/>In addition to the discrepancy in education level and skill training, the housing market may also be to blame for some of Boston’s equality problems.<br/>“Our research shows that in cities that have higher levels of inequality, housing for low-income workers and families tends to be more expensive relative to their incomes,” Berube said in an email. “This may be because developers in unequal cities are, at least in the short term, more responsive to the housing demands of the wealthy than those of the poor.”<br/>Construction of affordable housing presents another challenge, as development companies can struggle to turn a profit when tenants are paying lower rents, compared to the security of leasing to higher-paid residents.<br/>Matt Pritchard, the president and executive director of HomeStart, Inc., an organization that aims to end and prevent homelessness, spoke further about this problem. “The landlords’ incentive is to generate as much revenue as possible and some people are able to pay more so that squeezes out people who have a very low income,” he said.<br/>Pritchard continued to discuss the extremely narrow income margin that can result in someone becoming homeless. For someone living paycheck to paycheck, it just takes one isolated incident, like an argument in the home or the loss of a support system, for them to be left without a home.<br/>Michelle Chausse, the director of communications at Rosie’s Place, a women’s homeless shelter, echoed this statement.<br/>“We see women who are working, full time even, and who are just not making enough to make ends meet,” Chausse said. “One of the reasons behind their low earnings is income inequality and not being paid a fair wage for the jobs they are performing.”<br/>While Chausse acknowledged that the state’s minimum wage is one of the best in the country, it’s still not enough to keep up with the rising housing costs and job competition. Nguyen also addressed the city’s commitment to fighting this problem, referencing Massachusetts’ plan to have an $11 minimum wage by 2017.<br/>While there are many possible causes for Boston coming in as the city with the highest level of inequality and an even greater number of possible solutions, there is no quick fix. | CATTLE EAR TAGS<br/>By Lee Townsend, Extension Entomologist<br/>University of Kentucky College of Agriculture<br/>Insecticide-impregnated cattle ear tags release small amounts of an insecticide which are distributed over the animal during grooming or rubbing. In general, ear tags have provided excellent, long term control of horn flies and a reduction in face fly numbers.<br/>Factors to consider when using these products:<br/>- Read the label before you purchase and use insecticide ear tags.<br/>- All tags are labeled for beef cattle while only those with certain active ingredients are approved for use on lactating dairy cattle. Check for any limitations for use, such as animal age.<br/>- Look for the common name of the active ingredient (for example, permethrin). In some cases, different brands of tags contain the same active ingredient. You can save money by comparison shopping, or avoid inadvertently using the same active ingredient if resistance is a potential problem.<br/>- Consider the recommended number of tags per head. Some brands are used at the rate of one per animal. University of Kentucky research trials have generally shown that systems which use two tags per animal seem to provide better face fly control than those which rely on a single tag.<br/>- Animals only need to be handled one time to apply the tags. However, this is not necessarily when you would normally work your animals. For fly control, it is best to tag animals after horn fly numbers reach 50 or more per side. This reduces the chances of developing resistance to the active ingredients that are being used. Normally, tags provide 12 to 15 weeks of fly control. Tagging too early in the season can mean that the tags are not providing good control in the fall that will help to control the overwintering population.<br/>- With insecticidal ear tags, the control system moves with the animals. This may be an advantage if animals are moved at intervals and dust bags or back rubbers are not in place in every pasture or grazing area.<br/>What areas of the animal are treated by ear tags?<br/>Research with insecticide ear tags fitted with a dye showed that the area immediately behind the ears and forward to under the eye and down to the back of the mouth are treated pretty thoroughly. No dye was transferred to the legs, back-line, belly or hips. There is additional spread of the insecticide as the animal grooms itself and mingles with other cattle.<br/>Why do ear tags tend to work better against horn flies than face flies?<br/>Differences in behavior between these two fly species is probably part of the story. Horn flies spend most of their time on the animal. They suck small amounts of blood frequently and leave only to lay their eggs on fresh cattle droppings. This constant presence on the animal means they are certain to come in contact with some of the insecticide and be killed.<br/>Face flies spend only a small amount of time on the animal. Usually, they are off resting on vegetation. They visit the face, primarily under the eyes and around the mouth. Insecticide residues may not last as long on these surfaces, or the fly may not remain on the treated area long enough to pick up a lethal dose.<br/>However, some of the newer synthetic pyrethroid insecticides are active against flies in such small quantities, that much better reduction in face flies is seen.<br/>What types of insecticide ear tags are available now?<br/>There are three main types based on the active ingredient(s) that they contain.<br/>- Organophosphate (OP) insecticides such as diazinon, fenthion, pirimofos methyl, or a diazinon + chlorpyrifos<br/>combination. These tags provide good horn fly control and moderate face fly control.<br/>- Synthetic pyrethroid (SP) insecticides- fenvalerate and permethrin are the original members of this group. These tags are sold under a variety of brand names. Usually they are less expensive than the new, more expensive synthetic pyrethroids, such as cyfluthrin, lambda-cyhalothrin, and zeta-cypermethrin. These tags provide good horn fly control and better face fly control than the OP tags.<br/>These two groups of tags contain insecticides that attack the nervous system of the fly in different ways. Seasonal rotation between an OP and an SP insecticide can be useful in combatting insecticide resistance that has developed in horn flies in some areas of Kentucky, USA. Resistance, indicated by a failure in horn fly control, can develop when tags containing the synthetic pyrethroid permethrin have been used for several consecutive seasons. No resistance to organophosphates, or the new synthetic pyrethroids, has been seen.<br/>- A relatively new group of combination tags has appeared. These couple an OP and a SP in the same tag. Current examples pair lambda-cyhalothrin and pirimiphos methyl or cypermethrin and chlorpyrifos. The assumption is that the OP would control SP-resistant horn flies.<br/>Tags need to be removed at the end of the fly season in the fall to reduce the potential for insecticide resistance in both lice and flies.<br/>Are there any safety precautions associated with using insecticide ear tags?<br/>Non-permeable gloves should be worn when tagging animals. Tags should not be handled bare-handed under any circumstances. The concentration of insecticide in the tags varies from 8% to 36%. The tags are manufactured so that the insecticide is rubbed off the surface and on to the animal. Any handling of the tags leaves some insecticide on the hands. The insecticide then can be transferred easily to the mouth, eyes, face or other areas of the body.<br/>Signal words on the label range from CAUTION to WARNING. Several products carry statements about the<br/>potential for allergic reaction following exposure. Many are easily absorbed through the skin or eyes, some have<br/>irritation vapors. After being destroyed and then rebuilt by the Assyrians, Babylon became the capital of the short-lived Neo-Babylonian Empire from 609 to 539 BC. The Hanging Gardens of Babylon was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, although a number of scholars believe these were actually in the Assyrian capital of Nineveh. It was perhaps the first city to reach a population above 200,000. Estimates for the maximum extent of its area range from 890 to 900 hectares (2,200 acres).<br/>The main sources of information about Babylon—excavation of the site itself, references in cuneiform texts found elsewhere in Mesopotamia, references in the Bible, descriptions in classical writing (especially by Herodotus), and second-hand descriptions (citing the work of Ctesias and Berossus)—present an incomplete and sometimes contradictory picture of the ancient city even at its peak in the sixth century BC.<br/>Archibald Sayce, writing in the 1870s, considered Bab-ilu or Bab-ili to be the translation of an earlier Sumerian (formerly thought to be in the obsolete "Turanian" language-family) name Ca-dimirra, meaning "gate of god", based on the characters KAN4 DIĜIR.RAKI (corresponding to the Sumerian phrase kan diĝirak "god's gate") or perhaps based on other characters.<br/>According to Professor Dietz-Otto Edzard, the city was originally called Babilla, but by the time of the Third Dynasty of Ur, through a process of etymological speculation, had become Bāb-ili(m) meaning "gate of god" or "god's gate" (Bab-Il). Hammurabi also invaded and conquered Elam to the east, and the kingdoms of Mari and Ebla to the northwest. The city was renamed Karanduniash during this period. Kassite Babylon eventually became subject to the Middle Assyrian Empire (1365–1053 BC) to the north, and Elam to the east, with both powers vying for control of the city. The Assyrian king Tukulti-Ninurta I took the throne of Babylon in 1235 BC.<br/>By 1155 BC, after continued attacks and annexing of territory by the Assyrians and Elamites, the Kassites were deposed in Babylon. An Akkadian south Mesopotamian dynasty then ruled for the first time. However, Babylon remained weak and subject to domination by Assyria. Its ineffectual native kings were unable to prevent new waves of foreign West Semitic settlers from the deserts of the Levant, including the Arameans and Suteans in the 11th century BC, and finally the Chaldeans in the 9th century BC, entering and appropriating areas of Babylonia for themselves. The Arameans briefly ruled in Babylon during the late 11th century BC.<br/>During the rule of the Neo-Assyrian Empire (911–609 BC), Babylonia was under constant Assyrian domination or direct control. During the reign of Sennacherib of Assyria, Babylonia was in a constant state of revolt, led by a chieftain named Merodach-Baladan, in alliance with the Elamites, and suppressed only by the complete destruction of the city of Babylon. In 689 BC, its walls, temples and palaces were razed, and the rubble was thrown into the Arakhtu, the sea bordering the earlier Babylon on the south. Destruction of the religious center shocked many, and the subsequent murder of Sennacherib by two of his own sons while praying to the god Nisroch was considered an act of atonement. Consequently, his successor Esarhaddon hastened to rebuild the old city and make it his residence during part of the year. After his death, Babylonia was governed by his elder son, the Assyrian prince Shamash-shum-ukin, who eventually started a civil war in 652 BC against his own brother, Ashurbanipal, who ruled in Nineveh. Shamash-shum-ukin enlisted the help of other peoples subject to Assyria, including Elam, Persia, Chaldeans, and Suteans of southern Mesopotamia, and the Canaanites and Arabs dwelling in the deserts south of Mesopotamia.<br/>Once again, Babylon was besieged by the Assyrians, starved into surrender and its allies were defeated. Ashurbanipal celebrated a "service of reconciliation", but did not venture to "take the hands" of Bel. An Assyrian governor named Kandalanu was appointed as ruler of the city. Ashurbanipal did collect texts from Babylon for inclusion in his extensive library at Ninevah.<br/>After the death of Ashurbanipal, the Assyrian empire destabilized due to a series of internal civil wars throughout the reigns of Assyrian kings Ashur-etil-ilani, Sin-shumu-lishir and Sinsharishkun. Eventually Babylon, like many other parts of the near east, took advantage of the chaos within Assyria to free itself from Assyrian rule. In the subsequent overthrow of the Assyrian Empire by an alliance of peoples, the Babylonians saw another example of divine vengeance.<br/>Under Nabopolassar, a previously unknown Chaldean chieftain, Babylon escaped Assyrian rule, and in an alliance with Cyaxares, king of the Medes and Persians together with the Scythians and Cimmerians, finally destroyed the Assyrian Empire between 612 BC and 605 BC. Babylon thus became the capital of the Neo-Babylonian (sometimes and possibly erroneously called the Chaldean) Empire.<br/>With the recovery of Babylonian independence, a new era of architectural activity ensued, particularly during the reign of his son Nebuchadnezzar II (604–561 BC). Nebuchadnezzar ordered the complete reconstruction of the imperial grounds, including the Etemenanki ziggurat, and the construction of the Ishtar Gate—the most prominent of eight gates around Babylon. A reconstruction of the Ishtar Gate is located in the Pergamon Museum in Berlin.<br/>Nebuchadnezzar is also credited with the construction of the Hanging Gardens of Babylon—one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World—said to have been built for his homesick wife Amyitis. Whether the gardens actually existed is a matter of dispute. German archaeologist Robert Koldewey speculated that he had discovered its foundations, but many historians disagree about the location. Stephanie Dalley has argued that the hanging gardens were actually located in the Assyrian capital, Nineveh.<br/>Nebuchandnezzar is also notoriously associated with the Babylonian exile of the Jews, the result of an imperial technique of pacification, used also by the Assyrians, in which ethnic groups in conquered areas were deported en masse to the capital.<br/>In 539 BC, the Neo-Babylonian Empire fell to Cyrus the Great, king of Persia, with a military engagement known as the Battle of Opis. Babylon's walls were considered impenetrable. The only way into the city was through one of its many gates or through the Euphrates River. Metal grates were installed underwater, allowing the river to flow through the city walls while preventing intrusion. The Persians devised a plan to enter the city via the river. During a Babylonian national feast, Cyrus' troops diverted the Euphrates River upstream, allowing Cyrus' soldiers to enter the city through the lowered water. The Persian army conquered the outlying areas of the city while the majority of Babylonians at the city center were unaware of the breach. The account was elaborated upon by Herodotus and is also mentioned in parts of the Hebrew Bible. (Herodotus also described a moat, an enormously tall and broad wall cemented with bitumen and with buildings on top, and a hundred gates to the city. He also writes that the Babylonians wear turbans and perfume and bury their dead in honey, that they practice ritual prostitution, and that three tribes among them eat nothing but fish. The hundred gates can be considered a reference to Homer, and following the pronouncement of Archibald Henry Sayce in 1883, Herodotus' account of Babylon has largely been considered to represent Greek folklore rather than an authentic voyage to Babylon. However, recently, Dalley and others have suggested taking Herodotus' account seriously.)<br/>According to 2 Chronicles 36 of the Hebrew Bible, Cyrus later issued a decree permitting captive people, including the Jews, to return to their own lands. Text found on the Cyrus Cylinder has traditionally been seen by biblical scholars as corroborative evidence of this policy, although the interpretation is disputed because the text only identifies Mesopotamian sanctuaries but makes no mention of Jews, Jerusalem, or Judea.<br/>Under Cyrus and the subsequent Persian king Darius I, Babylon became the capital city of the 9th Satrapy (Babylonia in the south and Athura in the north), as well as a center of learning and scientific advancement. In Achaemenid Persia, the ancient Babylonian arts of astronomy and mathematics were revitalized, and Babylonian scholars completed maps of constellations. The city became the administrative capital of the Persian Empire and remained prominent for over two centuries. Many important archaeological discoveries have been made that can provide a better understanding of that era.<br/>The early Persian kings had attempted to maintain the religious ceremonies of Marduk, but by the reign of Darius III, over-taxation and the strain of numerous wars led to a deterioration of Babylon's main shrines and canals, and the destabilization of the surrounding region. There were numerous attempts at rebellion and in 522 BC (Nebuchadnezzar III), 521 BC (Nebuchadnezzar IV) and 482 BC (Bel-shimani and Shamash-eriba) native Babylonian kings briefly regained independence. However these revolts were quickly repressed and Babylon remained under Persian rule for two centuries, until Alexander the Great's entry in 331 BC.<br/>In October of 331 BC, Darius III, the last Achaemenid king of the Persian Empire, was defeated by the forces of the Ancient Macedonian ruler Alexander the Great at the Battle of Gaugamela. A native account of this invasion notes a ruling by Alexander not to enter the homes of its inhabitants.<br/>Under Alexander, Babylon again flourished as a center of learning and commerce. However, following Alexander's death in 323 BC in the palace of Nebuchadnezzar, his empire was divided amongst his generals, the Diadochi, and decades of fighting soon began. The constant turmoil virtually emptied the city of Babylon. A tablet dated 275 BC states that the inhabitants of Babylon were transported to Seleucia, where a palace and a temple (Esagila) were built. With this deportation, Babylon became insignificant as a city, although more than a century later, sacrifices were still performed in its old sanctuary.<br/>Renewed Persian rule<br/>Under the Parthian and Sassanid Empires, Babylon (like Assyria) became a province of these Persian Empires for nine centuries, until after AD 650. It maintained its own culture and people, who spoke varieties of Aramaic, and who continued to refer to their homeland as Babylon. Examples of their culture are found in the Babylonian Talmud, the Gnostic Mandaean religion, Eastern Rite Christianity and the religion of the prophet Mani. Christianity was introduced to Mesopotamia in the 1st and 2nd centuries AD, and Babylon was the seat of a Bishop of the Church of the East until well after the Arab/Islamic conquest.<br/>In the mid-7th century, Mesopotamia was invaded and settled by the expanding Muslim Empire, and a period of Islamization followed. Babylon was dissolved as a province and Aramaic and Church of the East Christianity eventually became marginalized. Ibn Hauqal mentions a small village called Babel in the tenth century; subsequent travelers describe only ruins.<br/>European travelers in many cases could not discover the city's location, or mistook Fallujah for it. Benjamin of Tudela, a 12th-century traveller, mentions Babylon but it is not clear if he went there. Others referred to Baghdad as Babylon or New Babylon and described various structures encountered in the region as the Tower of Babel. Pietro della Valle found the ancient site in the 17th century and noted the existence of both baked and dried mudbricks cemented with bitumen.<br/>Claudius J. Rich, Memoir on the Ruins of Babylon (1815), pp. 1–2.<br/>The eighteenth century saw an increasing flow of travelers to Babylon, including Carsten Niebuhr and Pierre-Joseph de Beauchamp, as well as measurements of its latitude. Beauchamp's memoir, published in English translation in 1792, provoked the British East India Company to direct its agents in Baghdad and Basra to acquire Mesopotamian relics for shipment to London.<br/>Excavation and research<br/>Austen Henry Layard made some soundings during a brief visit in 1850 before abandoning the site. Fulgence Fresnel and Julius Oppert heavily excavated Babylon from 1852 to 1854. However, many of the fruits of their work were lost when a raft containing over 40 crates of artifacts sank into the Tigris river.<br/>Henry Rawlinson and George Smith worked there briefly in 1854. The next excavation was conducted by Hormuzd Rassam on behalf of the British Museum. Work began in 1879, continuing until 1882, and was prompted by widespread looting of the site. Using industrial scale digging in search of artifacts, Rassam recovered a large quantity of cuneiform tablets and other finds. The zealous excavation methods, common at the time, caused significant damage to the archaeological context. Many tablets had appeared on the market in 1876 before Rassam's excavation began.<br/>A team from the German Oriental Society led by Robert Koldewey conducted the first scientific archaeological excavations at Babylon. The work was conducted daily from 1899 until 1917. Primary efforts of the dig involved the temple of Marduk and the processional way leading up to it, as well as the city wall. Artifacts including pieces of the Ishtar Gate and hundreds of recovered tablets were sent back to Germany, where Koldewey's colleague Walter Andrae reconstructed them into displays at Vorderasiatisches Museum Berlin., The German archaeologists fled before oncoming British troops in 1917 and again many objects went missing in the following years.<br/>Further work by the German Archaeological Institute was conducted by Heinrich J. Lenzen in 1956 and Hansjörg Schmid in 1962. Lenzen's work dealt primarily with the Hellenistic theatre, and Schmid focused on the temple ziggurat Etemenanki.<br/>The site was excavated in 1974 on behalf of the Turin Centre for Archaeological Research and Excavations in the Middle East and Asia and the Iraqi-Italian Institute of Archaeological Sciences. The focus was on clearing up issues raised by re-examination of the old German data. Additional work in 1987–1989 concentrated on the area surrounding the Ishara and Ninurta temples in the Shu-Anna city-quarter of Babylon.<br/>During the restoration efforts in Babylon, the Iraqi State Organization for Antiquities and Heritage conducted extensive research, excavation and clearing, but wider publication of these archaeological activities has been limited. Indeed, most of the known tablets from all modern excavation remain unpublished.<br/>The site of Babylon has been a cultural asset to Iraq since the creation of the modern Iraqi state in 1921. The site was officially protected and excavated by the Kingdom of Iraq under British Administration, which later became the Hashemite Kingdom of Iraq, and its successors: the Arab Federation, the Iraqi Republic, Ba'athist Iraq (also officially called the Iraqi Republic), and the Republic of Iraq. Babylonian images periodically appear on Iraqi postcards and stamps. In the 1960s, a replica of the Ishtar Gate and a reconstruction of Ninmakh Temple were built on site.<br/>On 14 February 1978, the Ba'athist government of Iraq under Saddam Hussein began the "Archaeological Restoration of Babylon Project": reconstructing features of the ancient city atop its ruins. These features included the Southern Palace of Nebuchandnezzar, with 250 rooms, five courtyards, and a 30-meter entrance arch. The project also reinforced the Processional Way, the Lion of Babylon, and an amphitheater constructed in the city's Hellenistic era. In 1982 the government minted a set of seven coins displaying iconic features of Babylon. A Babylon International Festival was held in September 1987, and annually thereafter until 2002 (excepting 1990 and 1991), to showcase this work. Proposed reconstruction of the Hanging Gardens and the great ziggurat never took place.<br/>Hussein installed a portrait of himself and Nebuchadnezzar at the entrance to the ruins and inscribed his name on many of the bricks, in imitation of Nebuchadnezzar. One frequent inscription reads: "This was built by Saddam Hussein, son of Nebuchadnezzar, to glorify Iraq". These bricks became sought after as collectors' items after Hussein's downfall. Similar projects were conducted at Nineveh, Nimrud, Assur and Hatra, to demonstrate the magnificence of Arab achievement.<br/>When the 1991 Gulf War ended, Hussein wanted to build a modern palace called Saddam Hill over some of the old ruins, in the pyramidal style of a ziggurat. In 2003, he intended the construction of a cable car line over Babylon, but plans were halted by the 2003 invasion of Iraq.<br/>US and Polish occupation<br/>Following the 2003 invasion of Iraq, the area around Babylon came under the control of US troops, before being handed over to Polish forces in September 2003. US forces under the command of General James T. Conway of the I Marine Expeditionary Force were criticized for building the military base "Camp Alpha", with a helipad and other facilities on ancient Babylonian ruins during the Iraq War. US forces have occupied the site for some time and have caused irreparable damage to the archaeological record. In a report of the British Museum's Near East department, Dr. John Curtis described how parts of the archaeological site were levelled to create a landing area for helicopters, and parking lots for heavy vehicles. Curtis wrote of the occupation forces:<br/>They caused substantial damage to the Ishtar Gate, one of the most famous monuments from antiquity [...] US military vehicles crushed 2,600-year-old brick pavements, archaeological fragments were scattered across the site, more than 12 trenches were driven into ancient deposits and military earth-moving projects contaminated the site for future generations of scientists.<br/>A US Military spokesman claimed that engineering operations were discussed with the "head of the Babylon museum". The head of the Iraqi State Board for Heritage and Antiquities, Donny George, said that the "mess will take decades to sort out" and criticised Polish troops for causing "terrible damage" to the site. Poland resolved in 2004 to place the city under Iraq control, and commissioned a report titled Report Concerning the Condition of the Preservation of the Babylon Archaeological Site, which it presented at a meeting on 11–13 December 2004. In 2005 the site was handed over to the Iraqi Ministry of Culture.<br/>In April 2006, Colonel John Coleman, former Chief of Staff for the 1st Marine Expeditionary Force, offered to issue an apology for the damage done by military personnel under his command. However, he also claimed that the US presence had deterred far greater damage by other looters. An article published in April 2006 stated that UN officials and Iraqi leaders have plans to restore Babylon, making it into a cultural center.<br/>Before modern archaeological excavations in Mesopotamia, the appearance of Babylon was largely a mystery, and typically envisioned by Western artists as a hybrid between ancient Egyptian, classical Greek, and contemporary Ottoman culture.<br/>Due to Babylon's historical significance as well as references to it in the Bible, the word "Babylon" in various languages has acquired a generic meaning of a large, bustling diverse city. Amorilaiset eivät aluksi harjoittaneet maanviljelyä kuten edistyneemmät mesopotamialaiset, vaan suosivat puolinomadista elämäntapaa, lampaiden paimentamista. Ajan myötä amorilaiset viljakauppiaat nousivat huomattavaan asemaan ja perustivat omia itsenäisiä dynastioitaan useisiin Etelä-Mesopotamian kaupunkivaltioihin, joista huomattavimmat olivat Isin, Larsa, Eshnunna, Lagash ja myöhemmin Babylonin perustaminen valtioksi.<br/>Vanha babylonialainen aika<br/>Babylonialaisen päivämääräluettelon mukaan amorilaisten valta Babyloniassa alkoi (n. 1800- tai 1700-luvulla eaa.) Sumu-abum-nimisestä päälliköstä, joka julistautui itsenäiseksi naapurikaupunki Kazallusta. Hammurabi (r. 1792-1750 eaa.) on kuuluisa Babylonian lakien kodifioinnista Hammurabin säännöstöksi. Hän valloitti kaikki Etelä-Mesopotamian kaupungit ja kaupunkivaltiot, joihin kuuluivat Isin, Larsa, Ur, Uruk, Nippur, Lagash, Eridu, Kish, Adab, Eshnunna, Akshak, Akkad, Shuruppak, Bad-tibira, Sippar ja Girsu, yhdistäen ne yhdeksi valtakunnaksi, joka hallitsi Babyloniasta. Hammurabi valtasi ja valloitti myös Elamin idässä ja Marin ja Eblan kuningaskunnat luoteessa. Pitkällisen taistelun jälkeen vanhan Assyrian valtakunnan voimakkaan kuninkaan Isme-Daganin kanssa hän pakotti seuraajansa maksamaan veroa hallituskautensa loppupuolella, levittäen Babylonian valtaa Assyrian Hattian ja Hurrian siirtokuntiin Vähässä-Aasiassa.<br/>Hammurabin hallituskauden jälkeen koko Etelä-Mesopotamia tuli tunnetuksi Babyloniana, kun taas pohjoinen oli yhdistynyt jo vuosisatoja aiemmin Assyriaksi. Assyrialaiset kukistivat ja ajoivat ulos babylonialaiset ja amorilaiset. Kaukana Mesopotamian eteläpuolella puhkesi levottomuuksia, jotka muodostivat synnyinseudun Sealand-dynastian, ja elamilaiset valtasivat alueita Itä-Mesopotamiassa. Amorilaisdynastia jäi valtaan Babyloniassa, josta tuli jälleen pieni kaupunkivaltio.<br/>Vanhan Babylonin teksteissä on usein viittauksia Shamashiin, Sipparin auringonjumalaan, jota pidettiin ylimpänä jumaluutena, ja Mardukiin, jota pidettiin hänen poikanaan. Marduk korotettiin myöhemmin korkeampaan asemaan ja Shamash madaltui, mikä ehkä heijasteli Babylonin nousevaa poliittista valtaa.<br/>Vuonna 1595 eaa. heettiläisten valtakunta kukisti kaupungin Vähästä-Aasiasta. Tämän jälkeen Kassites Zagros-vuorilta Luoteis-Iranista valloitti Babylonin, aloittaen dynastian, joka kesti 435 vuotta, vuoteen 1160 eaa. saakka. Kaupunki nimettiin tänä aikana uudelleen Karanduniashiksi. Kassite Babylonista tuli lopulta Keski-Assyrian valtakunnan alaisuudessa (1365-1053 eaa.) pohjoiseen, ja Elam itään, molempien valtojen kilpaillessa kaupungin hallinnasta. Assyrian kuningas Tukulti-Ninurta I nousi Babylonian valtaistuimelle vuonna 1235 eaa.<br/>Vuoteen 1155 eaa. mennessä assyrialaisten ja elamiittien jatkuvien hyökkäysten ja aluevaltausten jälkeen kassit syöstiin vallasta Babyloniassa. Tällöin hallitsi ensimmäistä kertaa akkadilainen Etelä-Mesopotamian dynastia. Babylonia pysyi kuitenkin heikkona ja Assyrian herruuden alaisena. Sen tehottomat alkuperäiskuninkaat eivät kyenneet estämään ulkomaisten länsiseemiläisten uudisasukkaiden uusia aaltoja Levantin aavikoilta, mukaan lukien aramealaiset ja sutealaiset 1000-luvulla eaa., ja lopulta kaldealaiset 800-luvulla eaa. tunkeutuivat Babylonian alueille ja anastivat ne itselleen. Aramealaiset hallitsivat lyhyesti Babyloniaa 1000-luvun lopulla eaa.<br/>Neo-Assyrian valtakunnan (911-609 eaa.) vallan aikana Babylonia oli jatkuvan assyrialaisen herruuden tai suoran kontrollin alaisena. Assyrian Sanheribin hallituskaudella Babylonia oli jatkuvassa kapinatilassa, jota johti Merodak-Baladan-niminen päällikkö, liitossa elamiittien kanssa, ja se tukahdutettiin vain Babylonin kaupungin täydellisellä tuholla. Vuonna 689 eaa. sen muurit, temppelit ja palatsit tuhottiin, ja raunioista heitettiin kivimurskaa Arakhtuun, mereen, joka rajoittui varhaisempaan Babyloniin etelässä. Uskonnollisen keskuksen tuhoaminen järkytti monia, ja sitä seurannut Sanheribin murha, jonka kaksi hänen omaa poikaansa tekivät rukoillessaan Nisrok-jumalaa, katsottiin sovitukseksi. Näin ollen hänen seuraajansa Esarhaddon kiirehti rakentamaan vanhaa kaupunkia uudelleen ja tekemään siitä asumuksensa osan vuotta. Kuolemansa jälkeen Babyloniaa hallitsi hänen vanhempi poikansa, assyrialainen prinssi Shamash-shum-ukin, joka lopulta aloitti sisällissodan vuonna 652 eaa. omaa veljeään Ashurbanipalia vastaan, joka hallitsi Ninivessä. Shamash-shum-ukin värväsi avukseen muita Assyrian alaisia kansoja, muun muassa Etelä-Mesopotamian elamilaiset, persialaiset, kaldealaiset ja sutealaiset sekä Mesopotamian eteläpuolisilla aavikoilla asuneet kanaanilaiset ja arabit.<br/>Jälleen kerran assyrialaiset piirittivät Babyloniaa, nälkiintyivät antautumiseen ja sen liittolaiset voitettiin. Ashurbanipal juhli "sovinnon palvelusta", mutta ei uskaltanut "ottaa Belin käsiä". Kaupungin hallitsijaksi nimitettiin assyrialainen maaherra nimeltä Kandalanu, ja Ashurbanipal keräsi Babylonista tekstejä, jotka sisällytettiin hänen laajaan kirjastoonsa Niniveen.<br/>Ashurbanipalin kuoleman jälkeen Assyrian valtakunta ajautui epävakauteen johtuen useista sisäisistä sisällissodista Assyrian kuninkaiden Ashur-etil-ilanin, Sin-shumu-lishirin ja Sinsharishkunin valtakausilla. Lopulta Babylon käytti monien muiden lähi-idän osien tavoin hyväkseen Assyrian sisäistä kaaosta vapauttaakseen itsensä Assyrian vallasta. Sitä seuranneessa Assyrian valtakunnan kukistamisessa kansojen liitolla babylonialaiset näkivät toisen esimerkin jumalallisesta kostosta.<br/>Aiemmin tuntemattoman kaldealaispäällikön Nabopolassarin johdolla Babylonia pakeni Assyrian vallasta ja liittoutui Kyaksareksen, meedialaisten ja persialaisten kuninkaan, sekä skyyttalaisten ja cimmerialaisten kanssa ja lopulta tuhosi Assyrian valtakunnan vuosien 612 ja 605 eaa. välillä. Näin Babylonista tuli uusbabylonialaisen (joskus ja mahdollisesti virheellisesti kaldealais-) valtakunnan pääkaupunki.<br/>Babylonian itsenäisyyden elpymisen myötä seurasi uusi arkkitehtonisen toiminnan aikakausi, erityisesti hänen poikansa Nebukadnessar II:n valtakaudella (604-561 eaa.). Nebukadnessar määräsi keisarilliset alueet, mukaan lukien Etemenanki zigguratin, rakennettavaksi kokonaan uudelleen ja Ishtarin portin, joka oli huomattavin kahdeksasta portista Babylonin ympärillä. Ishtarin portin rekonstruktio sijaitsee Pergamon-museossa Berliinissä.<br/>Nebukadnessarin ansioksi luetaan myös Babylonin riippuvien puutarhojen – yhden muinaisen maailman seitsemästä ihmeestä – rakentaminen, jonka hän sanoi rakennetun koti-ikäväiselle vaimolleen Amyitisille. Saksalainen arkeologi Robert Koldewey spekuloi löytäneensä sen perustukset, mutta monet historioitsijat ovat eri mieltä paikasta. Stephanie Dalley on väittänyt, että riippuvat puutarhat sijaitsivat todellisuudessa Assyrian pääkaupungissa Ninivessä.<br/>Nebuchandnezzar yhdistetään myös tunnetusti Babylonian juutalaisten pakkosiirtolaisuuteen, joka oli seurausta keisarillisesta rauhantekniikasta, jota assyrialaiset käyttivät ja jossa valloitettujen alueiden etniset ryhmät karkotettiin joukoittain pääkaupunkiin.<br/>Vuonna 539 eaa. uusbabylonialainen valtakunta kukistui Kyyros Suurelle, Persian kuninkaalle, sotaretkellä, joka tunnettiin Opiksen taisteluna. Babylonin muureja pidettiin läpipääsemättöminä. Ainoa tie kaupunkiin oli yhden sen monista porteista tai Eufrat-joen kautta. Metalliset ritilät asennettiin veden alle, jolloin joki pääsi virtaamaan kaupungin muurien läpi estäen samalla tunkeutumisen. Persialaiset laativat suunnitelman päästä kaupunkiin joen kautta. Babylonialaisen kansallisen juhlan aikana Kyyroksen joukot ohjasivat Eufratin yläjuoksulle, jolloin Kyyroksen sotilaat pääsivät kaupunkiin lasketun veden kautta. Persian armeija valloitti kaupungin syrjäseudut, kun taas suurin osa kaupungin keskustassa olevista babylonialaisista oli tietämättömiä murtumasta. Kertomuksen laati Herodotos, ja se mainitaan myös osissa heprealaista Raamattua. (Herodotos kuvaili myös vallihaudan, valtavan korkean ja leveän muurin, joka oli sementoitu bitumilla ja jonka päällä oli rakennuksia, sekä sata porttia kaupunkiin. Hän kirjoittaa myös, että babylonialaiset käyttävät turbaaneja ja hajuvettä ja hautaavat kuolleensa hunajaan, harjoittavat rituaalista prostituutiota ja että kolme heimoa heidän joukossaan ei syö muuta kuin kalaa. Sata porttia voidaan pitää viittauksena Homerokseen, ja Archibald Henry Saycen lausuttua vuonna 1883 Herodotoksen kertomuksen Babylonista on suurelta osin katsottu edustavan kreikkalaista kansanperinnettä autenttisen matkan sijaan. Viime aikoina Dalley ja muut ovat kuitenkin ehdottaneet Herodotoksen huomioon ottamista vakavasti.)<br/>Heprealaisen Raamatun 2. Aikakirjan 36:n mukaan Kyyros antoi myöhemmin määräyksen, jonka mukaan vangitut ihmiset, myös juutalaiset, voivat palata omille mailleen. Kyyroksen sylinteristä löytyvä teksti on perinteisesti nähty raamatullisten oppineiden mielestä vahvistavana todisteena tästä politiikasta, vaikka tulkinta on kiistanalainen, koska tekstissä tunnistetaan vain mesopotamialaiset pyhäköt, mutta siinä ei mainita lainkaan juutalaisia, Jerusalemia tai Juudeaa.<br/>Kyyroksen ja sitä seuranneen Persian kuninkaan Dareios I:n johdolla Babylonista tuli 9. satraapin (Babylonia etelässä ja Athura pohjoisessa) pääkaupunki sekä oppineisuuden ja tieteellisen edistyksen keskus. Achaemenid Persiassa muinaista babylonialaista tähtitieteen ja matematiikan taitoa elvytettiin, ja babylonialaiset oppineet täydensivät tähtikuvioiden karttoja. Kaupungista tuli Persian valtakunnan hallinnollinen pääkaupunki, ja se pysyi merkittävänä yli kaksi vuosisataa. On tehty monia tärkeitä arkeologisia löytöjä, jotka voivat antaa paremman käsityksen tuosta aikakaudesta.<br/>Varhaiset Persian kuninkaat olivat yrittäneet ylläpitää Mardukin uskonnollisia seremonioita, mutta Dareios III:n valtakaudella yliverotus ja lukuisten sotien rasitus johtivat Babylonin tärkeimpien pyhäkköjen ja kanavien rappeutumiseen sekä ympäröivän alueen epävakauteen. Kapinayrityksiä oli lukuisia ja vuonna 522 eaa. (Nebuchadnezzar III), 521 eaa. (Nebuchadnezzar IV) ja 482 eaa. (Bel-shimani ja Shamash-eriba) syntyperäiset babylonialaiset kuninkaat saivat lyhyesti takaisin itsenäisyytensä. Nämä kapinat kuitenkin tukahdutettiin nopeasti ja Babylonia pysyi Persian vallan alla kaksi vuosisataa, Aleksanteri Suuren tuloon vuonna 331 eaa. saakka.<br/>Lokakuussa 331 eaa. Dareios III, Persian valtakunnan viimeinen akhamenidikuningas, kukistettiin muinaisen Makedonian hallitsijan Aleksanteri Suuren voimin Gaugamelan taistelussa. Syntyperäinen kertomus tästä maahanhyökkäyksestä panee merkille Aleksanterin päätöksen olla menemättä sen asukkaiden koteihin.<br/>Aleksanterin aikana Babylon kukoisti jälleen oppineisuuden ja kaupankäynnin keskuksena, mutta Aleksanterin kuoltua vuonna 323 eaa. Nebukadnessarin palatsissa hänen valtakuntansa jaettiin hänen kenraaliensa, diadokkien, kesken, ja pian alkoivat vuosikymmenten taistelut. Jatkuva kuohunta käytännössä tyhjensi Babylonin kaupungin. 275 eaa. päivätyssä taulussa kerrotaan, että Babylonin asukkaat kuljetettiin Seleukiaan, jonne rakennettiin palatsi ja temppeli (Esagila). Tämän karkotuksen myötä Babylonista tuli mitätön kaupunki, vaikka yli sata vuotta myöhemmin sen vanhassa pyhäkössä suoritettiin yhä uhreja.<br/>Uudistunut Persian hallinto<br/>Parthian ja sassanidien valtakuntien alaisuudessa Babylonista (kuten Assyriasta) tuli näiden Persian valtakuntien provinssi yhdeksän vuosisadan ajan, aina vuoden 650 jälkeen. Se säilytti oman kulttuurinsa ja kansansa, jotka puhuivat aramean kielen muunnelmia ja jotka edelleen viittasivat kotimaahansa Babylonina. Esimerkkejä heidän kulttuuristaan löytyy Babylonian Talmudista, gnostilaisesta mandaelaisesta uskonnosta, itäisestä riitekristillisyydestä ja profeetta Manin uskonnosta. Kristinusko esiteltiin Mesopotamialle 1. ja 2. vuosisadalla jaa., ja Babylon oli idän kirkon piispan paikka aina arabien/islamilaisten valloituksen jälkeiseen aikaan saakka.<br/>700-luvun puolivälissä Mesopotamiaan tunkeutui ja sinne asettui laajeneva muslimivaltakunta, ja sen jälkeen seurasi islamisaation kausi. Babylon hajotettiin provinssiksi ja idän kristinuskon aramea ja kirkko syrjäytettiin lopulta. Ibn Hauqal mainitsee pienen Baabel-nimisen kylän 900-luvulla; myöhemmät matkailijat kuvaavat vain raunioita.<br/>Eurooppalaiset matkustajat eivät monissa tapauksissa voineet selvittää kaupungin sijaintia tai erehtyä Fallujasta sen vuoksi. 1100-luvulla matkustanut Benjamin Tudelalainen mainitsee Babylonin, mutta ei ole selvää, menikö hän sinne. Toiset viittasivat Bagdadiin Babylonina tai Uutena Babylonina ja kuvailivat alueella kohdattuja erilaisia rakennelmia Baabelin torniksi. Pietro della Valle löysi muinaisen paikan 1600-luvulla ja pani merkille bitumilla sementoitujen sekä paistettujen että kuivattujen mutatiilien olemassaolon.<br/>Claudius J. Rich, Muistelma Babylonin raunioista (1815), s.<br/>1700-luvulla Babyloniin virtasi yhä enemmän matkailijoita, muun muassa Carsten Niebuhr ja Pierre-Joseph de Beauchamp, sekä mittauksia sen leveysasteesta. Beauchampin vuonna 1792 englanniksi kääntämä muistelmateos sai Englannin Itä-Intian kauppakomppanian ohjaamaan Bagdadissa ja Basrassa toimivia agenttejaan hankkimaan Mesopotamian pyhäinjäännöksiä toimitettavaksi Lontooseen.<br/>Kaivaukset ja tutkimus<br/>Austen Henry Layard teki joitakin kaikuja lyhyen vierailun aikana vuonna 1850 ennen kuin hylkäsi paikan. Fulgence Fresnel ja Julius Oppert kaivoivat Babyloniaa raskaasti esiin 1852-1854. Monet heidän työnsä hedelmistä kuitenkin menetettiin, kun yli 40 laatikollista esineitä sisältävä lautta upposi Tigris-jokeen.<br/>Henry Rawlinson ja George Smith työskentelivät siellä lyhyesti vuonna 1854. Seuraavan kaivauksen suoritti Hormuzd Rassam British Museumin puolesta. Työt alkoivat vuonna 1879, jatkuen vuoteen 1882 asti, ja niiden taustalla oli alueen laajamittainen ryöstely. Käyttämällä teollisen mittakaavan kaivauksia esineiden etsimiseen Rassam sai talteen suuren määrän nuolenpäätabletteja ja muita löytöjä. Tuolloin yleiset innokkaat kaivausmenetelmät aiheuttivat merkittävää vahinkoa arkeologiselle kontekstille. Monet tabletit olivat ilmestyneet markkinoille vuonna 1876 ennen Rassamin kaivausten alkamista.<br/>Robert Koldeweyn johtama Saksan itämaisen seuran ryhmä suoritti ensimmäiset tieteelliset arkeologiset kaivaukset Babylonissa. Työtä tehtiin päivittäin vuodesta 1899 vuoteen 1917. Kaivauksen ensisijaisiin ponnisteluihin kuului Mardukin temppeli ja siihen johtava kulkue sekä kaupunginmuuri. Esineet, mukaan lukien Ishtarin portin palaset ja sadat talteenotetut taulut, lähetettiin takaisin Saksaan, jossa Koldeweyn kollega Walter Andrae rekonstruoi ne näyttelyiksi Vorderasiatisches Museum Berliniin., Saksalaiset arkeologit pakenivat ennen brittiläisiä joukkoja vuonna 1917 ja jälleen monet esineet katosivat seuraavina vuosina.<br/>Saksan arkeologisen instituutin muita töitä johtivat Heinrich J. Lenzen vuonna 1956 ja Hansjörg Schmid vuonna 1962. Lenzenin työt käsittelivät pääasiassa hellenististä teatteria, ja Schmid keskittyi temppelin ziggurat Etemenankiin.<br/>Paikkaa kaivettiin vuonna 1974 Torinon arkeologisen tutkimus- ja kaivauskeskuksen ja irakilais-italialaisen arkeologisten tieteiden instituutin puolesta. Pääpaino oli vanhojen saksalaisten tietojen uudelleentarkastelussa esiin tulleiden kysymysten selvittämisessä. Lisätyöt vuosina 1987-1989 keskittyivät Isharan ja Ninurtan temppeleitä ympäröivään alueeseen Shu-Annan kaupunginosassa Babylonissa.<br/>Babylonian restaurointipyrkimysten aikana Irakin valtion muinaismuisto- ja perintöjärjestö suoritti laajoja tutkimuksia, kaivauksia ja raivauksia, mutta näiden arkeologisten toimintojen laajempi julkaiseminen on ollut vähäistä. Itse asiassa suurin osa tunnetuista tauluista kaikista nykyaikaisista kaivauksista on edelleen julkaisematta.<br/>Babylonin sijaintipaikka on ollut Irakin kulttuurinen voimavara siitä lähtien, kun nykyinen Irakin valtio perustettiin vuonna 1921. Aluetta suojeli ja kaivettiin virallisesti Britannian hallinnon alaisen Irakin kuningaskunnan, josta myöhemmin tuli Irakin hashemiittinen kuningaskunta, ja sen seuraajat: Arabiliitto, Irakin tasavalta, Baathistinen Irak (kutsutaan virallisesti myös Irakin tasavallaksi) ja Irakin tasavalta. Babylonialaisia kuvia esiintyy ajoittain irakilaisissa postikorteissa ja postimerkeissä. 1960-luvulla paikalle rakennettiin kopio Ishtarin portista ja rekonstruktio Ninmakhin temppelistä.<br/>14. helmikuuta 1978 Irakin baathistihallitus aloitti Saddam Husseinin johdolla "Arkeologisen Babylonin restauroinnin": muinaisen kaupungin piirteiden rekonstruoimisen raunioidensa huipulle. Näihin ominaisuuksiin kuului Nebuchandnezzarin eteläinen palatsi, jossa oli 250 huonetta, viisi sisäpihaa ja 30 metrinen sisäänkäyntikaari. Hanke vahvisti myös Kulkureittiä, Babylonin leijonaa ja kaupungin hellenistisellä kaudella rakennettua amfiteatteria. Vuonna 1982 hallitus löi seitsemän kolikon sarjan, jossa oli Babylonin ikonisia piirteitä. Syyskuussa 1987 järjestettiin Babylonin kansainvälinen festivaali, ja vuosittain sen jälkeen vuoteen 2002 asti (lukuun ottamatta vuosia 1990 ja 1991) tämän teoksen esittelemiseksi.<br/>Hussein asensi itsensä ja Nebukadnessarin muotokuvan raunioiden suulle ja kaiversi nimensä moniin tiiliin Nebukadnessaria jäljitellen. Eräässä usein toistuvassa piirtokirjoituksessa lukee: "Tämän rakensi Saddam Hussein, Nebukadnessarin poika, Irakin kirkastamiseksi". Näistä tiilistä tuli Husseinin kukistumisen jälkeen haluttuja keräilyesineitä. Samanlaisia hankkeita toteutettiin Ninivessä, Nimrudissa, Assurissa ja Hatrassa arabisaavutusten suuruuden osoittamiseksi.<br/>Kun vuoden 1991 Persianlahden sota päättyi, Hussein halusi rakentaa Saddam Hill -nimisen modernin palatsin joidenkin vanhojen raunioiden ylle, zigguratin pyramidityyliin. Vuonna 2003 hän aikoi rakentaa köysiratalinjan Babylonin ylle, mutta suunnitelmat seisahtuivat vuoden 2003 Irakin valloitukseen.<br/>Yhdysvaltain ja Puolan miehitys<br/>Vuoden 2003 Irakin valloituksen jälkeen Babyloniaa ympäröivä alue tuli Yhdysvaltain joukkojen hallintaan, ennen kuin se luovutettiin puolalaisille joukoille syyskuussa 2003. Yhdysvaltain joukot ovat miehittäneet paikan jo jonkin aikaa ja aiheuttaneet korjaamatonta vahinkoa arkeologiselle aineistolle. British Museumin Lähi-idän osaston raportissa tohtori John Curtis kuvaili, kuinka osia arkeologisesta kohteesta tasoitettiin luomaan laskeutumisalue helikoptereille ja pysäköintialueet raskaille ajoneuvoille. Curtis kirjoitti miehitysjoukoista:<br/>Ne aiheuttivat huomattavaa vahinkoa Ishtarin portille, joka on yksi antiikin kuuluisimmista muistomerkeistä [...] Yhdysvaltain armeijan ajoneuvot murskasivat 2600 vuotta vanhat tiiliset jalkakäytävät, arkeologiset sirpaleet hajaantuivat eri puolille paikkaa, yli 12 juoksuhautaa ajettiin muinaisiin esiintymiin ja sotilaalliset maansiirtoprojektit saastuttivat paikan tuleville tutkijasukupolville.<br/>Yhdysvaltain armeijan tiedottaja väitti, että konepajatoiminnasta keskusteltiin "Babylon-museon johtajan" kanssa. Irakin valtion perintö- ja muinaismuistolautakunnan johtaja Donny George sanoi, että "viestien selvittäminen kestää vuosikymmeniä" ja arvosteli puolalaisia joukkoja siitä, että ne aiheuttivat "kauheita vahinkoja" paikalle. Puola päätti vuonna 2004 asettaa kaupungin Irakin hallintaan, ja tilasi raportin nimeltä Report Concerning the Condition of the Preservation of the Babylon Archaeological Site, jonka se esitteli kokouksessaan 11.-13. joulukuuta 2004.

| Photo: Karl Gabor.<br/>“Institutions rule”, economist Dani Rodrik and two colleagues wrote in 2004. By this, they meant that the laws that rule over politics, markets, and companies crucially determine how the economy works and develops. In countries where private property is protected, where freedom of contract obtains, and where democratic principles and the rule of law are established, there are more entrepreneurial initiatives creating value, and companies can grow and generate employment and pay good wages.<br/>This is the starting point for a lot of our research – we study how laws and rules affect human and corporate behavior.<br/>Laws and rules are referred to as formal institutions. There are also informal institutions, such as values, attitudes, morality, and norms, which are at least as important. They also matter for how markets and firms function. If a large share of the population trusts others it is, for example, less costly to do business, since a handshake suffices. If people, in general, are tolerant this might mean that the most suitable job applicants get the jobs irrespective of what group they belong to, which is beneficial for productivity.<br/>We focus, to a large extent, on conditions for enterprise. Actual and potential business people are influenced in their decision-making by laws and rules, as well as by values, morality, and norms in society. Innovations and investment are stimulated by stable and beneficial rules, e.g., with regard to the quality of the legal system, taxation, and regulation, and by co-workers whose values and attitudes are characterized by trust, tolerance, individualism, and responsibility.<br/>Some of our research questions are:<br/>- How are the incentives of companies to hire people who have a hard time getting into the labor market by employment protection laws?<br/>- Can the strong economic growth of Sweden from the late 1800s be explained by changed voting rules benefiting industrialists?<br/>- In what way has globalization affected tolerance and populism?<br/>- Has the spread of democracy and markets increased trust between people?<br/>- What explains the financial situation and decisions of immigrants?<br/>- Do companies work more or less well depending on the cultural background of their employees?<br/>- What are the consequences of the privatization of welfare services?<br/>The following researchers are working within the program:<br/>Niclas Berggren, Andreas Bergh, Lars Calmfors, Gabriel Heller Sahlgren, Joakim Jansson, Daniel Knutsson, Martin Ljunge, Therese Nilsson, Per Skedinger, Björn Tyrefors och Johan Wennström. | In our previous article we discussed the problem of underrepresented languages in NLP, and how John Snow Labs collaborated with the community to facilitate the use of the open-source Spark NLP library in over 190 languages.<br/>Part of these advancements of Spark NLP was the inclusion of languages with very different structures, such as Arabic, Persian or Urdu.<br/>In this post, we’ll dive deeper into the use of Spark NLP for the Arabic language, which is the fourth most used language on the internet, and thus deserves special attention.<br/>Characteristics of the Arabic language<br/>Arabic is a complex language with a very unique structure. Using NLP to analyze text in Arabic is, therefore, a very different endeavor than understanding the text in English or Latin languages. Here are some characteristics that make Arabic particularly distinct from other languages:<br/>- Words are written from right to left.<br/>- Arabic has MANY regional dialects, which are often incomprehensible to each other.<br/>- It’s a very rich language with many words describing a similar thing. For example, there are 14 stages of love alone, depending on how strong the feelings are, and there are hundreds of words in the Arabic language for one as common as “camel”.<br/>- Capitalization and abbreviations don’t exist in Arabic.<br/>- Complex and unusual methods of constructing words from a basic root and direct translation is very difficult without considering the context of the sentences and words around them.<br/>- Short vowels are not prominent in the Arabic language, and often not written. For example, maktab (office) is written as mktb, omitting the vowels, much like stenographic shorthand.<br/>Challenges of using NLP to understand Arabic<br/>The characteristics mentioned above make it particularly hard to apply existing NLP models to Arabic and provide a burden for innovation in NLP to understand Arabic languages. On top of that, big, high-quality datasets needed to train the models are limited, and those datasets that are publicly accessible are mostly only found in Arabic search engines and are therefore hard to find for English-speaking researchers and data scientists.<br/>Despite the challenges of developing NLP models to analyze Arabic, John Snow Labs has set a special focus on this research area to contribute to a much more efficient understanding of text written in Arabic, such as medical reports or important historic documents. Having NLP models for Arabic in place enables researchers and scientists around the globe to extract relevant information directly from the original documents instead of translating them first to English or another language and then running it through the model. This positively affects the accuracy when extracting entities in text.<br/>Developing Spark NLP models for Arabic<br/>Of all the libraries considering underrepresented languages, Spark NLP is probably the largest, and it was obvious for John Snow Labs that it should support the Arabic language as well, despite its complexity. The team began training the model for certain essential NLP tasks including named entity recognition and translation. With the support of internal collaborators who spoke Arabic, John Snow Labs also worked closely with Arabic speaking members of the Spark NLP community who contributed to the development of the models. The existence of vast amounts of textual material written in Arabic was used to efficiently train the models and measure their accuracies.<br/>In comparison to the analysis of languages written from left to right, Arabic required an approach beyond simple rule-based methods. Common tasks such as text simplification to identify the root of a specific word or comma-based rules were no longer reliable. Multiple machine learning and deep learning techniques were used instead to develop the models for these new supported languages, including Hebrew, Persian and Urdu.<br/>Tasks supported by Spark NLP for Arabic<br/>Today, Spark NLP has 45 pre-trained models that can annotate data provided in Arabic. Supported tasks include:<br/>- Named entity recognition -> extract key elements from text and classify them into predefined categories.<br/>- Translation -> translating Arabic to English, Spanish, Hebrew, Greek, French, and many more languages.<br/>- Word and sentence embeddings -> detecting text vectors for sentiment analytics, text classifications, Named Entity Recognition (NER)<br/>- Stop words removal -> filtering out irrelevant words before processing the analysis of the text, such as prepositions, pronouns, conjunctions, etc.<br/>- Part-of-Speech (POS) -> assigning parts of speech to each word, such as noun, verb, adjective, etc.<br/>- Lemmatization -> method that switches any kind of a word to its base root mode.<br/>You can check out the live demo and notebook here to see how entities in Arabic text are recognized using Spark NLP.<br/>Some simple and popular tasks, such as text classification, sentiment analysis, or summarization are still missing. The problem is not that these are too hard to train or that datasets are missing but with 190 languages supported by Spark NLP, it’s a question of prioritization. Before extending a model with further tasks and putting resources into it, it is evaluated what tasks are actually needed in a specific language to maximize the benefits of the library for the community. | From the moment of Israel’s birth, the army has occupied a central role in society. In 1948, with the country in the throes of its War for Independence, the interim government ordered the establishment of one unified military that was called Tzvah Hagannah L’Yisrael—abbreviated to Tzahal—Hebrew for “Israel Defense Forces.” Within months, Jewish underground movements that had fought the British Mandate were dismantled and assimilated into the new military whose job it was to fend off invading Arab armies.<br/>A Jewish Military<br/>During the first decades of the country’s existence the IDF was lionized by the public as the embodiment of Zionist values. The first Jewish military in 2,000 years was charged with protecting a nation still reeling from the genocide of European Jewry. And the stunning success of the small motivated army while surrounded by bigger enemies gave the military the image of a mythic David against Goliath.<br/>The army’s code of ethics features a section on “purity of arms,” reinforcing the image among Israelis that their army upheld humanistic universal values even under fire. This concept—called “toharat haneshek” in Hebrew—refers to a code of honor of the Israeli Defense Forces that states that arms are to be used only in defense, and even then judiciously with great care that innocent civilian lives be protected.<br/>The army also performed (and continues to perform) in an important social role as a primary melting pot and equalizer for a country of immigrants. From the age of 18 every Israeli male and female is required to serve three and two years, respectively, of compulsory military service. That requirement brought the rural kibbutz resident together with the Tel Aviv urbanite, the modern Orthodox together with the secular, and the Sabra (native Israeli) together with the immigrant. The army was decidedly informal, with enlisted men of different ranks dispensing with the salutes and formal greetings of other militaries. This also served to reinforce the country’s egalitarian spirit.<br/>Jewish Israelis are required to serve, as are male Israelis who are Druse and Cherkessian, two non-Jewish minority groups who are faithful to the state of Israel. Male Bedouin Israelis—members of semi-nomadic tribes in the southern portion of Israel—often volunteer for the draft. Most haredi (ultra-Orthodox) Israelis are exempted from service because they are studying at yeshivot (religious academies), a point of contention between secular and religious Israelis.<br/>The nation’s Arab-Israelis are exempt from compulsory army service due to the consideration that their military service might put them into a situation where they would be forced to engage in combat with relatives from neighboring Arab armies. While most Israelis—both Jewish and Arab—remain satisfied with this status quo, some have expressed concern that Arab-Israelis’ exclusion from mandatory military duty puts them at a distinct social and economic disadvantage because many Jewish-Israelis make social connections and receive training in the army that lay the foundation of their careers after the army service. Useimmat haredit (ultraortodoksit) israelilaiset on vapautettu palveluksesta, koska he opiskelevat jeshivotissa (uskonnollisissa akatemioissa), joka on kiistakohta maallisten ja uskonnollisten israelilaisten välillä.<br/>Kansakunnan arabi-israelilaiset on vapautettu pakollisesta asepalveluksesta johtuen siitä, että heidän asepalveluksensa saattaisi saattaa heidät tilanteeseen, jossa heidät pakotettaisiin taistelemaan naapurimaa arabiarmeijoiden sukulaisia vastaan. Vaikka useimmat israelilaiset - sekä juutalaiset että arabit - ovat edelleen tyytyväisiä tähän status quoon, jotkut ovat ilmaisseet huolensa siitä, että arabien ja Israelin sulkeminen pois pakollisesta asevelvollisuudesta asettaa heidät selvästi sosiaaliseen ja taloudelliseen epäedulliseen asemaan, koska monet juutalais-israelilaiset tekevät sosiaalisia yhteyksiä ja saavat koulutusta armeijassa, joka luo heidän uransa perustan armeijan palveluksen jälkeen. Jotkut arabi-israelilaiset palvelevat nyt samankaltaisessa ohjelmassa, joka on suunniteltu ortodoksisille naisille - Sherut Leumille eli kansalliselle palvelulle -, joka sallii arabi-israelilaisten osallistua maahansa ja saada joitakin armeijan palvelun etuja. Kuten mikä tahansa armeija, palvelu tarjoaa laajan valikoiman työpaikkoja, jotka vaihtelevat jalkaväestä tiedusteluun, sotilassoittokuntaan. Jäsenyys eliitin kommandoyksikössä kantaa eniten arvovaltaa. Kilpailu päästä näihin yksiköihin on usein kovaa, erityisesti tulla taistelukoneiden lentäjiksi kehutuissa ilmavoimissa. Israelin armeijalla on myös koulutusyksikkö ja viihdeyksikkö, sekä mediaan liittyviä yksiköitä, jotka tarjoavat esimerkiksi "armeijakuvaajan" tehtäviä.<br/>Yrittäessään antaa kaikille israelilaisille mahdollisuuden suorittaa kansallista palvelusta armeija tarjoaa useita raiteita maan eri väestöryhmille. Tal-laki julistettiin perustuslain vastaiseksi Israelin korkeimmassa oikeudessa 21. helmikuuta 2012.<br/>Jotkut sotilaat päättävät tehdä armeijasta uransa. Ammattisotilaat saavat joukon luontoisetuja, muun muassa anteliaita eläkesuunnitelmia, jotka alkavat, kun he saavuttavat nelikymppisenä. Urapalveluradan ehkä tärkein etu on palvelun aikana muodostettu tuttavien verkosto, joka usein avaa sotilaille ovia huomattaviin työpaikkoihin heidän lähdettyään armeijasta.<br/>Armeija on myös antanut korvaamattoman arvokkaan panoksen Israelin talouteen. Palkkio Israelin laadullisen etulyöntiaseman säilyttämisestä ylivoimaisiin vihollisjoukkoihin nähden on saanut armeijan etsimään huipputeknologiaa ja kannustanut sotilaita käyttämään laitteita luovasti. Monet armeijasta tulevat innovaatiot ovat myöhemmin käytöstä poistettujen sotilaiden siviilisovelluksiin käyttämiä, ilmiö, josta tuli Israelin kasvavan korkean teknologian teollisuuden perusta. Monet eliittiyksiköt jopa kehittivät maineen sellaisten whiz-lasten tuottamisesta, jotka jättivät armeijan perustaakseen startup-yrityksiä tai tullakseen nykyisten yritysten projektipäälliköiksi.<br/>Yksi ammatti, jossa kuuluisia sotilaita etsitään, on politiikka. Kolme neljästä viimeisestä Israelin pääministeristä tuli politiikkaan suoraan armeijasta. | In 1654, when New York governor Peter Stuyvesant needed help collecting taxes, he turned to Deborah Moody. The career of the Salem, Mass., woman then came full circle from outcast to political power broker.<br/>A friend of John Winthrop, Moody came to Massachusetts to escape religious intolerance in England sometime before 1640. Moody’s husband, a baronet, had died 10 years earlier. Her son had sold most of the familial lands in Wiltshire.<br/>In trying to reestablish her life as a widow, Lady Moody had visited London where she attended some of the more exotic religious services of the day among the Baptists and Quakers.<br/>Deborah Moody, Anabaptist<br/>Deborah Moody became an Anabaptist, a school of thought that held people should not be baptized as children but rather as adults.<br/>Anabaptism amounted to heresy in the 1630s. Lady Moody, now a heretic, had to appear before England’s Star Chamber, the high court that heard cases involving the nobility. The court had the power to decide how long nobles could live away from their ancestral estates, and it ordered Lady Moody to go home to Wiltshire.<br/>Moody had considered joining Winthrop and his settlement in America. Though she had long criticized the Church of England, the prospect of joining in the new colony had initially seemed too daunting. But with this new frustration, Deborah Moody decided to move.<br/>At age 54, she pulled up stakes and sailed for America. She first settled in Saugus on a grant of 400 acres for a plantation. Shortly, however, she bought a small house in Salem, and then a farm of more than 1,000 acres from John Humphrey in nearby Swampscott. Humphrey, a one-time deputy governor of Massachusetts, had grown tired of the new colony and was returning to England.<br/>She had the misfortune of moving into the neighborhood of the Rev. Hugh Peter, also a fellow member of the church at Salem. Given her unconventional beliefs she could hardly have chosen a worse neighbor. Peter was one of the ministers who had successfully prosecuted Anne Hutchinson just two years earlier for her Anabaptist beliefs.<br/>Peter succeeded in having her banished from Massachusetts for her illegal religious beliefs.<br/>Faced with the increasing hostility, Deborah Moody decided to leave Massachusetts just as Anne Hutchinson and Roger Williams had. Rather than head for Rhode Island, however, Moody chose New Amsterdam (now New York).<br/>John Winthrop noted in his journal: “The Ladye Moodye, a wise and anciently religious woman, being taken with the error of denying baptism to infants, was dealt withal by many of the elders and others, and admonished by the church of Salem (whereof she was a member); but persisting still, and to avoid further trouble, etc., she removed to the Dutch, at the advice of all her friends. Many others infected with Anabaptism removed thither also. She was after excommunicated.”<br/>Moody and a group of her followers who relocated in 1643 to New Amsterdam found the Dutch government much more tolerant. She still had considerable wealth, and New Amsterdam welcomed her. The governor granted her 7,000 acres on the southwestern tip of Long Island.<br/>She called her new settlement Gravesend. But the new town had a rocky beginning. Though she had cautioned all her fellow settlers to pay the local Native Americans for any land they wanted to use, there were misunderstandings. The indigenous people did not embrace the concept of property ownership. Payments were for fishing rights, they believed, while the colonists expected full ownership of the land they paid for.<br/>Soon, the Natives attacked and the settlers gathered in Lady Moody’s sizable home for protection. Deborah Moody toyed with the idea of returning to her home and land in Massachusetts. Peter had by now returned to England. He would eventually be executed as a regicide for his role in the trial and execution of Charles I.<br/>But Peter was not the only enemy Moody had. Moody wrote to Winthrop asking whether she might return, and on-again, off-again governor John Endecott urged him to advise her against it unless she was prepared to renounce her views.<br/>“I shall desire that she may not have advice to return to this jurisdiction, unless she will acknowledge her evil in opposing the churches and leave her opinions behind her, for she is a dangerous woman,” Endecott wrote.<br/>Deborah Moody, Brooklynite<br/>Moody and her fellow Gravesend villagers fortified their small town and dug in. Over the coming years, the town grew and Deborah Moody earned widespread respect. Gravesend did not adopt a single, sanctioned church. Rather, people worshiped as they wished with many, like Lady Moody, simply conducting services in their homes.<br/>As stability set in, Lady Moody emerged as a major power in the town. She had so much influence that in 1654 Gov. Stuyvesant had to call on her to mediate a dispute over taxes.<br/>Lady Deborah Moody died in 1659 at 73, and Gravesend — now part of Brooklyn — bears her stamp to this day. You can still walk some of the streets she laid out in 1643.<br/>With thanks to Liz Loveland for her editorial suggestion. Lady Moody, nyt kerettiläinen, joutui astumaan Englannin tähtikamarin eteen, korkeimpaan oikeuteen, joka käsitteli aatelistoa koskevia oikeusjuttuja. Tuomioistuimella oli valta päättää, kuinka kauan aateliset voisivat elää poissa esi-isiensä tiluksilta, ja se määräsi Lady Moodyn palaamaan kotiin Wiltshireen.<br/>Moody oli harkinnut liittymistä Winthropiin ja hänen asutukseensa Amerikassa. Vaikka hän oli jo pitkään arvostellut Englannin kirkkoa, mahdollisuus liittyä uuteen siirtokuntaan oli aluksi tuntunut liian pelottavalta. Mutta tämän uuden turhautumisen myötä Deborah Moody päätti muuttaa.<br/>54-vuotiaana hän nosti panoksia ja purjehti Amerikkaan, asettui ensin Saugukseen 400 eekkerin apurahalla plantaasia varten. Pian hän kuitenkin osti pienen talon Salemista ja sen jälkeen yli 1000 eekkerin maatilan John Humphreyltä läheisestä Swampscottista. Humphrey, Massachusettsin entinen varakuvernööri, oli kyllästynyt uuteen siirtokuntaan ja oli palaamassa Englantiin.<br/>Hänellä oli epäonnea muuttaa pastori Hugh Peterin naapurustoon, joka oli myös Salemin kirkon jäsen. Epätavanomaisten uskomustensa vuoksi hän tuskin olisi voinut valita huonompaa naapuria. Peter oli yksi niistä ministereistä, jotka olivat syyttäneet menestyksekkäästi Anne Hutchinsonia vain kaksi vuotta aiemmin anabaptistisista uskomuksistaan.<br/>Peterin onnistui saada hänet karkotetuksi Massachusettsista laittomien uskonnollisten vakaumustensa vuoksi.<br/>Kasvavan vihamielisyyden vuoksi Deborah Moody päätti lähteä Massachusettsista aivan kuten Anne Hutchinson ja Roger Williams. Sen sijaan, että suuntaisi Rhode Islandille, Moody kuitenkin valitsi New Amsterdamin (nykyinen New York).<br/>John Winthrop totesi päiväkirjassaan: "Ladye Moodye, viisas ja muinoin uskonnollinen nainen, joka oli otettu sillä erehdyksellä, että hän olisi kieltänyt kasteen imeväisille, joutui monien vanhinten ja muiden käsittelemäksi, ja jota Salemin kirkko (jonka jäsen hän oli) kehotti; mutta hellittämättä, ja välttääkseen lisää vaikeuksia jne., hän siirtyi hollantilaisille, kaikkien ystäviensä neuvosta. Myös monet muut anabaptismin tartuttamat poistettiin sieltä, ja hänet erotettiin kirkosta."<br/>Moody ja joukko hänen seuraajiaan, jotka muuttivat vuonna 1643 Uuteen-Amsterdamiin, totesivat Alankomaiden hallituksen olevan paljon suvaitsevaisempi. Kuvernööri myönsi hänelle 7 000 eekkeriä Long Islandin lounaiskärjessä.<br/>Hän kutsui uutta asutustaan Gravesendiksi, - mutta uudella kaupungilla oli kivinen alku, - vaikka hän oli varoittanut kaikkia siirtokuntalaisiaan maksamaan paikallisille intiaanille mistä tahansa maasta, jota he halusivat käyttää, - syntyi väärinkäsityksiä. Alkuperäiskansat eivät omaksuneet omistusoikeuden käsitettä, vaan maksut kohdistuivat kalastusoikeuksiin, he uskoivat, kun taas siirtolaiset odottivat maksamansa maan täyttä omistusta.<br/>Pian intiaanit hyökkäsivät ja uudisasukkaat kokoontuivat Lady Moodyn suureen kotiin suojaksi. Deborah Moody leikitteli ajatuksella palata kotiinsa ja mailleen Massachusettsiin. Peter oli jo palannut Englantiin. Hänet teloitettaisiin lopulta regicidinä roolistaan Kaarle I:n oikeudenkäynnissä ja teloituksessa.<br/>Mutta Peter ei ollut ainoa vihollinen, joka Moodylla oli. Moody kirjoitti Winthropille kysyen, voisiko tämä palata, ja jälleen kerran kuvernööri John Endecott kehotti häntä neuvomaan häntä sitä vastaan, ellei hän ollut valmis luopumaan näkemyksistään.<br/>– Toivon, ettei hänellä olisi neuvoja palata tälle hallintoalueelle, ellei hän tunnusta pahuuttaan vastustaessaan kirkkoja ja jätä mielipiteitään taakseen, sillä hän on vaarallinen nainen, Endecott kirjoitti.<br/>Deborah Moody, brooklyniläinen kirjailija<br/>Moody ja hänen Gravesend-kylätoverinsa linnoittivat pikkukaupunkinsa ja kaivoivat sisään. Tulevien vuosien aikana kaupunki kasvoi ja Deborah Moody ansaitsi laajaa kunnioitusta. Gravesend ei omaksunut yhtään, hyväksyttyä kirkkoa.

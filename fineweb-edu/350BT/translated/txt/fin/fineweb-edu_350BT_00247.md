| New idea for measurement, the logic behind, and the improvement of ”Standard model” written by Nimphius et al (2016)<br/>Before going down the rabbit hole I highly recommend you reading the article from Sophia Nimphius and her colleagues (2016): Change of direction deficit: A more isolated measure of change of direction performance than total 505 time. It represents a base of my idea written below. Further down I named their model a “Standard model”. IMPORTANT NOTE: If you are not interested in a construct and details, just skip to the “Simple instructions and equation” part.<br/>What is a COD deficit and why should we bother with validity?<br/>Change of direction (COD) can be many things. Often, we describe it as a “mechanical” part of agility, but it can also appear as its standalone athletic ability. For our purpose it will represent 180° change of direction after a linear sprint.<br/>In a similar manner, a COD deficit alone can have multiple definitions. Nimphius et al (2016) defined the COD deficit as a practical measure to isolate COD ability independent of sprint speed. The formula below is showing how COD deficit is being calculated. Basically, it’s a time difference between 10-meter time (standing start) and 505 test time.<br/>COD deficit = 505 time – 10-meter time<br/>The main point here is that we do a 10-meter linear sprint to determine athlete’s sprint ability. We do this because we know that 505 test (introduced in Figures 1 and 2), which should measure a COD ability, consists also of the linear sprint and thus making it less valid for determining athlete’s COD ability. Once we’ve done both tests, we subtract 10-meter time from 505 time and take care of removing linear sprint ability from 505 test. By doing so, we’ve extracted COD ability from 505 test, and this is called the COD deficit.<br/>COD deficit could also be a bilateral deficit where left leg turn is faster than the right one. But I’ll stick to the upper definition as it’s more relevant to the present text. I also won’t write about application of COD deficit in practice as it is planned for the Part 2 of COD deficit series. But for the sake of Part 1 let’s say knowing our players’ COD deficit is useful. That means we’ll measure it and make further intra/inter player comparisons. However, the idea of knowing your athletes’ COD deficit is certainly interesting but we as S&C coaches must ensure that our test is really measuring COD ability and not something else.<br/>And this is called validity. Nimphius et. al (2016) already excluded some of “wanna be” COD tests due to a lack of validity. Those tests (e. g. L-run, T-test, proagility shuttle…) either last too long (metabolic concern) or use non-sagittal plane (roughly speaking) movements prior to COD action which is often not the case within game type situations. Don’t get me wrong, it’s nothing wrong with those tests but they are not a relevant measure of pure COD ability.<br/>Fine tuning (increasing validity) of the best COD testing model we have right now!<br/>Standard model has already increased validity of measuring COD ability by introducing COD deficit point of view. Which, in my opinion is making this model the best we have right now for determining COD ability of an athlete. But let’s see if we can fine tune it… Without increasing complexity of field test execution, of course. Firstly, let’s take a look at both models (standard and improved one), which are designated in Figures 1 and 2. You’ll see that it’s all about mimicking the phases, divided into 5-meter sections. Note: Velocities are not real and are made up for better understanding.<br/>If we really want to ISOLATE COD ABILITY from noise (e. g. linear sprint), the execution of both tests (505 and linear sprint) must have as many common phases (and their distances) as possible except the characteristics which distinguish COD from linear sprint. The main characteristic here is the FINAL DECELERATION. The other, less distinct to sprint, is REACCELERATION. This one is similar to the acceleration, which is involved in the linear sprint test.<br/>Secondly, let’s take a closer look into 5-meter phases (Table 1). At the same time, I’ll try to prove why the improved model is more accurate in determining COD deficit than the standard one. Remember, each of two phases from linear sprint test has to mimic its own phase in 505 test. This way we can minimize the influence of sprint ability in 505 test.<br/>As we see in Table 1 the improved model succeeds in mimicking both phases whereas the standard model’s first phase of 505 mimicking is questionable.<br/>Comparison of linear sprint and 505<br/>Phase 0-5 m (“before” COD)<br/>The first part of this phase is the same in both tests. An athlete needs to accelerate from standing start. The difference comes into play in the second part of this phase where in linear sprint test an athlete continues to accelerate but in 505 test he starts to decelerate. So, everything is the same, except the COD part (deceleration), which is great. In standard model we must connect late acceleration (linear sprint) with early + final deceleration (505) which is at least questionable if not a bit misused and so making that model less accurate.<br/>Phase 5-10 m (“after” COD)<br/>Both models succeed in this phase. In improved model we connect an early acceleration (the same 5-meter sprint time as before) with reacceleration (505) and in standard model we connect the early acceleration of 10-meter sprint (the first 5 meters) with reacceleration. Do early acceleration and reacceleration differ? Yes, but only in COD biomechanics and this is exactly what we want.<br/>I want classical 505 test with 10-meter pre-sprint (3rd model)!<br/>Classical 505 test as presented within Standard model is still one of the most used “COD” tests, so I will briefly touch on using it as a part of COD deficit equation. Mladen made an important note, that first 10 meters of such 505 test should be timed. It’s because athletes are “pacing” these first 10 meters as they often fear of getting too fast into COD action (doubting their deceleration abilities). So, if the initial sprint is not performed with maximal intent, we can’t say it’ll mimic this phase in linear sprint test and we can’t compare it. Roughly speaking, the first 10-meter time in 505 test should be maximally 102% (and not more) of first 10-meter time in linear sprint test. E.g. athlete with 2,100 sec time in 10-meter linear sprint test should run the first 10 meters in 505 with at least 2,163 sec if not better. If we increase “toleration zone” (from 102% to 105%), we risk to diminish validity of COD deficit result.<br/>For the 3rd model, we must perform 2 tests. Remember, logic is the same. They are constructed to mimic each other as much as possible (except COD phase) to extract COD ability (via equation written in the next chapter).<br/>15-meter linear sprint test:<br/>We do 1 15-meter linear sprint and time it at 5, 10 and 15 meters.<br/>Total 505 test:<br/>We do the classical 505 test and time it right from the start. Therefore, athletes sprint for 15 meters, change direction for 180° and run back for 5 meters. We need 2 split times and final time. First split time is at 10 meters. We use it to compare it with 10-meter split time from the upper test for preliminary reasons. 102% rule, as written above. Second split time is at 15 meters (COD point) and we need it for comparison with total 15-meter linear sprint time from the upper test. And in the end we need total 505 test time (whole 20 meters (15+5)) to compare it with summation of total (15 meters) and first 5-meter time of linear sprint test (15+5=20).<br/>As you can see in Figure 3, I have used 15-meter linear sprint to compare it with the first phase of 505 test (before COD). In the second phase (after COD) we compare the first 5 meters of the same 15-meter linear sprint test with the second phase of 505 test (reacceleration).<br/>Simple instructions & equation of improved model and 3rd model<br/>As much this model seems a bit complicated in theory, it’s very simplistic in its nature and EASY TO USE on field. All you need to do is 1 5-meter sprint and a modified 505 test as designated in Figure 2. Remember the COD deficit equation from above? Improved model’s equation is almost the same, just remember to duplicate 5-meter sprint time in order to get the same distance as in 505 test.<br/>COD deficit = modified 505 time – (2 x 5-meter linear sprint time)<br/>If you want to use the 3rd model, use the whole 505 time (20 meters (15+5)) and instead of subtracting 2 x 5-meter time, subtract 15-meter time plus first 5-meter time of the same linear sprint test.<br/>COD deficit = 20-meter 505 time – (15 + 5-meter linear sprint time)<br/>Conclusion and explaining the title (Is COD deficit really a deficit?)<br/>About the purpose of this article: All of these “COD” tests are equally complicated to conduct so why not use the best one? With “best” I mean the one that adds more validity and hence more accurately targets athletes with possible COD issues.<br/>Is COD deficit really a deficit? With improved model, maybe we’ll find out that deficit is not really a deficit as our athletes may be faster in 505 test that in duplicated 5-meter test. And maybe we won’t. So, it’s up to you, fellow S&C coaches to test it and report your findings.<br/>Again, currently this is just a theoretical approach appearing to be near the best on field solution we could have for measuring general COD ability. Toisessa tapauksessa, kun asiakkaat haluavat kulkea paikallisen intranetin kautta päästäkseen palvelimeen eikä haittaohjelmien häirintä ole mahdollista, asiakkaat voivat valita Self-signed Sertifikaatit.<br/>Itse allekirjoitetun sertifikaatin haitat:<br/>Alla on joitain haittoja, jotka voisivat estää sinua ajattelemasta itse allekirjoitettua varmennetta, ja saatat päätyä kolmannen osapuolen SSL-varmenteeseen.<br/>- Verkkosivuilla on vähemmän liiketoimintavolyymiä, koska asiakkaat välttävät vierailemasta verkkosivuillasi edelleen.<br/>- Hyökkääjät voivat hyödyntää tällaista haavoittuvaa verkkosivustoa ja tunkeutua tällaiseen epävarmaan verkkosivustoon saadakseen etuja. OTA: n 2018-raportin mukaan verkkotapahtumat kaksinkertaistuivat vuonna 2017.<br/>- Yrityksillä on pienin verkkouskottavuus ja ne voivat menettää luottamuksensa tuleviin kävijöihinsä ja asiakkaisiinsa. Brändillä on korjaamattomia menetyksiä pitkällä aikavälillä.<br/>- Tällainen sertifikaatti on kuitenkin saatavilla edulliseen hintaan, mutta se muuttuu kalliimmaksi, kun hyökkääjät varastavat tietoja verkkosivustoltasi.<br/>- Varmenteenhallintaa on myös vaikea valvoa kuten sen uusimista ja vanhenemista. PKI:tä (Public Key Infrastructure) ei ole, joten verkossa ei ole peruutuslistapäivitystä.<br/>- Itse allekirjoitetuilla varmenteilla ei ole takuusummaa, jonka varmentavat varmenteen myöntäjät antavat, jos todistusta ei anneta.<br/>Itse allekirjoitetun varmenteen tarkistaminen:<br/>Voit tarkistaa selaimessa klikkaamalla punaista ristiä tai käyttää SSL-tarkistajatyökalua, joka paljastaa varmenteen yksityiskohdat. Esimerkiksi Chromessa voit painaa F12 >> Klikkaa nähdäksesi Varmenteen. Firefoxissa voit napsauttaa riippulukkoa selaimessa ja tarkistaa varmenteen tilan. Jopa useimmat selaimet näyttävät sivulla varoituksia vaarallisista verkkosivustoista.<br/>Kolmannen osapuolen SSL-sertifikaatit:<br/>Jotta asiakkaat olisivat huolissaan itseallekirjoitetuista varmennevaroituksista, verkkosivuston omistajan tulisi ajatella kolmannen osapuolen SSL-varmenteita. Varmenteita on useita luokkia, kuten verkkotunnuksen validointi, organisaation validointi ja laajennettu varmenne.<br/>- Domain Validation Certificate: Domain Validation Certificate on varmenteen ensisijainen taso, joka myönnetään yhdelle tai useammalle verkkotunnukselle. Se ei vaadi muita liiketoimintaan liittyviä todistuksia tai todennusta yrityksen aitoudesta.<br/>- Organisaation validointi: Organisaation validointi on varmenteen toinen taso, jossa yrityksen aitous on todennettava voimassa olevalla laillisella liiketoimintatodistuksella verkkotunnuksen validoinnin ohella.<br/>- Extended Validation: Extended Validation on korkein todennustaso, ja se voidaan myöntää vain organisaatioille. Se auttaa lisäämään liiketoiminnan kuvaa verkossa, kun se saa vihreän osoiterivin, joka näyttää yrityksen nimen maakoodilla. Vierailijat, kun he näkevät vihreän palkin, he saavat luottamuksen verkkosivuston legitimiteettiin.<br/>Kasvavan kilpailun ja kybervälikohtausten myötä verkkokauppayritykset, pankki-, rahoitus- ja muut verkkoyritykset joutuvat etsimään laillista SSL-sertifikaattia itseallekirjoitetun sertifikaatin sijaan. On olemassa HSTS-ominaisuus, jonka jokaisen verkkosivuston tulisi suosia suojattua yhteyttä, jossa se ilmoittaa, että palvelimet saavat käsitellä selainten kautta vain HTTPS: n kautta ja siten auttaa torjumaan downgrad-protokollan hyökkäystä ja evästeiden kaappaamista. |

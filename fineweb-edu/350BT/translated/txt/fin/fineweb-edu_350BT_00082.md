| Vitamin B12, also known as cobalamin, is a water-soluble vitamin that plays a crucial role in various bodily functions. It is essential for maintaining the health of nerve cells, producing DNA and RNA, forming red blood cells, and aiding in the metabolism of fats and carbohydrates. Animal Products: The primary dietary sources of vitamin B12 are animal-based foods such as meat, fish, poultry, eggs, and dairy products.<br/>2. Fortified Foods: Some plant-based foods like breakfast cereals, plant-based milk alternatives, and nutritional yeast are fortified with synthetic vitamin B12.<br/>Causes of Vitamin B12 Deficiency:<br/>Several factors can contribute to a deficiency in vitamin B12. Understanding these causes is essential for preventing and addressing deficiencies:<br/>1. Inadequate Dietary Intake: A diet lacking in animal products or fortified foods can lead to B12 deficiency, especially in vegans and vegetarians.<br/>2. Malabsorption Disorders: Certain medical conditions like celiac disease, Crohn’s disease, atrophic gastritis, or pernicious anemia can interfere with the absorption of vitamin B12 in the digestive tract.<br/>3. Age: As people age, their ability to absorb B12 from food decreases, which can result in deficiency, particularly in older adults.<br/>4. Medications: Long-term use of certain medications, such as proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) and metformin, can hinder B12 absorption.<br/>5. These symptoms can be subtle and develop gradually, making it important to recognize them early to prevent complications. Dietary Modifications: Incorporate vitamin B12-rich foods into your diet, such as lean meats, fish, eggs, dairy products, and fortified foods if you’re a vegetarian or vegan.<br/>2.

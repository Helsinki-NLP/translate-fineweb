| About the Apgar Score<br/>The very first test given to your newborn, the Apgar score occurs right after your baby's birth in the delivery or birthing room. The test was designed to quickly evaluate a newborn's physical condition after delivery and to determine any immediate need for extra medical or emergency care.<br/>Although the Apgar score was developed in 1952 by an anesthesiologist named Virginia Apgar, you may have also heard it referred to as an acronym for: Activity, Pulse, Grimace, Appearance, and Respiration.<br/>The Apgar test is usually given to your baby twice: once at 1 minute after birth, and again at 5 minutes after birth. Rarely, if there are concerns about the baby's condition and the first two scores are low, the test may be scored for a third time at 10 minutes after birth.<br/>Five factors are used to evaluate the baby's condition and each factor is scored on a scale of 0 to 2, with 2 being the best score:<br/>- activity and muscle tone<br/>- pulse (heart rate)<br/>- grimace response (medically known as "reflex irritability")<br/>- appearance (skin coloration)<br/>- respiration (breathing rate and effort)<br/>Doctors, midwives, or nurses add these five factors together to calculate the Apgar score. Scores obtainable are between 10 and 0, with 10 being the highest possible score.<br/>&#124;Normal (above 100 beats per minute)<br/>&#124;&#124;Below 100 beats per minute<br/>(rate and effort)<br/>&#124;Normal rate and effort, good cry<br/>&#124;&#124;Slow or irregular breathing, weak cry<br/>&#124;&#124;Absent (no breathing)<br/>&#124;Grimace (responsiveness or "reflex irritability")<br/>&#124;&#124;Pulls away, sneezes, or coughs with stimulation<br/>&#124;&#124;Facial movement only (grimace) with stimulation<br/>&#124;&#124;Absent (no response to stimulation)<br/>&#124;Active, spontaneous movement<br/>&#124;&#124;Arms and legs flexed with little movement<br/>&#124;&#124;No movement, "floppy" tone<br/>&#124;Normal color all over (hands and feet are pink)<br/>&#124;&#124;Normal color (but hands and feet are bluish)<br/>&#124;&#124;Bluish-gray or pale all over<br/>What Apgar Scores Mean<br/>A baby who scores a 7 or above on the test at 1 minute after birth is generally considered in good health. However, a lower score doesn't necessarily mean that your baby is unhealthy or abnormal. But it may mean that your baby simply needs some special immediate care, such as suctioning of the airways or oxygen to help him or her breathe, after which your baby may improve.<br/>At 5 minutes after birth, the Apgar score is recalculated, and if your baby's score hasn't improved to 7 or greater, or there are other concerns, the doctors and nurses may continue any necessary medical care and will closely monitor your baby. Some babies are born with heart or lung conditions or other problems that require extra medical care; others just take a little longer than usual to adjust to life outside the womb. Most newborns with initial Apgar scores of less than 7 will eventually do just fine.<br/>It's important for new parents to keep their baby's Apgar score in perspective. The test was designed to help health care providers assess a newborn's overall physical condition so that they could quickly determine whether the baby needed immediate medical care. It was not designed to predict a baby's long-term health, behavior, intellectual status, or outcome. Few babies score a perfect 10, and perfectly healthy babies sometimes have a lower-than-usual score, especially in the first few minutes after birth.<br/>Keep in mind that a slightly low Apgar score (especially at 1 minute) is normal for some newborns, especially those born after a high-risk pregnancy, cesarean section, or a complicated labor and delivery. Lower Apgar scores are also seen in premature babies, who usually have less muscle tone than full-term newborns and who, in many cases, will require extra monitoring and breathing assistance because of their immature lungs.<br/>If your doctor or midwife is concerned about your baby's score, he or she will let you know and will explain how your baby is doing, what might be causing problems, if any, and what care is being given. | The first pillar of wisdom<br/>For reading & meditation: Proverbs 3:1-18<br/>"Trust in the Lord with all your heart and lean not on your own understanding '" (v.5)<br/>We turn now to consider the first of the seven pillars on which I believe that wisdom is built - trust. The theme of trust is everywhere in Proverbs; it punctuates almost every passage. The word "trust" itself occurs quite often, the frequency varying according to the translation you read (in the King James Version, for example, "trust" appears ten times) and its synonyms, such as "lean," "acknowledge," "depend," are found scattered through the book. According to Rabbi Bar Kappa, the verse before us today is the pivot around which all the essential principles of Judaism revolve. He claims that these words summarize the teaching of the whole Old Testament and give a clear focus to the fact that the wise are those who trust God and follow His directions for living. But what exactly is "trust"? How important is it to daily living? Why do the word and its synonyms occur so many times, not only in Proverbs but in other parts of Scripture as well? The dictionary defines trust as "a firm belief in the reliability, honesty, veracity, justice and strength of a person or thing." Basically "trust" is confidence that what we believe about a person or thing is true. We tend to think of trust as a spiritual quality, but actually it is an essential posture of life for everyone. It would be very difficult to get through a single day without the exercise of trust. All government, all economics, all institutions, all marriages, all relationships between people, are fundamentally governed by trust. | Two weeks ago in this column, when I compared science to seeing in the dark, I was speaking figuratively. But the column sparked a response from a reader who had a more literal reading in mind.<br/>Joel Parker, a scientist at the Southwest Research Institute, collaborates on an instrument called LAMP. LAMP flies aboard the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter, a spacecraft launched in 2009 to pave the way for the return of humans to the moon. That means identifying safe landing sites, understanding the health hazards of the lunar environment (like radiation), and identifying where future lunar astronauts may be able to tap useful resources, like water.<br/>If there is water ice on the moon, it could be locked up in deep craters at the moon's poles. While the Earth leans over (its axis is tilted by about 23.5 degrees), allowing both the north and south poles to get some sunshine, the moon stands up almost perfectly straight, meaning that some regions within polar craters are trapped in darkness forever. Because they are protected from sunlight, these permanently shadowed regions could be reservoirs for water ice.<br/>But the very thing that makes them scientifically exciting -- their darkness -- makes them difficult to observe. So how do you go about searching for something where, by definition, the sun doesn't shine? It requires, as the LAMP team says, "seeing in the dark."<br/>But scientists get a little help from the fact that, even in the absence of direct sunlight, these shadowed craters aren't totally dark: They are bathed in a very faint glow from distant starlight and from sunlight that bounces off the stray particles floating in space. By tuning in to a narrow window of the ultraviolet spectrum, LAMP creates a map of exactly how this ultraviolet radiation bounces off the moon, even in the deep recesses of polar craters. Engraved on this map could be hints of the presence of water ice.<br/>I say "hints" because LAMP cannot answer once and for all whether water exists in the lunar craters: It can only return readings that are "consistent with" the presence of water.<br/>That's why LAMP isn't going it alone. Four more instruments on the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter spacecraft are also searching for evidence of water ice. There's the Orbiter Laser Altimeter, which shines lasers at the lunar surface and measures the brightness of their reflection. (More ice equals a brighter reflection.) A system called Mini-RF will create radar maps of the poles. There's also Diviner, which measures the temperature on and below the moon's surface. Finally, an instrument called the Lunar Exploration Neutron Detector counts neutrons coming off the surface of the moon. Because water absorbs neutrons, a neutron deficit could point the way to water ice.<br/>Which brings us back to the figurative version of "seeing in the dark" that I mentioned last time: None of these instruments can actually "see" water ice directly. Instead, they take measurements that are suggestive of the presence or absence of water ice. Only when these measurements are combined can scientists rule out other possibilities and "rule in" water ice.<br/>Even then, the job isn't straightforward. For if ice is present on the moon -- and so far the evidence from the LRO instruments as well as from other spacecraft suggests that it is -- it probably isn't in the form of big, smooth "skating rinks," says Parker. Instead, it will be combined with, and possibly hidden under, lunar dust called regolith. The intervention – through which the learners were taught behavioral strategies such as relaxation techniques – was conducted as part of each school’s counseling curriculum.<br/>The wider age group who received the intervention found it to be useful, felt glad they had participated and effectively learned the intervention content. In turn, decreases in test anxiety were linked with changes in symptoms of depression and anxiety , such as posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD).

| Cleavage of ascospores<br/><br/>during cleavage of the ascospores have shown that a system of double membranes continuous with the endoplasmic reticulum extends from the envelope of the diploid fusion nucleus.<br/><br/>The double membrane develops to form a cylindrical envelope lining the young ascus.<br/><br/>This peripheral membrane cylinder or lining layer is termed the ascus vesicle or ascospore-delimiting membrane.<br/><br/>The ascospores are cut out from the cytoplasm within the ascus by infolding and fusion of the inner edges of the double membrane around a portion of cytoplasm and a nucleus.<br/><br/>In some ascomycetes, e.g. Taphrina, a peripheral membrane cylinder has not been observed and the nuclei within the ascus become enveloped by ascospore-delimiting membranes formed by direct invagination of discrete parts of the ascus plasma membrane.<br/><br/>Between the two layers of the ascospore delimiting membrane enclosing the ascospores, the primary spore wall is secreted.<br/><br/>The inner membrane forms the plasma membrane of the ascospore and the outer membrane becomes the spore-investing membrane.<br/><br/>Secondary wall material is secreted within the primary wall. There may be several such layers. In Sordaria humana a total of four spore wall layers have been distinguished, a primary wall layer and three secondary layers.<br/><br/>The secondary wall layers are often quite thick, and in dark-walled ascospores the pigment is usually laid down within the secondary wall layers.<br/><br/>The spore wall may be smooth or extended to form a variety of ornamentations such as spines, ridges or reticulations.<br/><br/>The ascus epiplasm, i.e. the residual cytoplasm remaining outside the spores after these have become cleaved out, may continue to play a part in the formation of the ascospore wall.<br/><br/>This secondary wall material ornaments the ascospore wall but is not involved in the formation of the purple pigment characteristic of Ascobolus ascospores.

| Winning<br/><br/>By Jack and Suzy Welch<br/><br/>12:01AM GMT 09 Jan 2006<br/><br/>Jack Welch, retired chairman and CEO of GE, and Suzy Welch, ex editor-in-chief of Harvard Business Review, respond to readers' business issues.<br/><br/>Q Ever since the Czech Republic and other post-communist countries have opened to outside investment, many foreign companies - in particular American and European ones - have mainly sent in their own people to run operations.<br/><br/>The problem is that these managers are usually incompetent and have only one skill, an ability to speak the mother tongue. They add nothing, and end up relying on the innate loyalty and enthusiasm of local employees to get things done. Why are companies so foolish this way?<br/><br/>Peter Pesan, Czech Republic<br/><br/>A They're not foolish - they're just uneasy. Like American tourists who eat at the McDonald's on the Champs-Elysee or French tourists who bring their own wine into Disney World, they'd rather have comfort than authenticity. Now, "preferring the familiar" may not be a principle of great management, but it is certainly part of human behaviour.<br/><br/>And look, importantly, the problem you describe is universal. From the beginning of modern globalisation, companies have tended to "stick with their own kind" when opening foreign operations.<br/><br/>When the Japanese first moved into the US, or anywhere else for that matter, they typically installed Japanese bosses. And the same pattern is true of Germans, Britons and many others. They all want their own, trusted people in charge of far-flung operations, especially at the beginning, when so much is unknown about the local environment.<br/><br/>The key phrase here is "at the beginning" -- because trouble of the type you describe begins when foreign companies stay in comfort mode and keep their own people in charge for longer than a few years. This tactic misses a real opportunity. Why?<br/><br/>Because local people always know their own country better; they know how its government works and how its people think. They know which local universities produce the best minds; they can understand what people in bars and on the factory floor are really saying about the country's political and economic future. They can always "work the system" with more insight and ease.<br/><br/>Good companies know that the sooner you put local executives in charge of foreign operations the better. And the best companies work hard from the day they arrive to find and develop local talent with training programmes, creating a pipeline of middle managers with a shot at the top.<br/><br/>Those global companies that don't move quickly enough to turn management over to local executives eventually suffer from real brain drain, as smarter companies move in and steal the local talent for their own expanding operations.<br/><br/>In thriving Eastern Europe, as in Asia, the competition for professional management is fierce. No foreign company can afford to keep local employees in lower-level positions. The local talent will leave for companies that offer growth and a future, taking their knowledge with them.<br/><br/>So while we understand your frustration with the "foolish" companies in your region who continue to value comfort over a leap into the unfamiliar, don't worry too much. This problem is typically self-correcting. In time, good companies put local managers in charge. They have to.<br/><br/>Q Over the years, I have noticed that people with an MBA, irrespective of the providing institute and with less experience and less salubrious CVs than myself, land bigger jobs, especially in the top-level executive and leadership positions. This puzzles me. What is it in an MBA that qualifies a graduate to be an automatic winner?<br/><br/>Jitesh Jairam, Durban, South Africa<br/><br/>A There is no question that an MBA puts a stamp of approval on your forehead. People like credentials, and any MBA separates its "owner" from the pack. A puzzling phenomenon to you, maybe, yet nothing new.<br/><br/>But the impact of an MBA is really only good for a year or so. It helps a graduate get a higher starting salary or land a better job out of the gate, or both.<br/><br/>And it creates something of a little halo effect. MBAs are presumed to be intelligent and capable; their bosses watch them hopefully, and in many cases, give them extra opportunities to contribute and grow.<br/><br/>In short order, however, an MBA's performance kicks in, and in the vast majority of organisations, that is all that comes to matter. The MBA excels or sinks - and the advanced degree becomes forgotten either way. In business, your only real credential, ultimately, is results.<br/><br/>So when you see those MBAs around you who appear to be "automatic winners," look beyond their diplomas. We would bet they are doing a lot more than carrying around a piece of paper.<br/><br/>Most likely, they are over-delivering on performance and buying into the values of the company, demonstrating them with their every day behaviours. On top of that, they are probably exuding a positive, can-do attitude, taking on tough challenges with a healthy mixture of optimism and realism.<br/><br/>Finally, they are almost certainly making the people around them look good too, by sharing credit and building the team. In other words, we would bet they're moving the company forward in some meaningful way or another. They're making a difference.<br/><br/>Don't get us wrong. We are definitely not discouraging anyone from getting an MBA. It is a credential with clout - it does help critical thinking and introduce you to important managerial concepts best taught in a classroom. It does get you a better starting salary and more early visibility. But an MBA's leg-up only lasts for a finite period, after which performance takes precedence.<br/><br/>So look again. The "automatic winners" you see around you probably aren't automatic at all. They are successful not just because of a credential they received on graduation day, but because of their performance ever since.<br/><br/>• Jack and Suzy Welch are the authors of the international best-seller Winning, published by HarperCollins<br/><br/>You can email them your questions at winning@nytimes.com.

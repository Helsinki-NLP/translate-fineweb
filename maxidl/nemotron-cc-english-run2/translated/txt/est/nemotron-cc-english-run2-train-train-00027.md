| The Hueco Bolson is a thick pocket of sediments derived from nearby mountains<br/>that extends from New Mexico, through Texas, and into Mexico in the El Paso and<br/>Ciudad Juarez area. Over time, these sediments filled with water and became the Hueco<br/>Bolson aquifer: an oasis of plentiful water in the northern part of the Chihuahuan<br/>Desert. El Paso and Ciudad Juarez have relied on the Hueco Bolson aquifer as a primary source of drinking water for several decades (Sayre and Penn, 1945; White and others,<br/>1997). Ciudad Juarez, several communities in New Mexico, and the Fort Bliss Military Reservation currently depend on the Hueco Bolson aquifer as their sole source of drinking water (Sheng and others, 2001). Because of the desert climate and the<br/>local geology, the aquifer is not easily replenished, and recharge is low. Low<br/>recharge and high pumping rates have caused large water-level declines and large decreases in<br/>fresh-water volumes in the aquifer.<br/><br/>The aquifer and the El Paso-Ciudad Juarez area are at the crossroads. With<br/>current trends, groundwater models predict that El Paso will pump the last of its fresh water by<br/>2025, and Ciudad Juarez will pump the last of its fresh water by 2005 (Sheng and<br/>others, 2001). The El Paso Water Utilities/Public Service Board (EPWU) has recognized the<br/>nature of limited groundwater resources in the area and has investigated and invested in<br/>several strategies to increase the longevity and usefulness of the aquifer. The Hueco Bolson aquifer consists of unconsolidated to slightly consolidated deposits composed of fine-<br/>to medium-grained sand with interbedded lenses of clay, silt, gravel, and caliche.<br/>Sediments in the bolson are fluvial, evaporitic, alluvial fan, and aeolian in origin and<br/>have a maximum thickness of 9,000 ft (Mattick, 1967; Cliett, 1969; Abeyta and Thomas,<br/>1996). The bottom part of the Hueco Bolson is primarily clay and silt. Therefore, only<br/>the top several hundred feet produce good-quality water.<br/><br/>Recharge<br/><br/>The Hueco Bolson aquifer is recharged by mountain-front recharge; seepage from<br/>the Rio Grande, canals, and agricultural drains; and deep-well injection (Knorr and<br/>Cliett, 1985;<br/><br/>Land and Armstrong, 1985; White and others, 1997). Mountain-front<br/>recharge is the<br/><br/>seepage of surface run-off after rainfalls into the aquifer where the bolson<br/>laps up against<br/><br/>bordering mountains. Before the aquifer was heavily pumped, water in the aquifer naturally discharged to the Rio Grande. After pumping caused water levels to<br/>decline, the Rio Grande began to lose water into the aquifer, so much so that a part of the<br/>river through El Paso-Ciudad Juarez has been lined with concrete to minimize leakage. Unlined irrigation canals and drains also leak water into the aquifer, although<br/>the water is usually of poor quality. Water quality has been affected by the large<br/>waterlevel declines in the aquifer, which have induced flow of poor-quality water into<br/>areas of fresh water. Water quality in the shallow part of the aquifer along the Rio<br/>Grande in the alluvium has degraded because of leakage of poor-quality irrigation return-flow<br/>into the aquifer (Sheng and others, 2001). Water quality beneath Ciudad Juarez is<br/>generally less than 1,000 mg/L TDS (Hibbs and others, 1997), however, water-quality<br/>deterioration has been observed in wells along the border and in the downtown area.<br/><br/>Water Levels and Groundwater Flow<br/><br/>Depth to water in the Hueco Bolson aquifer ranges from very shallow to very<br/>deep. Depth to groundwater near the Cities of Tularosa and Alamogordo is between 20<br/>and 150 ft, whereas depth to water below El Paso ranges from 250 to 400 ft in depth, and<br/>depth to water below Ciudad Juarez ranges between 100 and 250 ft (Hibbs and others,<br/>1997). Depth to water below the Rio Grande is less than 70 ft. Groundwater flows from<br/>the Tularosa Basin southward into the Hueco Bolson and into Texas (Hibbs and others,<br/>1997, their fig. 3.8). Little drawdown has been recorded in the northern part of the<br/>aquifer. The drawdown in Hueco Bolson along the Texas-New Mexico border has been relatively small, not exceeding 30 ft (Hibbs and other 1997). In heavily developed parts of<br/>the Hueco Bolson aquifer, drawdowns since predevelopment in 1903 are up to 170 ft.<br/>Focal points of drawdown are beneath the City of El Paso and Ciudad Juarez (Hibbs and<br/>others, 1997).<br/><br/>The model by Heywood and Yager (in review) suggests that about 6,000 acre-ft/yr<br/>of groundwater flowed in the Hueco Bolson aquifer from New Mexico into Texas before large-scale pumping by El Paso in the 1960's. Since then, the amount of flow has increased to about 18,000 acre-ft/yr. In the El Paso-Ciudad Juarez area,<br/>groundwater flows toward cones of depression. Between 1910 and 1960, groundwater flowed from Mexico into Texas toward pumping centers in El Paso (Sheng and others, 2001).<br/>Since 1960, groundwater, generally of poor quality, has flowed from Texas into Mexico<br/>(Sheng and others, 2001).<br/><br/>Groundwater Models<br/><br/>Several groundwater flow models have been constructed for the Hueco Bolson<br/>aquifer system. These models include an early electric-analog model of the El Paso area<br/>(Leggat and Davis, 1966) and three numerical models developed by the U.S. Geological<br/>Survey, including (1) Meyer and Gordon (1973) and Meyer (1975, 1976) (later updated by Knowles and Alvarez, 1979), (2) Groschen (1994), and (3) an as yet unpublished<br/>model (Heywood and Yager, in review). Mullican and Senger (1990, 1992) developed a<br/>model of the southeastern part of the Hueco Bolson. Mexico has also developed a<br/>groundwater flow model for part of the area. Wilson and others (1986) used a preexisting<br/>model to predict water resources through 2060.<br/><br/>Models by Groschen (1994) and Heywood and Yager (in review) simulate potential water-level declines, as well as changes in water quality due to pumping.<br/>Groschen (1994) showed that water quality in the bolson is most likely affected by<br/>horizontal movement of saline water in response to pumping.<br/><br/>The integrated flow and water-quality model by Heywood and Yager (in review) represents the cooperation of EPWU, the USGS, the International Boundary and<br/>Water Commission (IBWC), Fort Bliss Military Reservation, JMAS (Junta Municipal de<br/>Agua y Saneamiento de Ciudad Juarez), and CILA (Comision Internacional de Limites y Aguas). Binational coordination has included the exchange of aquifer information<br/>and comparison of water-resource management plans. The model is being used to assess<br/>(1) water storage in the aquifer, (2) the optimization of pumping for fresh and<br/>brackish water, (3) the location of new production wells, (4) the control of brackish-water<br/>intrusion, (5) the design of an aquifer storage and recovery program, and (6) the planning of<br/>water resources among Texas, New Mexico, and Mexico (Sheng and others, 2001). Groundwater Availability<br/><br/>Groundwater availability represents the amount of water that can be used from an<br/>aquifer. Groundwater availability can be defined in many different ways depending on the<br/>local socioeconomic needs (Mace and others, 2001). In the El Paso area, groundwater availability has been defined using a systematic depletion approach, where the<br/>total amount of recoverable water is considered the amount of water available for use.<br/>In general, groundwater availability is assessed for the fresh-water part of the<br/>aquifer. However, as water resources become scarcer in the state, more and more areas,<br/>including El Paso, are also evaluating the usable amounts of slightly saline water for<br/>ongoing or potential desalination projects.<br/><br/>Fresh Water<br/><br/>The approximate volume of recoverable freshwater in the entire Hueco Bolson<br/>aquifer is about 7.5 million acre-ft, with 3 million acre-ft in Texas, 3.9 million acre-ft<br/>in New Mexico, and 600,000 acre-ft in Mexico (Sheng and others, 2001, on the basis of a<br/>review of USGS publications). The Far West Texas Planning Group estimated that there<br/>were about 3 million acre-ft of fresh water in the Hueco Bolson and 2.5 million<br/>acre-ft of slightly saline water for desalination (FWTPG, 2001). Recoverable fresh water<br/>accounts for economic and geologic constraints and does not represent all of the fresh<br/>water in the aquifer.<br/><br/>Other studies have suggested differing volumes of fresh water. Knowles and<br/>Kennedy (1956) estimated that the Hueco Bolson in Texas had about 7.4 million acre-ft of recoverable water, with less than 250 mg/L chloride (~750 mg/L TDS). Meyer<br/>(1976) estimated the recoverable amount of fresh water in the Texas part of the Hueco<br/>Bolson to hold 10.64 million acre-ft. White (1987) estimated that the Hueco Bolson aquifer<br/>in Texas holds about 9.95 million acre-ft of recoverable fresh water. The TWDB<br/>(1997)<br/><br/>estimated that there was about 9 million acre-feet of fresh water in the<br/>Texas part of the that there was about 9 million acre-feet of fresh water in the Texas part of the<br/>Hueco Bolson.<br/><br/>Slightly Saline Water<br/><br/>Slightly saline water may be a large potential water resource in the El Paso<br/>area. There is an estimated 20 million acre-ft of slightly saline water (TDS between 1,000 and<br/>3,000 mg/L) in the Hueco Bolson aquifer in El Paso County (Sheng and others, 2001).<br/>Similar volumes of slightly saline water may also exist in New Mexico and Mexico (Sheng<br/>and others, 2001). Sheng and others (2001) recommended additional studies to<br/>quantify a more exact volume of poor-quality water in the aquifer.<br/><br/>Strategies to Increase Groundwater Availability<br/><br/>Although recent modeling work suggests that the Hueco Bolson in the El Paso area<br/>will run out of fresh water by 2025, it is not a forgone conclusion. For prediction<br/>purposes, the model assumes that current trends and practices will remain the same.<br/>However, the life of the fresh groundwater resource can be extended by implementing<br/>strategies to increase the availability of groundwater.<br/><br/>Increase Surface-Water Use<br/><br/>By increasing the use of surface water, groundwater use can be minimized, thus extending the useful life of the fresh-water part of the aquifer. In this case,<br/>surface water is relied upon when plentiful, and groundwater is relied upon when surface water<br/>is not plentiful. Regional water providers are pursuing this strategy by the<br/>implementation of the Regional Sustainable Water Project (IBWC and EPWU, 2000). The Far West Texas Planning Group also identifies the pursuit of additional surface-water supplies<br/>as a recommended water management strategy for the area (FWTPG, 2001). However, the planning group noted that El Paso cannot rely on the Rio Grande for water during<br/>times of severe drought (FWTPG, 2001).<br/><br/>Hydraulic Control and Desalination<br/><br/>To reduce the degradation of groundwater quality due to laterally flowing poorer<br/>quality water, wells can be installed to hydraulically control the migration of poorer<br/>quality water by capturing the poorer quality water before it mixes with fresher water. The<br/>produced water can then be desalinated. EPWU and the Department of Defense at the Fort<br/>Bliss Military Reservation are investigating this approach in existing wells in the Airport/Montana well field (Sheng and others, 2001). To maximize the water<br/>supply, the desalinated water (~200 mg/L TDS) can then be blended with slightly saline water (~1,500 mg/L TDS) to produce a water with a TDS of about 900 mg/L TDS. Hydraulic control and desalination extend the life of the fresh-water part of the aquifer<br/>by protecting existing fresh-water resources from further intrusions of poor-quality water and decreasing the reliance on the fresh-water part of the aquifer. Hydraulic<br/>control and<br/><br/>desalination are also being considered in other El Paso wellfields (Sheng<br/>and others, 2001).<br/><br/>Pumping Optimization<br/><br/>Pumping of wells can be optimized to minimize the migration of poor-quality<br/>water and the depth of cones of depression around pumping centers. Pumping of water-supply<br/>wells should be optimized aquiferwide to minimize the effects of pumping on the<br/>migration of poor-quality water into areas of fresh water. An operational priority list for<br/>the Hueco well fields has been developed and used in well-field operation for over a year<br/>(Sheng and others, 2001). Results of the optimization program will be evaluated to<br/>further improve operation of the well fields. Pumping optimization extends the life of<br/>the freshwater resource by minimizing the impacts of poor-quality water intrusions.<br/><br/>Aquifer Storage and Recovery<br/><br/>Aquifer Storage and Recovery (ASR) is when treated surface water is injected<br/>into an aquifer when it is plentiful and demand is low, and then recovered the stored<br/>water from the aquifer when demand is high or during times of drought. ASR extends the life<br/>of the aquifer by maximizing the use of surface water and recharging the aquifer. In<br/>addition, it will also prevent brackish water intrusion if injection wells are located along<br/>the transition zone of marginal quality groundwater.<br/><br/>Blending high-grade water with poor-quality water<br/><br/>Using the best quality water first has often been the preferred method of<br/>groundwater production. However, by blending good quality water with poorer quality water up<br/>to the Safe Drinking Water Act standards for TDS, chloride, and sulfate secondary<br/>maximum contamination levels, water providers can enhance their production capacity. The blending method extends the life of the aquifer by maximizing the use of the<br/>freshwater resource. When combined with hydraulic control, existing freshwater resources<br/>can also be additionally protected.<br/><br/>Conclusions<br/><br/>The Hueco Bolson aquifer and the El Paso-Ciudad Juarez area are at the<br/>crossroads. Several scientific studies and recent modeling projects suggest that, under<br/>current trends, fresh water from the Hueco Bolson aquifer in Texas will be depleted by 2025.<br/>However, using groundwater more strategically can extend the longevity of fresh-water<br/>resources in the aquifer. EPWU and FWTPG are actively researching and implementing a number<br/>of strategies to do just this, including increased surface-water use, hydraulic<br/>control and desalination, pumping optimization, aquifer storage and recovery, and blending<br/>to increase freshwater supplies. The area will need to continue to follow this path<br/>to ensure that future water needs of El Paso are met.<br/><br/>Acknowledgments<br/><br/>Authors would like to thank Mr. Edmund G. Pumbamise optimeerimine pikendab mageveeressursi eluiga, minimeerides halva kvaliteediga vee sissetungi mõju.<br/><br/>Akviferi ladustamine ja taastamine<br/><br/>Akvifer Storage and Recovery (ASR) on see, kui töödeldud pinnavesi süstitakse põhjaveekihti, kui see on külluslik ja nõudlus on väike, ning seejärel kogutakse salvestatud vesi põhjaveekihist, kui nõudlus on suur või põua ajal. ASR pikendab põhjaveekihi eluiga, maksimeerides pinnavee kasutamist ja täites põhjaveekihti. Lisaks takistab see ka murrangulist vee sissetungimist, kui sisselaskeavad asuvad mööda marginaalse kvaliteediga põhjavee üleminekutsooni.<br/><br/>Kõrgekvaliteedilise vee segamine halva kvaliteediga veega<br/><br/>Parima kvaliteediga vee kasutamine on sageli olnud eelistatud põhjavee tootmise meetod.

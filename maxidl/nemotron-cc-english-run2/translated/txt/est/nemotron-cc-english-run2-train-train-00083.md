The<br/>sheer detail and attention that Plato has lavished on attempting to understand<br/>the workings of the human body and his reflections on its proper function (44d–47e;<br/>64a–69a; 69d–76e; 80d–89d) suggest that giving an account of the cosmos was his<br/>primary interest in the Timaeus.<br/><br/>And what of humans? The embodied human, a<br/>rational being that is mortal, while less perfect than the cosmos itself,[10]<br/>nevertheless is necessary for the completion and perfection of the cosmos<br/>(41b7–d3).[11]<br/>The implantation of the human souls in bodies is thus considered to be "a<br/>necessity" (42a3). Human embodiment is part of the perfection of the cosmos. In<br/>the Timaeus, embodiment is not a<br/>punishment for human failure, but essential to what humans were created to be.[12]<br/>"[T]he preincarnate state is shown not as a condition desirable in its own<br/>right but as an interlude entirely occupied with preparation for life in the body.[13]<br/>. . . The incarnate soul<br/>needs to maintain its embodied self and for this the appetite—the desire for<br/>food and drink and rest and sex—is required. But appetites can get out of hand<br/>and tend not to find their own healthy limits, so the spirited part of soul<br/>develops to keep them in check.[16]<br/>In a healthy person appetite is in submission to spirit, which is in submission<br/>to intellect.[17] (But the<br/>soul is intended by the divine Craftsman to be embodied, and hence to be<br/>tripartite.)<br/><br/>The immortal soul is created directly by the divine Craftsman, while the<br/>body (and hence the mortal soul) are made by created celestial gods, at the command<br/>of the Craftsman (Timaeus 41a-d).[19]<br/>These gods, we are told "built on" (prosôikondomoun)<br/>and "composed" (sunethesan) another,<br/>mortal form of soul by mixing the affections (69c9, d6). The affections in<br/>question are pleasure, pains, daring and fear, passion, hope, perception, and<br/>desire, all of which can create trouble for the soul and all of which arise of<br/>necessity from embodiment. Embodiment thus gives rise to the lesser, mortal soul.[20]<br/>The single human creature is thus a paradoxical and conflicted being, an<br/>immortal soul living a mortal life.<br/><br/>The ancillary gods, by careful and rational<br/>design, linked the different parts of the soul with different parts of the<br/>body. The head had been created to house the immortal soul. They created a<br/>buffer zone (the neck) to keep this away from the mortal soul in the torso. The<br/>organization of the body mirrored the ideal organization of the soul—the<br/>spirited part of the soul is intended to mediate the intellect's wisdom to the<br/>appetite's desires. Kuid isu võib minna käest ära ja ei leia oma tervislikke piire, nii et hinge vaimne osa areneb, et neid kontrollida.<br/>Terve inimese isu on alistumine vaimule, mis on allumine intellektile.[17] (Aga hing on jumaliku käsitöölise poolt ette nähtud kehastuma ja seega olema kolmepoolne.)<br/><br/>Surematu hinge loob otse jumalik käsitööline, samas kui keha (ja seega ka surelik hing) on loodud taevaste jumalate poolt käsitöölise (Timaeus 41a-d) käsul.<br/>Need jumalad, meile öeldakse "ehitatud" (prosôikondomoun)<br/>ja "koostatud" (sunethesan) teise sureliku hinge vormi, segades tundeid (69c9, d6). Surelik hing, kehastumisest tulenevad osad, ei ole lihtsalt piiratud, vaid annavad tegelikult positiivse panuse inimelu heaks.

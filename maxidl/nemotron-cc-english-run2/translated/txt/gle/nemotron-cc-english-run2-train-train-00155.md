| Primate<br/><br/>PRIMATE (from Low Lat. primas = one who held the first place, primas partes). During the 4th and 5th centuries A.D. the title was<br/>applied to both secular and ecclesiastical officials. The Theodosian Code mentions primates of towns, districts and fortified places (Primates urbium, vicorum,<br/>castellorum) . The Pragmatic Sanction of Justinian also mentions primates governing a district, primates regionis; and in this sense the title survived, under<br/>Turkish rule, in Greece until the 1pth century. An official called " primate of the palace " is mentioned in the laws of the Visigoths. Primas also seems to<br/>have been used loosely during the middle ages for " head " or " chief." Du Cange cites primas castri. The title, however, has been more generally used to denote<br/>a bishop with special privileges and powers. It was first employed almost synonymously with metropolitan to denote the chief bishop of a province having his see<br/>in the capital and certain rights of superintendence over the whole province. At the Council of Nicaea (A.D. 325) the metropolitan constitution was assumed as<br/>universal, and after this the terms " metropolitan," and " primate," to denote the chief bishop of a province, came into general use. The title of primate was<br/>used more generally in Africa, while elsewhere metropolitan was more generally employed. The primates in Africa differed from those elsewhere in that the title<br/>always belonged to the longest ordained bishop in a province, who had not necessarily his see in the capital, except in the case of the bishop of Carthage, who<br/>was head also of the other five African provinces. There were also three sorts of honorary primates: (i) primates aevo, the oldest bishop in a province next to<br/>the primate, on whom power devolved when the primate was disabled or disqualified; (2) titular metropolitans, the bishops of certain cities which had the name<br/>and title of civil metropoles bestowed on them by some emperor; (3) the bishops of some mother-churches which were honoured by ancient custom but were subject<br/>to the ordinary metropolitan, e.g. the bishop of Jerusalem, who was subject to his metropolitan at Caesarea.<br/><br/>At a later date " primate " became the official title of certain metropolitans who obtained from the pope a position of episcopal authority over several<br/>other metropolitans and who were, at the same time, appointed vicars of the Holy See. This was done in the case of the bishops of Aries and Thessalonica as<br/>early as the 5th century.

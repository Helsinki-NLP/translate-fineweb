| Month: December 2016<br/><br/>This is a picture of a bicycle frame and a bicycle tire both of which are securely chained to the stand. Unfortunately, all the rest of the bicycle is gone. You are standing in line to get to the cashier and someone cuts in line. This is a "threat" to your view of right and wrong. In other words, it isn't right and it isn't fair. You get annoyed (mild anger) or even angry, gauge your response to the situation and assertively tell the person that you (and others) have been waiting in line and that they need to go to the back of the line.<br/><br/>When we get angry, it means we have perceived a threat to our egos, our goals, our values, our relationship, or our resources from our point of view. This is what anger, as a threat detector, is designed to do.<br/><br/>You expect the line-cutter to apologize and exit the line. This is what they "should" do.<br/><br/>Instead, they glare at you and tell you to mind your own business.<br/><br/>What wentwrong?<br/><br/>In short, the person with whom you are interacting had not read the social "script" which states what a reasonable person should do in this situation. Their anger tells you that they see you as a threat and that they are attempting to minimize the damage they believe you have caused them.<br/><br/>To put it another way, you viewed the "threat" from your point of view and acted accordingly. Valid means that your feelings are consistent with how you view the world.<br/><br/>Your feelings may be valid for you but wrong for the situation.<br/><br/>Or, in this case, his feelings may be valid for him but wrong (according to social norms) for the situation.<br/><br/>You, however, have experienced a valid feeling (anger), your emotion is right for the situation, and you took the right action to nullify the threat.

|  MALI HUMAN RIGHTS PRACTICES, 1993<br/>DATE: JANUARY 31, 1994<br/>AUTHOR: U.S. DEPARTMENT OF STATE<br/>MALI<br/>Mali has a constitutional Government headed by President Alpha<br/>Konare. After the overthrow of the Moussa Traore regime in<br/>March 1991, the country went through a 14-month transition<br/>period during which Mali held its first democratic national<br/>elections. In a series of six direct elections between January<br/>and April 1992, Malians ratified a new Constitution, elected<br/>municipal councilors, National Assembly deputies, and, finally,<br/>a President. Twenty-one political parties nationwide<br/>participated in elections, judged by international observers to<br/>be free and fair.<br/>There was a gradual normalization of the security situation<br/>following the signing of the National Pact in April 1992, a<br/>major truce agreement, which ended the insurgency in the north<br/>involving varied Tuareg and Maur groups, ranging from<br/>politically motivated persons seeking an autonomous Tuareg<br/>state to those engaged in simple banditry. The improved<br/>security situation relieved some domestic pressure but allowed<br/>other problems to come to the fore. Violent student<br/>demonstrations over government efforts to restrict scholarships<br/>in April forced the resignation of the first government of the<br/>Third Republic. The President, in a bid to develop a<br/>consensual approach to the nation's problems, incorporated<br/>several former opposition political parties into the second<br/>Government, headed by Prime Minister Abdoulaye Sekou Sow.<br/>Mali's military and security forces number some 7,000 and are<br/>divided between the Army, Air Force, Gendarmerie, the<br/>Republican Guard, and the police. The Army and Air Force are<br/>under the control of a civilian Minister of Defense. The<br/>Gendarmerie and Republican Guard are also part of the defense<br/>structure but are assigned, for operational purposes, along<br/>with the police, to the Ministry of Territorial<br/>Administration. This was done in an attempt to provide a more<br/>concerted and better organized force in the face of rising<br/>civil disturbances. With the end of the Tuareg rebellion,<br/>there were few reports of abuses by the military and security<br/>forces, who remain supportive of the young democracy.<br/>Mali continues to be beset by a myriad of economic problems<br/>including a sagging economy, a national debt roughly equal to<br/>its gross domestic product, dependence on international donors,<br/>inadequate government revenues, demands from a number of vocal<br/>special interest groups, and a literacy rate of only 23<br/>percent. Its annual per capita gross national product is<br/>approximately $300. Mali's economy is based primarily on<br/>subsistence farming and animal husbandry, making it highly<br/>dependent on adequate rainfall for its economic well-being.<br/>The Government, at the senior levels, continues to be committed<br/>to implementing reforms aimed at modernizing the economy<br/>through fiscal and regulatory reforms.<br/>The Government respected constitutional provisions on freedom<br/>of speech, press, assembly, association, and religion, and,<br/>with the signing of the National Pact and the end of the Tuareg<br/>rebellion, there were no new extrajudicial killings committed<br/>by the security forces. Nevertheless, there were human rights<br/>abuses, notably in the Government's inability to process the<br/>large backlog of judicial cases in accordance with the law.<br/>This has resulted in persons languishing in prison for several<br/>years awaiting trial. Social and cultural factors continued to<br/>sharply limit economic and educational opportunities for most<br/>women.<br/>RESPECT FOR HUMAN RIGHTS<br/>Section 1 Respect for the Integrity of the Person, Including<br/>Freedom from:<br/>a. Political and Other Extrajudicial Killing<br/>There were no reports of targeted political killings and no<br/>credible reports of extrajudicial killings on the part of<br/>security forces. The signing of the National Pact in 1992 with<br/>the Tuareg rebels, the formation of mixed patrols, and the<br/>integration of 600 former Tuareg rebels into the armed forces<br/>in the north brought an end to most organized insurgency in<br/>region. However, the Commission of Inquiry that was to have<br/>been established to investigate all deaths on both sides of the<br/>conflict had not been created by year's end.<br/>b. Disappearance<br/>There have been no incidents of abduction or disappearances<br/>attributable to government or opposition forces.<br/>c. Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman, or Degrading<br/>Treatment or Punishment<br/>The Constitution states that no one will be subjected to<br/>torture and cruel, inhuman, degrading, or humiliating<br/>treatment. There were no known reports, including in the<br/>media, of police or military beating of detainees.<br/>Prison conditions are still characterized by overcrowding,<br/>inadequate medical facilities, and limited food supplies. The<br/>Government is seeking foreign assistance to help improve the<br/>situation, and several associations are working with women and<br/>juvenile prisoners to improve their conditions. Juvenile<br/>offenders are generally held in the same prison as adult<br/>offenders. Women are housed in the same prison as men but live<br/>in a separate compound. During past prison riots, male inmates<br/>have attacked and raped female inmates.<br/>d. Arbitrary Arrest, Detention, or Exile<br/>The Constitution has expanded the rights of arrested persons.<br/>Arrested persons must be charged or released within 48 hours,<br/>and they are entitled to counsel. In practice, detainees are<br/>not always charged within the 48-hour period, and<br/>administrative backlogs as well as insufficient numbers of<br/>lawyers, judges, and courts often cause lengthy delays in<br/>bringing people to trial. In extreme cases, individuals can<br/>languish several years in prison before coming to trial.<br/>The law does not provide for release on bail, but detainees are<br/>sometimes released on their own recognizance. In October the<br/>court released on their own recognizance six former government<br/>and party officials from the Moussa Traore regime, awaiting<br/>trial for economic crimes against the State (see Section 1.e.).<br/>In July 1991, the Government arrested Commandant Lamine Diabira<br/>and several other military personnel for plotting to overthrow<br/>the then transitional government. At year's end, Diabira had<br/>still not been charged with a crime and remained in custody.<br/>Meanwhile, the Government has released his accused<br/>coconspirators. In response to diplomatic inquiries, the<br/>Ministries of Justice and Human Rights insisted that the<br/>Government was not technically in violation of the law,<br/>although the authorities failed to clarify the legal ground for<br/>the continued imprisonment of Diabira without trial.<br/>e. Denial of Fair Public Trial<br/>The Constitution provides for the independence of the<br/>judiciary. However, the executive branch has considerable<br/>influence on the judicial system: The Ministry of Justice<br/>appoints judges and supervises both law enforcement and<br/>judicial functions, and the Superior Judicial Council, which<br/>supervises judicial activity, is headed by the President. The<br/>Supreme Court has both judicial and administrative powers. The<br/>Constitution established a separate Constitutional Court and a<br/>High Court of Justice with the power to try senior government<br/>officials in cases of treason. Except in the case of minors,<br/>trials are public, and defendants have the right to be present<br/>and to have an attorney of their choice. Defendants are<br/>presumed innocent and have the right to appeal decisions to the<br/>Supreme Court. Court-appointed attorneys are provided for the<br/>indigent.<br/>Following the overthrow of Moussa Traore in 1991, the new<br/>government arrested him, his family, and about two dozen<br/>members of his government and political party and charged them<br/>with various crimes. A court found the former president and<br/>three former government officials to be responsible for the<br/>deaths of over 100 people during the demonstrations that led to<br/>Traore's overthrow and sentenced the four to death in 1993.<br/>Although the Supreme Court denied their subsequent appeal, the<br/>President could still commute their sentences. These 4 men and<br/>17 others, including the former president's wife, son, and<br/>brother-in-law, face additional charges for economic crimes<br/>against the State.<br/>f. Arbitrary Interference with Privacy, Family, Home, or<br/>Correspondence<br/>Inviolability of the home is provided for in the Constitution<br/>and is respected in practice. Police searches are infrequent,<br/>and warrants, issued by judges, are required. The security<br/>forces maintain physical surveillance of individuals and groups<br/>suspected or known to be a threat to internal security, and,<br/>with court approval, may maintain technical surveillance as<br/>well.<br/>Section 2 Respect for Civil Liberties, Including:<br/>a. Freedom of Speech and Press<br/>The Constitution provides for freedom of speech and press. The<br/>Government controls the only television station, one radio<br/>station, and the only daily newspaper, but these all operate on<br/>a semi-independent basis and are open to a wide range of views,<br/>including those critical of the President, the Government, and<br/>other politicians. Apart from the government radio chain,<br/>there are seven independent stations in Bamako and another six<br/>independent regional stations. In spite of only a 23-percent<br/>literacy rate, there are nearly 50 independent newspapers and<br/>journals. Political meetings take place openly and without<br/>interference.<br/>The National Assembly passed new laws regulating the press.<br/>These laws provide substantial penalties, including<br/>imprisonment, for slander and for public injury to the Head of<br/>State and other officials, including foreign diplomats. Such<br/>injury is not defined in the law and therefore is subject to<br/>judicial interpretation. In a case currently under way, the<br/>President of the Supreme Court is suing the publisher of two<br/>weekly newspapers for slanderous comments.<br/>Following complaints from journalists' organizations, the<br/>Government asked the National Assembly to review all press<br/>laws, particularly those on the composition of the proposed<br/>National Press Council that will be responsible for enforcing<br/>press regulations. No results were available at year's end.<br/>Academic freedom is respected.<br/>b. Freedom of Peaceful Assembly and Association<br/>The Constitution protects the right of assembly and<br/>association. Permits must be obtained for mass demonstrations,<br/>and these are routinely granted. However, in Bamako in April,<br/>students did not obtain a permit, and their demonstrations<br/>protesting reductions in scholarships turned violent, leading<br/>to destruction of property.<br/>There are dozens of political parties and hundreds of<br/>professional and special interest associations. The<br/>Constitution forbids the formation of political parties based<br/>on religion, region, or ethnicity, and political parties must<br/>have their charters approved by the Ministry of Territorial<br/>Administration. Following the former President's overthrow,<br/>the transitional government banned the party of Moussa Traore,<br/>the Democratic Union of the Malian People (UDPM). The UDPM<br/>applied in 1993 for recognition, but the Government denied the<br/>application on a technicality. This action engendered much<br/>public debate and criticism, and the UDPM planned to appeal the<br/>Government's decision.<br/>c. Freedom of Religion<br/>Mali is a secular state. The Government does not discriminate<br/>on religious grounds. Although 90-95 percent of the population<br/>are Muslim, members of other religions freely practice their<br/>faiths and are permitted to establish houses of worship and<br/>schools. Christian missionaries of various denominations,<br/>including foreign missionaries, operate freely. Proselytizing<br/>and conversion are permitted. The Government prohibits<br/>publications in which one religious group defames another; the<br/>Minister of Territorial Administration determines whether a<br/>publication is defamatory.<br/>In October the Ministry of Territorial Administration canceled<br/>the visa of a Protestant German evangelist who had been<br/>planning to hold a number of revival meetings in Bamako later<br/>that month. The official reason for the cancellation was fear<br/>of civil unrest as the evangelist's visit to Nigeria in 1991<br/>had led to large-scale riots. However, it was widely assumed<br/>that the Government canceled the visa as a result of pressure<br/>from local Islamic organizations and associations. The press<br/>was critical of the Government's decision, calling it a<br/>violation of the Constitution.<br/>Administrative orders promulgated in 1977 prohibiting members<br/>of the Baha'i faith from meeting in groups of more than three<br/>people are not enforced, and Baha'i practice their faith<br/>without interference.<br/>d. Freedom of Movement Within the Country, Foreign<br/>Travel, Emigration, and Repatriation<br/>Freedom of movement is generally unrestricted although police<br/>checks occur in which Malians and foreigners are stopped,<br/>particularly at night. These checks are ostensibly used to<br/>restrict the movement of contraband goods and to verify vehicle<br/>registrations, but some police and gendarmes use the occasion<br/>to extort bribes. An exit visa is no longer needed.<br/>In recent years, Mali has both accepted and generated displaced<br/>persons. Approximately 13,000 Mauritanian Peuhl refugees, who<br/>fled strife in their country in 1990, settled in Mali. For the<br/>most part, they have been absorbed into the local economy.<br/>The fighting in the north between government troops and Tuareg<br/>rebels generated an estimated 100,000 Tuareg and Maur refugees,<br/>most of whom fled to Mauritania, Algeria, and Burkina Faso.<br/>With the improved security situation in the three northern<br/>regions, the flow of Malian refugees declined, and the<br/>Government, together with its neighbors and the United Nations<br/>High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR), planned to repatriate<br/>Malians refugees in those countries. However, at year's end,<br/>only a few families had returned.<br/>Initial reports of an incident on December 12 indicate that an<br/>army patrol killed three Tuareg refugees returning from<br/>Mauritania. The patrol contends that the three were bandits<br/>killed in a fire fight as the patrol attempted to apprehend<br/>them following an earlier attack on a transport convoy. The<br/>Office of the Chief of Staff of the Armed Forces and senior<br/>representatives of the rebel movement formed a joint commission<br/>to investigate the incident but had not released a report by<br/>the end of the year.<br/>Section 3 Respect for Political Rights: The Right of Citizens<br/>to Change Their Government<br/>In 1992 Malians for the first time exercised their right to<br/>change their government through peaceful means, electing a new<br/>President and a new multiparty National Assembly.<br/>The Third Republic is a multiparty democracy, based on the<br/>Constitution drawn up at the August 1991 National Conference<br/>and ratified in the January 1992 referendum. Under the<br/>Constitution, the President is Chief of State and Commander in<br/>Chief of the Armed Forces and is elected for a term of 5 years<br/>with a limit of two terms. The President appoints a Prime<br/>Minister, who is Head of Government. The National Assembly<br/>consists of 116 members, and representation is apportioned<br/>according to the population of administrative districts.<br/>Election is both direct and by party list. The Constitution<br/>provides for a separation of executive, legislative, and<br/>judicial powers and for control of the military by the<br/>President.<br/>Twenty-one political parties nationwide participated in the<br/>municipal, legislative, and presidential elections held between<br/>February and April 1992. The parties campaigned freely and had<br/>broad access to the state-owned media. Citizen participation<br/>in the elections was low but was fairly evenly distributed<br/>across most regions, ethnic groups, and between men and women.<br/>Balloting was secret.<br/>Although there were complaints about some irregularities, all<br/>parties accepted the outcome. President Alpha Oumar Konare won<br/>60 percent of the vote in the second round of presidential<br/>balloting. Konare's party, the Association for Democracy in<br/>Mali, also won the majority of seats in the National Assembly<br/>in which 10 other parties are represented. The National<br/>Assembly is still a young institution. Most legislation<br/>originates in the Government and its ministries and is approved<br/>by the Assembly, but debate on key issues is vigorous.<br/>Women are poorly represented in the political process.

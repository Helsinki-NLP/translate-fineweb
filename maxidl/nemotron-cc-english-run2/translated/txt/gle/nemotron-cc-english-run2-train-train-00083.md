| Subscribe to this blog<br/><br/>Follow by Email<br/><br/>Search This Blog<br/><br/>Plato and the goodness of the body. Part 3. Plato's cosmology and embodiment<br/><br/>II. Plato's Cosmology and Embodiment<br/><br/>A second line of argument can be made for a positive evaluation of<br/>embodiment in Plato—namely the developed, albeit cautiously offered,[1]<br/>cosmology found in one of his later works, the Timaeus. Here the divine Craftsman (demiourgos), conceived of as pure intellect (nous), creates the cosmos according to the eternal intelligible<br/>paradigms of the forms.[2]<br/>The forms should not be thought of as something existing in addition to and<br/>outside of the Craftsman—the Craftsman is the eternal divine intellect and the<br/>forms are akin to eternal ideas in his mind.[3]<br/><br/>The cosmos is a living creature (30c–31a)—indeed,<br/>a god (34b)—composed of a body and soul.[4]<br/>The human creature partly mirrors this cosmic animal in its own body-soul composition[5]<br/>so it pays to note several things about this divine cosmic creature.<br/><br/>First of all, the Craftsman forges the soul of<br/>the cosmos and then its body. It may surprise us to discover that both soul and<br/>body occupy space: the body in three dimensions and the soul in two dimensions.<br/>As soul has length and breadth but not depth it has literally no thickness at<br/>all and so, unlike the cosmic body, cannot be seen. However, as spatially<br/>extended, the soul of the world is "wrapped around" its body, and extends from<br/>the centre of the cosmos to its periphery (34b).[6]<br/>Indeed, he crafted the body "within" the soul, perfectly aligning them (36d). The<br/>integration of the cosmic body and soul are shown in that the very bodily<br/>movements of the cosmos, the orbits of the planets, are in perfect<br/>synchronicity with, indeed are bodily manifestations of, the circular movements<br/>of its intelligence, its thought, its soul. Body and soul interpenetrate. The<br/>same is true, as we will see, of the human body and soul.<br/><br/>Second, God creates the cosmos out of his<br/>overflowing goodness. "He [the Craftsman] was good, and in the good there never<br/>occurs any jealousy [or grudgingness] about anything whatsoever. Being devoid<br/>of this motive, he formed the desire that everything should become as close in<br/>nature to himself as possible" (29e).<br/><br/>Third, that the cosmos is embodied and not<br/>simply a soul is the divine intention and part of its perfection. Note that<br/>making a cosmos "as close in nature to [the Craftsman] as possible" did not<br/>result merely in a cosmic soul, but in a comic soul and body.<br/><br/>Fourth, the embodied cosmos is, in Plato's<br/>view, very good; indeed, being based by the Craftsman on the eternal forms, it<br/>is as good, beautiful, and perfect as it is possible for some empirical<br/>particular to be. "[F]or the world is the best of things that have become, and<br/>he [the Craftsman] is the best of causes" (29a);[7]<br/>"Now it was not, nor can it ever be, permitted that the work of the supremely<br/>good [i.e., the Craftsman] should be anything but that which is best<br/>. . . [W]hen he framed the universe, he fashioned reason within soul<br/>and soul within body, to the end that the work he accomplished might be by<br/>nature as excellent and as perfect as possible" (30a-b). "Having received and<br/>been filled with mortal and immortal living things, thus this cosmos, a visible<br/>living thing containing visible ones, image of the intelligible, a sensible<br/>god, greatest, best, most beautiful, and most perfect, has come to be, this<br/>which is the one only-begotten universe" (92c). Notice how overflowingly<br/>positive Plato is about the goodness of creation. It's body and soul—both of<br/>which are everlasting[8]—are arranged<br/>to function together for the greatest possible good. He is no nature-hating<br/>proto-Gnostic. In the Laws the<br/>citizens of the city must revere the earth: "The land is [our] ancestral home<br/>and [we] must cherish it even more than children cherish their mother;<br/>furthermore, the Earth is a goddess and mistress of mortal men, and the gods<br/>and spirits already established in the locality must be treated with the same<br/>respect" (Laws 5.740, cf. 6.761).<br/><br/>Fifth, as Sarah Broadie has argued convincingly,<br/>Plato's interest in the Timaeus was<br/>not to use cosmology as a way of getting at his real interest, metaphysics.<br/>Quite the contrary. His primary focus in this text was developing a cosmology,<br/>and he deployed the metaphysics as a means to that end.[9]<br/>So while it is true that Plato can use the sensible world as a stepping-stone<br/>to ascend to a knowledge of the forms, he can also reverse the trajectory and<br/>use the forms as a foundation for better understanding the sensible world. The<br/>sheer detail and attention that Plato has lavished on attempting to understand<br/>the workings of the human body and his reflections on its proper function (44d–47e;<br/>64a–69a; 69d–76e; 80d–89d) suggest that giving an account of the cosmos was his<br/>primary interest in the Timaeus.<br/><br/>And what of humans? The embodied human, a<br/>rational being that is mortal, while less perfect than the cosmos itself,[10]<br/>nevertheless is necessary for the completion and perfection of the cosmos<br/>(41b7–d3).[11]<br/>The implantation of the human souls in bodies is thus considered to be "a<br/>necessity" (42a3). Human embodiment is part of the perfection of the cosmos. In<br/>the Timaeus, embodiment is not a<br/>punishment for human failure, but essential to what humans were created to be.[12]<br/>"[T]he preincarnate state is shown not as a condition desirable in its own<br/>right but as an interlude entirely occupied with preparation for life in the body.[13]<br/>. . . This means that their mortal embodiment will come as a<br/>fulfillment, even though one fraught with dangers. The body is not to be a bunker in which it is trapped against its nature<br/>and from which its most rational wish would be to be allowed to escape"[14]Let's put the matter this way: let's suppose we<br/>put the following question to Plato: would it have been a better world if souls<br/>had never been embodied, but had been able to contemplate the forms in an<br/>unmediated way? How would he reply? Whatever we may say about earlier<br/>dialogues, the answer in the Timaeus seems<br/>clear: "Absolutely not!"<br/><br/>The soul for Plato, certainly from the middle<br/>dialogues onward, is not an undifferentiated unity, but is famously tripartite.[15]<br/>Two of these three parts of the soul are essentially tied to embodiment (Republic 518d–519a). The incarnate soul<br/>needs to maintain its embodied self and for this the appetite—the desire for<br/>food and drink and rest and sex—is required. But appetites can get out of hand<br/>and tend not to find their own healthy limits, so the spirited part of soul<br/>develops to keep them in check.[16]<br/>In a healthy person appetite is in submission to spirit, which is in submission<br/>to intellect.[17] (But the<br/>soul is intended by the divine Craftsman to be embodied, and hence to be<br/>tripartite.)<br/><br/>The immortal soul is created directly by the divine Craftsman, while the<br/>body (and hence the mortal soul) are made by created celestial gods, at the command<br/>of the Craftsman (Timaeus 41a-d).[19]<br/>These gods, we are told "built on" (prosôikondomoun)<br/>and "composed" (sunethesan) another,<br/>mortal form of soul by mixing the affections (69c9, d6). The affections in<br/>question are pleasure, pains, daring and fear, passion, hope, perception, and<br/>desire, all of which can create trouble for the soul and all of which arise of<br/>necessity from embodiment. Embodiment thus gives rise to the lesser, mortal soul.[20]<br/>The single human creature is thus a paradoxical and conflicted being, an<br/>immortal soul living a mortal life.<br/><br/>The ancillary gods, by careful and rational<br/>design, linked the different parts of the soul with different parts of the<br/>body. The head had been created to house the immortal soul. They created a<br/>buffer zone (the neck) to keep this away from the mortal soul in the torso. The<br/>organization of the body mirrored the ideal organization of the soul—the<br/>spirited part of the soul is intended to mediate the intellect's wisdom to the<br/>appetite's desires.

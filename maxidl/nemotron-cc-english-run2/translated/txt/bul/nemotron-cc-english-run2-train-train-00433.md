The atmosphere is tense, and my motivational levels are at rock bottom, as are those of the people opposite. What's happening is that I have taken on their mood of dissatisfaction.<br/><br/>2. What is making me feel uncomfortable?<br/><br/>A few years back I attended a presentation on self-defence[i] in which we were taught to utter the following sentence when faced with a type of behaviour that made us feel uneasy: 'When you …, I feel …, so I'd like you to …' By memorising this template, I realised that what was destabilising me most in these heated exchanges was that the other person opposite stopped after the first two utterances. How would I like things to be?<br/><br/>When the sentence is complete, I don't feel any hostility and have a better sense of how the other person feels threatened. I don't feel the obligation to take their moods into account. I feel that the other person is calm and intent on working to find a solution. I understand you need to manage what is going on in the company, but I'd prefer if you gave me goals to aim for. For example, you could frame the policy as follows: ….".<br/><br/>5. What can I do to ensure this 'ideal outcome' occurs?<br/><br/>That's not easy. The solution is out of my hands. It's up to the others to change. Hang on, that's not the right way of thinking! Am I not trying to rid myself of my own monkey and pass it on to someone else? So, having discussed this issue with several people, here is what I can do:<br/><br/>First, I need to ignore the calls from the other person's monkey. My technique should be RPA: Reflection, Preparation, Action. Then, I should help the people opposite me to finish their sentence.

In a small study, she and a colleague found that the circulation system in people who are highly depressed does not appear to control changes in their blood pressure very well. Such a lack of autonomic regulation can put these patients at risk of heart damage from improperly regulated heart beats.<br/><br/>The loss of this autonomic function was substantial - one-third of normal regulation - the researchers report in the March issue of the American Heart Journal.<br/><br/>But they also say there is an effective and simple treatment for patients who may be suffering from this phenomenon. These receptors are connected to the heart by nerves, which carry the message to pump faster or slower in response to pressure changes.<br/><br/>"As a result of this study, it appears that reduced baroreflex sensitivity (BRS) may be a marker of increased risk for patients with heart disease and symptoms of depression," Watkins said. Her colleague in the study was Paul Grossman, Ph.D, at the Lown Cardiovascular Center in Brookline, Mass., which funded the study.<br/><br/>"In order to demonstrate the predictive value of measuring BRS in heart patients, a larger study needs to be conducted," she said.

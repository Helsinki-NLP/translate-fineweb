Modern Unixes use 64-bit inode numbers and generally 64-bit<br/>device numbers, so you're looking at 16 bytes per hardlinked file.<br/>Since you don't want to do a linear search of a potentially very<br/>large array every time you encounter a hardlinked file, you're also<br/>going to need additional space for some sort of random access data<br/>structure. It's probably not worth keeping track of the link count,<br/>especially since it's also a 64-bit integer on at least one popular<br/>platform (Linux); you're probably losing more space than you gain<br/>back by being able to drop entries when you've seen all their links.<br/>However, all of this space usage is relatively trivial by modern<br/>standards.<br/><br/>(You can reduce the memory needs in most situations by having a<br/>two-level data structure, where you give each separate device number<br/>its own inode lookup structure. This reduces the per-file information<br/>down to one 64-bit number from two, since you're storing the device<br/>number only once instead of once per hardlinked file. Usually you'll<br/>only be dealing with a few device numbers so this is a real win. It does<br/>complicate the code a bit. I don't know if GNU du uses this approach.)<br/><br/>A program like rsync or tar has a harder problem, because it<br/>also needs to keep track of the full file name for some instance<br/>of the hardlinked file (normally it will keep track of the first<br/>one). | Macarius of Alexandria<br/><br/>Quick Facts<br/><br/>Biography<br/><br/>Saint Macarius of Alexandria (died 395) was a monk in the Nitrian Desert. He was a slightly younger contemporary of Macarius of Egypt, and is thus also known as Macarius the Younger.<br/><br/>Life<br/><br/>Macarius was born about the year 300 in Alexandria. He was a merchant until the age of 40, when he was baptized and went off into the desert. After several years of ascetic life he was ordained a presbyter and appointed prior of a monastery known as the "Kellii," or "cells" in the Egyptian desert, between the Nitria mountain and a skete in which monastic hermits lived in silence, each in his own cell.<br/><br/>About the year 335 he retired to live alone as a recluse in el-Natroun desert. Many miracles were ascribed to him. He presided over five thousand Nitric monks.<br/><br/>Having learned of the extremely strict rule for monastic life observed at the Tabbenesiot Monastery, whose prior was Venerable St. Pachomios the Great (+ 348), St. Macarius disguised himself in secular clothing, and over the course of the entire Quadragesima [the 40-day Great Lent] neither ate bread nor drank water. No one saw him eating or sitting down. He was making baskets of palm leaves while he was standing. The monks said to Saint Pachomius: "Cast out this man from here, for he is not human." A divine inspiration subsequently revealed Macarius' identity to him, and the monks rushed to receive his blessings. Having demonstrated humility and taught a lesson to all, St. Macarius returned to his own monastery.<br/><br/>At the age of 73 Macarius of Alexandria was exiled by Emperor Valens, along with Macarius of Egypt to an island, which they subsequently Christianized.<br/><br/>According to Eastern Orthodox tradition, Macarius of Alexandria departed on 2 January 395.

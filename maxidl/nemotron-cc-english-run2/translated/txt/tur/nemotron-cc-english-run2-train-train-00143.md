| Chronic Cancer Pain<br/><br/>Whether intermittent, mild, moderate, or chronic, cancer pain can have an ongoing impact on quality of life. Your odds of experiencing pain increase if your cancer is in an advanced stage or has spread from its original location. Pain can be especially severe if the location of the tumor results in nerve root compression, as may be the case with a spinal tumor. Soft tissue pain results when a tumor places added pressure on an organ or muscle and damages supporting tissues.<br/><br/>Cancer pain can be related to a number of factors, although it usually stems from a combination of biologically issues from the disease itself and the treatments. For instance, some chemotherapy drugs have side effects that include burning sensations at the injection site and tingling and numbness in feet and hands.<br/><br/>Believed to be linked to an issue with the brain's interpretation of pain, some patients experience phantom pain if a limb or other body part needs to removed due to cancer. For instance, a lump or tender spot on your skin may be sensitive to the touch and produce some degree of pain when pressed. Some patients may experience a burning sensation after undergoing radiation treatment. Less obvious symptoms associated with cancer pain may include:<br/><br/>Increased fatigue<br/><br/>Discomfort associated with movement<br/><br/>Increased sensitivity to pain following treatment<br/><br/>Dull aches that return intermittently<br/><br/>Treatments<br/><br/>The preferred treatment for cancer pain is to remove the source of the pain through surgery, radiation therapy, or chemotherapy. If stronger opioids are needed, take steps to carefully manage medications to avoid interactions and reduce the risk of addiction.<br/><br/>Some patients benefit from ongoing physiotherapy through all stages of their cancer.

| UK: Bribery Act 2010 - What's In It For Me?<br/><br/>The Bribery Act 2010 received Royal Assent on 8 April 2010. The<br/>substantive provisions of the Act are not yet in force, and are now<br/>not expected to come into force until April 2011. When the Act does<br/>come into force, it will have significant implications, in<br/>particular for businesses, and that includes those conducting<br/>business in the UK but not constituted here.<br/><br/>Everyone affected by the Act faces the challenge of reviewing<br/>and adapting their business practices and structures to safeguard<br/>themselves (and certain officers) from substantial penalties.<br/><br/>The Bribery Act 2010 creates:-<br/><br/>Two new general offences, relating to bribing or being bribed<br/>– summarised below;<br/><br/>A specific offence of bribing a foreign public official;<br/>and<br/><br/>An offence for commercial organisations which fail to prevent<br/>bribing by a person associated with them.<br/><br/>The Act reflects a generally more aggressive, and international,<br/>approach to corruption, with the result that it is likely to be<br/>enforced vigorously where offences are committed. The Act uses<br/>carefully defined terms to achieve its ends. Any overview, like<br/>this, can't fully reflect the substance of the Act.<br/>However...........<br/><br/>Bribing<br/><br/>In terms of the Act, an offence is committed if a person<br/>promises or gives financial or other advantage to another person<br/>and intends, by doing so, to induce or reward that<br/>other person for improper performance of an activity.<br/><br/>The offence covers "financial or other advantage", a<br/>term which is used consistently throughout the Act. This is about<br/>many more things than cash. The test is the<br/>intention of the bribe, not the outcome.<br/><br/>Being bribed<br/><br/>On the other side of the transaction, if it can be called that,<br/>a person is also guilty of an offence when (summarising) that<br/>person requests, agrees to receive, or accepts a financial or other<br/>advantage as a reward for improper performance, or where a person<br/>makes improper performance anticipating financial or other<br/>advantage.<br/><br/>Effect of Bribery<br/><br/>The offences of bribing or being bribed can be committed in<br/>relation to many activities:<br/><br/>functions of a public nature;<br/><br/>any activity connected with a business;<br/><br/>any activity performed in the course of a person's<br/>employment; and<br/><br/>any activity performed by or on behalf of a body of persons<br/>(corporate or not).<br/><br/>For an offence to be committed, though, the person performing<br/>the function or activity must:<br/><br/>be one who is expected to perform the function or activity in<br/>good faith and/or<br/><br/>be one who is expected to perform the function or activity<br/>impartially; or<br/><br/>be in a position of trust.<br/><br/>The concept of improper performance is at the core of the Act.<br/>The test of improper performance turns substantially on the<br/>standard of performance which may normally be expected. The test of<br/>expectation is what "a reasonable person in the United Kingdom<br/>would expect".<br/><br/>The test of expectation in the United Kingdom applies even if<br/>the function or activity concerned has no connection with the<br/>United Kingdom, or is performance in a country outside the United<br/>Kingdom. The Act is quite clear that what might be called the<br/>"When in Rome........." defence is not available. UK<br/>standards are, in effect, exported. Local custom or practice is<br/>disregarded unless permitted or required by the written law<br/>applicable to that locality.<br/><br/>Bribing foreign public officials<br/><br/>What about just "oiling the wheels" abroad,<br/>cultivating the influential? In addition to the general offences<br/>described above, the Act makes it an offence to bribe a foreign<br/>public official with the intention of influencing that official, in<br/>his or her capacity as a public official and with the intention of<br/>obtaining or retaining business, or an advantage in the conduct of<br/>business. For this offence, the Act does not refer to<br/>"improper" performance. Instead, the focus is simply on<br/>intention to influence, linked to intention to obtain or retain<br/>business or an advantage in its conduct.<br/><br/>Requirement on commercial organisations to prevent bribery<br/><br/>Perhaps there are always going to be rogue employees or<br/>consultants, people who go further than their employers would<br/>consider right. Whatever the position in the past, their activities<br/>will be a clear threat once the Act comes into force. There will be<br/>a new offence, for commercial organisations, committed if a person<br/>associated with it bribes another person with the intention either<br/>of obtaining or retaining business for the organisation, or<br/>obtaining or retaining an advantage for the organisation in the<br/>conduct of business.<br/><br/>For this purpose "bribe" means both the offering of<br/>financial or other advantage, as described above, and bribing a<br/>foreign public official, again as described above.<br/><br/>This provision applies to incorporated bodies, and partnerships,<br/>wherever formed or incorporated. The rules catch corporate or<br/>partnerships not formed in the UK, but carrying on business<br/>here.<br/><br/>Who is an "associated person"? An associated person is<br/>any person who performs services for or on behalf of the commercial<br/>organisation, in any capacity. That includes, but isn't<br/>restricted to, employees. The Act suggests agents or subsidiaries<br/>as examples of associated persons but clearly the definition does<br/>not stop there. It is not hard to see that, as further examples,<br/>consultants or contractors also fall into the definition.<br/><br/>Is there any defence? Yes - if a commercial organisation can<br/>show that it had in place adequate procedures designed to prevent<br/>associated persons from bribing. The Act does give any definition<br/>of "adequate procedures".<br/><br/>The government must publish guidance about procedures which can<br/>be put in place. It is not suggested that such procedures will by<br/>definition be adequate, but obviously, when available, the guidance<br/>will be an important benchmark. The government will shortly launch<br/>a consultation on what should be in the guidance, and say they will<br/>publish it before the Act comes into force.<br/><br/>Top-down responsibility – directors responsible for<br/>establishing a culture in which corruption is eradicated, including<br/>effective design and implementation of an anti-corruption<br/>programme;<br/><br/>A clear statement by management about the expected culture, and<br/>consequences of breaching stated ideals;<br/><br/>A senior officer appointed and given authority to put<br/>anti-corruption measures into effect, and monitor them;<br/><br/>A clear code of conduct for the organisation's business,<br/>including an anti-corruption element, publicised internally and<br/>externally;<br/><br/>A gifts and hospitality policy giving guidance to employees in<br/>relation to giving and receiving gifts and entertainment, and<br/>monitoring of all such activity;<br/><br/>Overall, the court will be expected to look at procedures and<br/>consider whether they are adequate and appropriate to the<br/>particular organisation, and to the level of risk it faces of<br/>persons associated with it committing bribery offences.<br/><br/>What about corporate hospitality?<br/><br/>The indications are that the government recognises that<br/>corporate hospitality is an accepted part of modern business<br/>practice. Corporate hospitality, however, is not excluded from the<br/>bribery offences. Reasonable corporate hospitality, within the<br/>normal range of relevant industry activity, is unlikely to cause<br/>difficulty.

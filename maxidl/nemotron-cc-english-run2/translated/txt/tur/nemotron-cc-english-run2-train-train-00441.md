| geography<br/><br/>Süderbrarup is located on the fishing peninsula . It is a sub-center and a recognized resort . In Süderbrarup there was a radium-containing spring, which was the destination of pilgrimages in pre-Christian times , but has dried up since the stadium was built.<br/><br/>history<br/><br/>The municipality has been inhabited since the Bronze Age, from which several burial mounds have been preserved. Particularly important, however, is the Kummerhy hill grave west of the Thorsberger Moors, which was first opened in 1861 and was built around 650 BC. BC and is surrounded by a stone circle. There are a total of 22 burial mounds in the area.<br/><br/>The Thorsberger Moor , from which many objects of daily use and weapons as well as decorative objects such as jewelry etc. a. were recovered, was - as the name suggests - consecrated to the Germanic god of war Thor as a place of sacrifice. It is assumed that Thorsberg was one of the most important places of worship in the region - the fact that the old name Thorsberg was not forgotten even in Christian times should indicate this. Most of the finds are kept in the Schleswig-Holstein State Museum in Gottorf Castle. About 2000 urns from the period up to AD 400 were excavated on the market square. The large number of used, jagged weapons that were sunk in the bog in the 4th century is interpreted as an indication of fighting that took place in Jutland during this time and may be related to the migration of the peoples . It is conceivable that the military conflicts were important in the context of the emigration of most of the Angles to Britain .<br/><br/>The place Süderbrarup was first mentioned in 1231 as Syndräbrathorp ( brathorp : village on a slope). On the other side of the Oxbek there is accordingly also Norderbrarup , which was the more important place until the 19th century. Labashi-Marduk 556 BC: Was of the royal lineage, the grandson of Nebuchadnezzar II (child of his daughter and<br/>her husband Neriglissar). Labashi-Marduk succeeded his father when still only a boy, and reigned for nine-months. Possibly because of his tender<br/>age, he was deemed unfit to rule, and was beaten to death in a conspiracy by the nobles of the kingdom, who then appointed one of their own, a<br/>man named Nabonidus, to the throne.<br/><br/>VI. Nabonidus 556–539 BC: One of those involved in the conspiracy to murder his predecessor, Nabonidus was<br/>appointed to the throne by his co-conspirators, but was not of the royal lineage of Nebuchadnezzar II. Nabonidus' background is not clear. He never<br/>claimed to have noble origins, in fact, in his own writings he says that he is of unimportant origins. His mother lived to old age, and was a priestess<br/>in the temple of the moon god, Sin, whom Nabonidus also worshipped, although worship of the sun god Marduk was the prevailing religion.<br/><br/>Nabonidus is considered by historians to be the last king of the Neo-Babylonian Empire. After reigning for only a few years, he left his position in<br/>Babylon, going to the oasis of Tayma to devote himself to the worship of the moon god, Sin. He appointed his son Belshazzar as his co-regent to<br/>protect and defend the kingdom in his absence. In 540 BC, in response to attacks by the Persians, led by Cyrus the Great, Nabonidus returned<br/>from Tayma to defend his kingdom. Belshazzar remained in Babylon to hold the capital, but instead held a drunken feast while his father marched<br/>his troops north to meet Cyrus. On October 10, 539 BC, Nabonidus surrendered to Cyrus. Two days later, the armies of Cyrus the Great overthrew<br/>the city of Babylon.<br/><br/>VII. Belshazzar 542 (?) - 539 BC: According to the Bible, Belshazzar was the reigning king at the time of the fall of<br/>Babylon, the last king of the Babylonian Empire. Daniel names him as the son of Nebuchadnezzar in Dan. 5: 22. (This does not necessarily mean<br/>that he is a direct descendant, however. Biblical scholars are themselves divided over his lineage, some calling him the grandson of Nebuchadnezzar,<br/>and others saying he and Nabonidus were one and the same. The Jewish historian Josephus, in Antiquities of the Jews, says that Belshazzar was<br/>another name for Nabonidus.) Most historians do not record him as ever being the king, however, naming Nabonidus as the last king of the<br/>Neo-Babylonian Empire.<br/><br/>Whatever confusion may exist about his name and kingship, the Greek historian, Herodotus, confirms that the drunken<br/>feast hosted by Belshazzar, (in the third year of his reign, according to Daniel ch. 5), occurred on October 12 of 539 BC, and that Babylon fell<br/>that night to the Medo-Persian armies of<br/>Cyrus the Great.

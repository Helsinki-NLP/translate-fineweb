| In this interview, Dr. Horstmann shares three key insights for teaching programming that every computer science instructor should add to their pedagogical playbook.<br/><br/>Let's start with the challenges of teaching programming. What do you see?<br/><br/>Dr. Horstmann Computer science involves mastering a range of subjects, but what seems to be the biggest challenge for many students is programming. Students learn by doing a lot of stuff, over and over. So literally, the way to teach loops is to make sure that students write 100 loops. And then the 101st one will not be all that hard.<br/><br/>You can't accomplish this with large, complex assignments. And I don't believe that many students can learn the material by reading through the principles and applying them by the force of logic. They will figure it out simply by habit and by practice.<br/><br/>Dr. Horstmann's Tips for Teaching Programming:<br/><br/>Assign lots of small problems<br/><br/>Start with Parson's Problems to focus on logic<br/><br/>Provide starter code to limit cognitive load<br/><br/>You're talking about the power of short assignments.<br/><br/>Dr. Horstmann Yes. When they've done something a lot of times, the next time gets a little easier, and more automatic.<br/><br/>When you start learning computer science, it's totally brand new. There's the programming environment, there's the programming language with all of its weird exceptions and minor rules, and then there's the problem you want to solve. Pretty soon you have cognitive overload.<br/><br/>If you have to think about every little decision in the language, you won't have enough brain cells left to also solve your problem. This way, students don't have to worry about getting bogged down with syntax and can focus solely on logic. I think of Parson's Problems as a kind of icebreaker activity which I like to assign at the start of a course, so students can begin to think about logic and algorithms right away.<br/><br/>You also believe in giving students "starter code." Why?<br/><br/>Dr. Horstmann Another way to avoid the blank slate problem is to give your students some starter code to get them going. And it also reduces their cognitive load. But that is not what I have ever experienced.<br/><br/>I can't remember a single project in the last 30 years where I started from nothing. I have always had prior code that serves as a starting point, and developed the project from there. So I think starting from something and modifying it is very useful.

| UK: Bribery Act 2010 - What's In It For Me?<br/><br/>The Bribery Act 2010 received Royal Assent on 8 April 2010. The<br/>substantive provisions of the Act are not yet in force, and are now<br/>not expected to come into force until April 2011. Any overview, like<br/>this, can't fully reflect the substance of the Act.<br/>However...........<br/><br/>Bribing<br/><br/>In terms of the Act, an offence is committed if a person<br/>promises or gives financial or other advantage to another person<br/>and intends, by doing so, to induce or reward that<br/>other person for improper performance of an activity.<br/><br/>The offence covers "financial or other advantage", a<br/>term which is used consistently throughout the Act. This is about<br/>many more things than cash. The test is the<br/>intention of the bribe, not the outcome.<br/><br/>Being bribed<br/><br/>On the other side of the transaction, if it can be called that,<br/>a person is also guilty of an offence when (summarising) that<br/>person requests, agrees to receive, or accepts a financial or other<br/>advantage as a reward for improper performance, or where a person<br/>makes improper performance anticipating financial or other<br/>advantage.<br/><br/>Effect of Bribery<br/><br/>The offences of bribing or being bribed can be committed in<br/>relation to many activities:<br/><br/>functions of a public nature;<br/><br/>any activity connected with a business;<br/><br/>any activity performed in the course of a person's<br/>employment; and<br/><br/>any activity performed by or on behalf of a body of persons<br/>(corporate or not).<br/><br/>For an offence to be committed, though, the person performing<br/>the function or activity must:<br/><br/>be one who is expected to perform the function or activity in<br/>good faith and/or<br/><br/>be one who is expected to perform the function or activity<br/>impartially; or<br/><br/>be in a position of trust.<br/><br/>The concept of improper performance is at the core of the Act.<br/>The test of improper performance turns substantially on the<br/>standard of performance which may normally be expected. The test of<br/>expectation is what "a reasonable person in the United Kingdom<br/>would expect".<br/><br/>The test of expectation in the United Kingdom applies even if<br/>the function or activity concerned has no connection with the<br/>United Kingdom, or is performance in a country outside the United<br/>Kingdom. The Act is quite clear that what might be called the<br/>"When in Rome........." defence is not available. UK<br/>standards are, in effect, exported. For this offence, the Act does not refer to<br/>"improper" performance.

During the 4th and 5th centuries A.D. the title was<br/>applied to both secular and ecclesiastical officials. The Theodosian Code mentions primates of towns, districts and fortified places (Primates urbium, vicorum,<br/>castellorum) . The Pragmatic Sanction of Justinian also mentions primates governing a district, primates regionis; and in this sense the title survived, under<br/>Turkish rule, in Greece until the 1pth century. An official called " primate of the palace " is mentioned in the laws of the Visigoths. Primas also seems to<br/>have been used loosely during the middle ages for " head " or " chief." The title of primate was<br/>used more generally in Africa, while elsewhere metropolitan was more generally employed. The primates in Africa differed from those elsewhere in that the title<br/>always belonged to the longest ordained bishop in a province, who had not necessarily his see in the capital, except in the case of the bishop of Carthage, who<br/>was head also of the other five African provinces. There were also three sorts of honorary primates: (i) primates aevo, the oldest bishop in a province next to<br/>the primate, on whom power devolved when the primate was disabled or disqualified; (2) titular metropolitans, the bishops of certain cities which had the name<br/>and title of civil metropoles bestowed on them by some emperor; (3) the bishops of some mother-churches which were honoured by ancient custom but were subject<br/>to the ordinary metropolitan, e.g. the bishop of Jerusalem, who was subject to his metropolitan at Caesarea.<br/><br/>At a later date " primate " became the official title of certain metropolitans who obtained from the pope a position of episcopal authority over several<br/>other metropolitans and who were, at the same time, appointed vicars of the Holy See. After the company refused to budge on both demands, the former country star's music suddenly disappeared, and hasn't been heard (on that platform) since.<br/><br/>There were surely quite a few factors that went into the decision to take her catalog off the streaming service, but the debate raised an important question: should artists get any say in when, where, and who can listen to their music after signing off on allowing Spotify playing rights?<br/><br/>Singer Taylor Swift performs during her "1989" world tour at MetLife Stadium in East Rutherford on Friday, July 10, 2015, in New Jersey. (Photo by Evan Agostini/Invision/AP)<br/><br/>The immediate reaction of many people is to say yes without giving much thought to both sides, but it is a complicated issue. If services like Spotify were to begin allowing artists to put restrictions on their music, it could very quickly complicate the service, and the overall experience would suffer. One act could refuse fans the ability to listen on a free tier for the first six months that an album is available, while another could forgo the ad-supported option entirely. Someone like Taylor Swift could attempt to broaden her global reach by offering her new album for free only to those countries where she isn't yet a huge star, while forcing others to pay for subscriptions if they really want it. That may be a good marketing tactic for her, but it could very easily backfire for the streaming service, making the entire system seem unfair.<br/><br/>Once that door is open, it would be all but impossible to close. Allowing Swift to have her way would then lead to other powerful names attempting to do the same, and from there it's open season on the streaming service. Spotify CEO Daniel Ek made a tough choice when he said no to Taylor, and while I'm sure the company would love nothing more than to have her music available, it's likely in the best interest of the business for him to continue to refuse her demands.<br/><br/>While the company isn't profitable, things are working pretty well so far for Spotify, which is quickly becoming the way the world accesses music. Despite not having the biggest album in the world this year, new users are signing up in droves, so it is unlikely to change its stance on allowing artists the right to have a say over the accessibility of their work. They can of course still choose to avoid Spotify entirely, but for many, that ends up being more of a disadvantage than a win. As sales dwindle, streaming is becoming increasingly important for all musicians, and Spotify argues that their free tier is essential to eventually convincing users to sign up as paying customers.<br/><br/>In an ideal world, artists would be allowed the freedom to distribute their work how they want, where they want, and for how much they want, but unfortunately as the music industry is still figuring out how to stay alive now that many no longer feel the need to buy products, that just isn't' the case right now.

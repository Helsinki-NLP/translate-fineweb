Here the divine Craftsman (demiourgos), conceived of as pure intellect (nous), creates the cosmos according to the eternal intelligible<br/>paradigms of the forms.[2]<br/>The forms should not be thought of as something existing in addition to and<br/>outside of the Craftsman—the Craftsman is the eternal divine intellect and the<br/>forms are akin to eternal ideas in his mind.[3]<br/><br/>The cosmos is a living creature (30c–31a)—indeed,<br/>a god (34b)—composed of a body and soul.[4]<br/>The human creature partly mirrors this cosmic animal in its own body-soul composition[5]<br/>so it pays to note several things about this divine cosmic creature.<br/><br/>First of all, the Craftsman forges the soul of<br/>the cosmos and then its body. It may surprise us to discover that both soul and<br/>body occupy space: the body in three dimensions and the soul in two dimensions.<br/>As soul has length and breadth but not depth it has literally no thickness at<br/>all and so, unlike the cosmic body, cannot be seen. However, as spatially<br/>extended, the soul of the world is "wrapped around" its body, and extends from<br/>the centre of the cosmos to its periphery (34b).[6]<br/>Indeed, he crafted the body "within" the soul, perfectly aligning them (36d). The<br/>integration of the cosmic body and soul are shown in that the very bodily<br/>movements of the cosmos, the orbits of the planets, are in perfect<br/>synchronicity with, indeed are bodily manifestations of, the circular movements<br/>of its intelligence, its thought, its soul. Body and soul interpenetrate. The<br/>same is true, as we will see, of the human body and soul.<br/><br/>Second, God creates the cosmos out of his<br/>overflowing goodness. "He [the Craftsman] was good, and in the good there never<br/>occurs any jealousy [or grudgingness] about anything whatsoever. Being devoid<br/>of this motive, he formed the desire that everything should become as close in<br/>nature to himself as possible" (29e).<br/><br/>Third, that the cosmos is embodied and not<br/>simply a soul is the divine intention and part of its perfection. Note that<br/>making a cosmos "as close in nature to [the Craftsman] as possible" did not<br/>result merely in a cosmic soul, but in a comic soul and body.<br/><br/>Fourth, the embodied cosmos is, in Plato's<br/>view, very good; indeed, being based by the Craftsman on the eternal forms, it<br/>is as good, beautiful, and perfect as it is possible for some empirical<br/>particular to be. "[F]or the world is the best of things that have become, and<br/>he [the Craftsman] is the best of causes" (29a);[7]<br/>"Now it was not, nor can it ever be, permitted that the work of the supremely<br/>good [i.e., the Craftsman] should be anything but that which is best<br/>. . . [W]hen he framed the universe, he fashioned reason within soul<br/>and soul within body, to the end that the work he accomplished might be by<br/>nature as excellent and as perfect as possible" (30a-b). "Having received and<br/>been filled with mortal and immortal living things, thus this cosmos, a visible<br/>living thing containing visible ones, image of the intelligible, a sensible<br/>god, greatest, best, most beautiful, and most perfect, has come to be, this<br/>which is the one only-begotten universe" (92c). Notice how overflowingly<br/>positive Plato is about the goodness of creation. It's body and soul—both of<br/>which are everlasting[8]—are arranged<br/>to function together for the greatest possible good. He is no nature-hating<br/>proto-Gnostic. In the Laws the<br/>citizens of the city must revere the earth: "The land is [our] ancestral home<br/>and [we] must cherish it even more than children cherish their mother;<br/>furthermore, the Earth is a goddess and mistress of mortal men, and the gods<br/>and spirits already established in the locality must be treated with the same<br/>respect" (Laws 5.740, cf. 6.761).<br/><br/>Fifth, as Sarah Broadie has argued convincingly,<br/>Plato's interest in the Timaeus was<br/>not to use cosmology as a way of getting at his real interest, metaphysics.<br/>Quite the contrary. His primary focus in this text was developing a cosmology,<br/>and he deployed the metaphysics as a means to that end.[9]<br/>So while it is true that Plato can use the sensible world as a stepping-stone<br/>to ascend to a knowledge of the forms, he can also reverse the trajectory and<br/>use the forms as a foundation for better understanding the sensible world. The<br/>sheer detail and attention that Plato has lavished on attempting to understand<br/>the workings of the human body and his reflections on its proper function (44d–47e;<br/>64a–69a; 69d–76e; 80d–89d) suggest that giving an account of the cosmos was his<br/>primary interest in the Timaeus.<br/><br/>And what of humans? The embodied human, a<br/>rational being that is mortal, while less perfect than the cosmos itself,[10]<br/>nevertheless is necessary for the completion and perfection of the cosmos<br/>(41b7–d3).[11]<br/>The implantation of the human souls in bodies is thus considered to be "a<br/>necessity" (42a3). Human embodiment is part of the perfection of the cosmos. In<br/>the Timaeus, embodiment is not a<br/>punishment for human failure, but essential to what humans were created to be.[12]<br/>"[T]he preincarnate state is shown not as a condition desirable in its own<br/>right but as an interlude entirely occupied with preparation for life in the body.[13]<br/>. . . This means that their mortal embodiment will come as a<br/>fulfillment, even though one fraught with dangers. Thus . . . the<br/>mortal soul will enter into the mortal condition as if into its inheritance . .<br/>. . How would he reply? Whatever we may say about earlier<br/>dialogues, the answer in the Timaeus seems<br/>clear: "Absolutely not!"<br/><br/>The soul for Plato, certainly from the middle<br/>dialogues onward, is not an undifferentiated unity, but is famously tripartite.[15]<br/>Two of these three parts of the soul are essentially tied to embodiment (Republic 518d–519a). The incarnate soul<br/>needs to maintain its embodied self and for this the appetite—the desire for<br/>food and drink and rest and sex—is required. But appetites can get out of hand<br/>and tend not to find their own healthy limits, so the spirited part of soul<br/>develops to keep them in check.[16]<br/>In a healthy person appetite is in submission to spirit, which is in submission<br/>to intellect.[17] (But the<br/>soul is intended by the divine Craftsman to be embodied, and hence to be<br/>tripartite.)<br/><br/>The immortal soul is created directly by the divine Craftsman, while the<br/>body (and hence the mortal soul) are made by created celestial gods, at the command<br/>of the Craftsman (Timaeus 41a-d).[19]<br/>These gods, we are told "built on" (prosôikondomoun)<br/>and "composed" (sunethesan) another,<br/>mortal form of soul by mixing the affections (69c9, d6). The affections in<br/>question are pleasure, pains, daring and fear, passion, hope, perception, and<br/>desire, all of which can create trouble for the soul and all of which arise of<br/>necessity from embodiment. Embodiment thus gives rise to the lesser, mortal soul.[20]<br/>The single human creature is thus a paradoxical and conflicted being, an<br/>immortal soul living a mortal life.<br/><br/>The ancillary gods, by careful and rational<br/>design, linked the different parts of the soul with different parts of the<br/>body. The head had been created to house the immortal soul. They created a<br/>buffer zone (the neck) to keep this away from the mortal soul in the torso. The<br/>organization of the body mirrored the ideal organization of the soul—the<br/>spirited part of the soul is intended to mediate the intellect's wisdom to the<br/>appetite's desires. It is located between the head and the lower torso so that<br/>"it might be within hearing of the discourse of reason and join with it in<br/>restraining by force the desires, whenever they should not willingly consent to<br/>obey the word of command from the citadel" (70a4–7). The heart, the location of<br/>spirit, sends blood around the body to communicate between the intellect and<br/>the body as a whole. The liver, with its smooth shiny surface, is intended to<br/>reflect back reason's image. Johansen observes that the plan of the ancillary<br/>gods was to take the disruptive rectilinear movements that humans undergo[21]<br/>and to make them part of the body's rational order. Even the disturbing motions<br/>are given a teleological purpose (i.e., nourishing the body, 70d-e) and made<br/>subject to the rule of reason. In infancy we are in a state of deep confusion (Tim. 42e–44d), and without proper education and training we will<br/>grow into de-formed adults (as explored at length in the Republic). Being a good human being is a very difficult challenge.<br/>Yet even the struggle may be part of the divine plan. Justice in an individual, according to the Republic, simply is the right ordering of the soul. And, according to the Timaeus, when the soul is rightly<br/>ordered in this way the lower parts of the soul play an important role in<br/>contributing to the purposes of the intellect. The mortal soul, the parts that<br/>arise from embodiment, are not simply restrained, but actually positively<br/>contribute to the good human life. It is necessary to pay<br/>attention to the needs of both soul and<br/>body. And we need to appreciate that these two are not unrelated. The<br/>philosopher rulers of the Republic<br/>are required to train their bodies, not simply for the sake of their bodies,<br/>but also as a way to train the spirited part of the soul (Republic 410b-c).<br/><br/>As a final aside, the interpenetration of the<br/>parts of the soul finds a social analogy in the city of the Republic. Být dobrým člověkem je velmi těžká výzva.<br/>Sarah Broadie navrhla, že boje duše, aby se orientovala na ztělesnění života a na jeho řád, jsou součástí dobra, které řemeslník zamýšlel.<br/><br/>Řemeslník má plán, že duše by měla být uspořádána hierarchicky s choutkami podrobenými duchu a duchu podrobenému intelektu. Smrtelná duše, části, které vznikají z vtělení, nejsou jednoduše omezeny, ale ve skutečnosti pozitivně přispívají k dobrému lidskému životu. Nepořádek duše může způsobit zmatek – klíčové téma v republice – ale to by nás nemělo uvést v omyl, že Platón považoval nižší části duše, části spojené s vtělením, za špatné, pouze že jsou špatné, když nehrají správnou roli v lidském životě. Existuje způsob racionality, který proniká do nejnižších částí duše, které mohou spolupracovat s rozumem, a proto je platonická racionalita ztělesněnou racionalitou.<br/><br/>Psychosomatická jednota lidské bytosti je také odhalena v Platónově analýze lidské nemoci.

But that does not make it meaningful. Digital cameras are a case in point. So how do you decide which one to buy? If you believe most advertising, only one figure matters, the number of megapixels. But what are they?<br/><br/>Put simply, megapixels are the millions of dots on a light-sensitive chip which a digital camera uses instead of film. There is a parallel with printing, in that if you look at the pictures in this newspaper you will see that they are made up of dots. A glossy magazine's pictures will look sharper, largely because they contain more and smaller dots. This is "resolution".<br/><br/>That does not mean, however, that the number of megapixels is all you should consider when buying a digital camera; that would be the same as saying that the quality of a non-digital camera was defined simply by the type of film it could use. In fact, it is possible to take perfectly good pictures with even a very cheap film camera.<br/><br/>Until recently, this was not true of digital cameras. Enlarge a digital picture and it will eventually break up or "pixelate". A one-megapixel camera cannot really produce prints bigger than 5"x7" without severe distortion. But now even some key-ring sized cameras have resolutions better than this.<br/><br/>Further up the scale, digital cameras have started to match their film counterparts in pricing scales. It costs about 280.<br/><br/>For an additional 50 or so, the S304 looks more like an SLR film camera. It has more exposure modes, a 6X zoom and a continuous shooting function.<br/><br/>Jump another 200 or so and you can buy the S602. This is a serious camera. The important point is it should be possible to take pictures as good with the cheapest of these three cameras as with the most expensive. In practical terms, a 3.1-megapixel camera's prints can generally be enlarged up to 16"x12" without distortion.<br/><br/>The only pity is that it is now much harder to choose a digital camera.

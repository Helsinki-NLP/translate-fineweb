This conflict gave rise to the<br/>beginning of World War I.<br/><br/>The first of the conflicts, between the British and the Muslim Sudanese, exemplified<br/>the dedication of time, resources, and life exerted by the European powers in their race<br/>to control Africa and out-do the other European nations. Britain, having assumed control<br/>of Egypt in 1882, was presented with the problem of the Egyptian- controlled Sudan, an<br/>Islamic land south of Egypt where the Nile's tributaries are located. In a revolt against<br/>the increasing Christian influence brought by the British, manifested mainly in an<br/>attempt to end the historic Muslim slave trade discovered by Livingstone, led by Muslim Muhammad<br/>Ahmad, who claimed to be the "Mahdi" (Muslim messiah delivered to free the<br/>followers of Islam from non-believers), Islamic armies battled the British in attempt to<br/>create an independent Muslim commonwealth. The Egyptian armies, under British control,<br/>were defeated at El Obeid (1883) and Khartoum (1885), and the Sudan was independent until<br/>1898, when the British regained control at Omdurman. These engagements demonstrated the<br/>British determination to control Africa in light of tremendous opposition from a native<br/>source.<br/><br/>The second conflict of the Sudan Wars exemplified the British resolve to stand fast<br/>against another European power of equal determination- France. France controlled African<br/>lands in the east, namely Somaliland, and in the west, Senegal. A major French goal was to<br/>secure an east-west belt of control between the two possessions. After lending aid to<br/>Ethiopia in its defense against Italy, France exercised much control over Ethiopia and<br/>used it as a staging ground for exploration into the southern Sudan, namely the upper Nile<br/>area, to find a corridor for the belt of influence. This belt of influence conflicted with<br/>the British goal of establishing a railway from Cairo to Cape Town, which required a<br/>north-south belt of control running through southern Sudan. France, having been deploying<br/>General Marchand from French West Africa on missions to "explore" the area, was<br/>infringing on the British control of the area. The British deployed their army from Egypt,<br/>under the command of Kitchener, and the two armies met at Fashoda in 1898. A full-scale<br/>war was averted by diplomatic intervention, which arrived at a settlement to partition the<br/>disputed area. Britain received east Sudan with Egypt, while France gained control of the<br/>expanse from the Congo Darfur.<br/><br/>The willingness of the European powers to exert such resources into retaining<br/>possessions in Africa showed the intense rivalry the countries had with each other.<br/>Fighting African natives and even themselves demonstrated the stubborn tenacity the<br/>nations bore in pursuing their goals, and when these goals were not realized, national<br/>pride was affected.

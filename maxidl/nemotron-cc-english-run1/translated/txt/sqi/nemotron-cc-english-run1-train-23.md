The conflicts erupted between the British<br/>and the Muslims Africans in the Sudan in 1881 because of religious disputes and the<br/>British attempt to end the "new" slavery trade. Muslim fundamentalists<br/>instigated a rebellion against the British, driving them and their Egyptian armies from<br/>Sudan. This conflict gave rise to the<br/>beginning of World War I.<br/><br/>The first of the conflicts, between the British and the Muslim Sudanese, exemplified<br/>the dedication of time, resources, and life exerted by the European powers in their race<br/>to control Africa and out-do the other European nations. Britain, having assumed control<br/>of Egypt in 1882, was presented with the problem of the Egyptian- controlled Sudan, an<br/>Islamic land south of Egypt where the Nile's tributaries are located. In a revolt against<br/>the increasing Christian influence brought by the British, manifested mainly in an<br/>attempt to end the historic Muslim slave trade discovered by Livingstone, led by Muslim Muhammad<br/>Ahmad, who claimed to be the "Mahdi" (Muslim messiah delivered to free the<br/>followers of Islam from non-believers), Islamic armies battled the British in attempt to<br/>create an independent Muslim commonwealth. The Egyptian armies, under British control,<br/>were defeated at El Obeid (1883) and Khartoum (1885), and the Sudan was independent until<br/>1898, when the British regained control at Omdurman. These engagements demonstrated the<br/>British determination to control Africa in light of tremendous opposition from a native<br/>source.<br/><br/>The second conflict of the Sudan Wars exemplified the British resolve to stand fast<br/>against another European power of equal determination- France. France controlled African<br/>lands in the east, namely Somaliland, and in the west, Senegal. A major French goal was to<br/>secure an east-west belt of control between the two possessions. After lending aid to<br/>Ethiopia in its defense against Italy, France exercised much control over Ethiopia and<br/>used it as a staging ground for exploration into the southern Sudan, namely the upper Nile<br/>area, to find a corridor for the belt of influence. This belt of influence conflicted with<br/>the British goal of establishing a railway from Cairo to Cape Town, which required a<br/>north-south belt of control running through southern Sudan.

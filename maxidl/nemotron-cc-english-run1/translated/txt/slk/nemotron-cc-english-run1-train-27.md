| Multiple sequence alignment<br/><br/>First 90 positions of a protein multiple sequence alignment of instances of the acidic ribosomal protein P0 (L10E) from several organisms. Generated with ClustalW.<br/><br/>A multiple sequence alignment (MSA) is a sequence alignment of three or more biological sequences, generally protein, DNA, or RNA. In general, the input set of query sequences are assumed to have an evolutionary relationship by which they share a lineage and are descended from a common ancestor. From the resulting MSA, sequence homology can be inferred and phylogenetic analysis can be conducted to assess the sequences' shared evolutionary origins. Visual depictions of the alignment as in the image at right illustrate mutation events such as point mutations (single amino acid or nucleotide changes) that appear as differing characters in a single alignment column, and insertion or deletion mutations (or indels) that appear as gaps in one or more of the sequences in the alignment. Multiple sequence alignment is often used to assess sequence conservation of protein domains, tertiary and secondary structures, and even individual amino acids or nucleotides.<br/><br/>Multiple sequence alignment also refers to the process of aligning such a sequence set. Because three or more sequences of biologically relevant length are nearly impossible to align by hand, computational algorithms are used to produce and analyze the alignments. MSAs require more sophisticated methodologies than pairwise alignment because they are more computationally complex to produce. Most multiple sequence alignment programs use heuristic methods rather than global optimization because identifying the optimal alignment between more than a few sequences of moderate length is prohibitively computationally expensive.<br/><br/>Dynamic programming and computational complexity<br/><br/>The most direct method for producing an MSA uses the dynamic programming technique to identify the globally optimal alignment solution. For proteins, this method usually involves two sets of parameters: a gap penalty and a substitution matrix assigning scores or probabilities to the alignment of each possible pair of amino acids based on the similarity of the amino acids' chemical properties and the evolutionary probability of the mutation. For nucleotide sequences a substitution matrix can be used, but since there are only four possible standard characters per sequence and the individual nucleotides do not typically differ much in substitution probability, the parameters for DNA and RNA sequences usually consist of a gap penalty, a positive score for character matches, and a negative score for mismatches.<br/><br/>For n individual sequences, the method requires constructing the n-dimensional equivalent of the matrix formed in standard pairwise dynamic programming. The search space thus increases exponentially with increasing n and is also strongly dependent on sequence length. To find the global optimum for n sequences this way has been shown to be an NP-complete problem.[1][2] Methods to reduce the search space by first performing pairwise dynamic programming on each pair of sequences in the query set and searching only the solution space near these results (effectively finding the intersection between local paths immediately surrounding each pairwise optimum solution) render the dynamic programming technique more efficient. The so-called "sum of pairs" method has been implemented in the software package MSA, but it is still impractical for many MSA applications that require the simultaneous alignment of dozens or even a few hundred sequences. Dynamic programming methods are now used only when an extremely high-quality alignment of a small number of sequences is needed, and as a benchmarking standard in evaluating new or refined heuristic techniques.<br/><br/>Progressive alignment construction<br/><br/>One method of performing a heuristic alignment search is the progressive technique (also known as the hierarchical or tree method) that builds up a final MSA by first performing a series of pairwise alignments on successively less closely related sequences. Such methods begin by aligning the two most closely related sequences first and then successively aligning the next most closely related sequence in the query set to the alignment produced in the previous step. The initial "most related" pair is determined by an efficient clustering method such as neighbor-joining based on a simple heuristic search of the query set with a tool like FASTA. Progressive techniques therefore automatically construct a phylogenetic tree as well as an alignment.<br/><br/>One major limitation of progressive methods is their heavy dependence on the initial assignment of relatedness and on the quality of the initial alignment. The methods are thus sensitive as well to the distribution of sequences in the query set; performance improves when relatedness among query sequences is a relatively smooth gradient rather than distantly separated clusters. Performance also degrades significantly when all of the sequences in the set are rather distantly related, because inaccuracies in the initial alignment are then more likely. Most modern progressive methods modify their scoring function with a secondary weighting function that assigns scaling factors to individual members of the query set in a nonlinear fashion based on their phylogenetic distance from their nearest neighbors. Judicious choice of weighting can aid in evaluating relatedness and mitigate the effects of relatively poor initial alignments early in the progression.<br/><br/>Progressive alignment methods are efficient enough to implement on a large scale for many sequences and are often run on publicly accessible web servers so users need not locally install the applications of interest. A very popular progressive alignment method is the Clustal family,[3] especially the weighted variant ClustalW[4] to which access is provided by a large number of web portals including GenomeNet, EBI, and EMBNet. Different portals or implementations can vary in user interface and make different parameters accessible to the user. Clustal is used extensively for phylogenetic tree construction and as input for protein structure prediction by homology modeling.<br/><br/>Another common progressive alignment method called T-Coffee[5] is slower than Clustal and its derivatives but generally produces more accurate alignments for distantly related sequence sets. T-coffee calculates pairwise alignments by combining the direct alignment of the pair with indirect alignments that aligns each sequence of the pair to a third sequence. It uses the output from Clustal as well as another local alignment program LALIGN, which finds multiple regions of local alignment between two sequences. The resulting alignment and phylogenetic tree are used as a guide to produce new and more accurate weighting factors.<br/><br/>Because progressive methods are heuristics that are not guaranteed to converge to a global optimum, alignment quality can be difficult to evaluate and their true biological significance can be obscure. A very recent semi-progressive method that improves alignment quality and does not use a lossy heuristic while still running in polynomial time[6] has been implemented in the program PSAlign.<br/><br/>Iterative methods<br/><br/>A set of methods to produce MSAs while reducing the errors inherent in progressive methods are classified as "iterative" because they work similarly to progressive methods but repeatedly realign the initial sequences as well as adding new sequences to the growing MSA. One reason progressive methods are so strongly dependent on a high-quality initial alignment is the fact that these alignments are always incorporated into the final result - that is, once a sequence has been aligned into the MSA, its alignment is not considered further. This approximation improves efficiency at the cost of accuracy. By contrast, iterative methods can return to previously calculated pairwise alignments or sub-MSAs incorporating subsets of the query sequence as a means of optimizing a general objective function such as finding a high-quality alignment score.<br/><br/>A variety of subtly different iteration methods have been implemented and made available in software packages; reviews and comparisons have been useful but generally refrain from choosing a "best" technique.[7] The software package PRRN/PRRP uses a hill-climbing algorithm to optimize its MSA alignment score[8] and iteratively corrects both alignment weights and locally divergent or "gappy" regions of the growing MSA.[9] PRRP performs best when refining an alignment previously constructed by a faster method. The alignment of individual motifs is then achieved with a matrix representation similar to a dot-matrix plot in a pairwise alignment. An alternative method that uses fast local alignments as anchor points or "seeds" for a slower global-alignment procedure is implemented in the CHAOS/DIALIGN suite.[10]<br/><br/>A third popular iteration-based method called MUSCLE (multiple sequence alignment by log-expectation) improves on progressive methods with a more accurate distance measure to assess the relatedness of two sequences.[11] The distance measure is updated between iteration stages (although, in its original form, MUSCLE contained only 2-3 iterations depending on whether refinement was enabled).<br/><br/>Hidden Markov models<br/><br/>Hidden Markov models are probabilistic models that can assign likelihoods to all possible combinations of gaps, matches, and mismatches to determine the most likely MSA or set of possible MSAs. HMMs can produce a single highest-scoring output but can also generate a family of possible alignments that can then be evaluated for biological significance. Because HMMs are probabilistic, they do not produce the same solution every time they are run on the same dataset; thus they cannot be guaranteed to converge to an optimal alignment. HMMs can produce both global and local alignments. Although HMM-based methods have been developed relatively recently, they offer significant improvements in computational speed, especially for sequences that contain overlapping regions.[9]<br/><br/>Typical HMM-based methods work by representing an MSA as a form of directed acyclic graph known as a partial-order graph, which consists of a series of nodes representing possible entries in the columns of an MSA. In this representation a column that is absolutely conserved (that is, that all the sequences in the MSA share a particular character at a particular position) is coded as a single node with as many outgoing connections as there are possible characters in the next column of the alignment. In the terms of a typical hidden Markov model, the observed states are the individual alignment columns and the "hidden" states represent the presumed ancestral sequence from which the sequences in the query set are hypothesized to have descended. A efficient search variant of the dynamic programming method, known as the Viterbi algorithm, is generally used to successively align the growing MSA to the next sequence in the query set to produce a new MSA.[12] This is distinct from progressive alignment methods because the alignment of prior sequences is updated at each new sequence addition. However, like progressive methods, this technique can be influenced by the order in which the sequences in the query set are integrated into the alignment, especially when the sequences are distantly related.[9]<br/><br/>Several software programs are available in which variants of HMM-based methods have been implemented and which are noted for their scalability and efficiency, although properly using an HMM method is more complex than using more common progressive methods. The simplest is POA (Partial-Order Alignment)[13]; a similar but more generalized method is implemented in the package SAM (Sequence Alignment and Modeling System).[12] SAM has been used as a source of alignments for protein structure prediction to participate in the CASP structure prediction experiment and to develop a database of predicted proteins in the yeast species S. cerevisiae. The method works by breaking a series of possible MSAs into fragments and repeatedly rearranging those fragments with the introduction of gaps at varying positions. A general objective function is optimized during the simulation, most generally the "sum of pairs" maximization function introduced in dynamic programming-based MSA methods. Naproti tomu iteračné metódy sa môžu vrátiť k predtým vypočítaným párovým zarovnaniam alebo sub-MSA, ktoré zahŕňajú podmnožiny sekvencie dotazu ako prostriedok na optimalizáciu všeobecnej objektívnej funkcie, ako je nájdenie vysokokvalitného zarovnania.<br/><br/>Boli implementované a sprístupnené v softvérových balíkoch rôzne metódy iterácie; recenzie a porovnania boli užitočné, ale vo všeobecnosti sa zdržali výberu "najlepšej" techniky.[7] Softvérový balík PRRN/PRRP používa algoritmus na optimalizáciu svojho skóre zarovnania MSA[8] a iteratívne koriguje obe zarovnania a lokálne rozdielne alebo "gappy" regióny rastúcej MSA.[9] PRRP najlepšie funguje, keď bolo skonštruované rýchlejšie zarovnanie. Alternatívna metóda, ktorá používa rýchle miestne zarovnanie ako kotviace body alebo "semená" pre pomalší postup globálneho zarovnania, je implementovaná v sade CHAOS / DIALIGN.<br/><br/>Tretia populárna metóda založená na iterácii nazývaná MUSCLE (viacnásobné zarovnanie sekvencií podľa očakávaní logov) zlepšuje progresívne metódy s presnejším meraním vzdialenosti na posúdenie príbuznosti dvoch sekvencií.[11] Meranie vzdialenosti sa aktualizuje medzi iteračnými fázami (aj keď v pôvodnej forme MUSCLE obsahovalo iba 2-3 iterácie v závislosti od toho, či bolo povolené zjemnenie).<br/><br/>Skrytý Markov modely<br/><br/>Skryté Markovove modely sú pravdepodobnostné modely, ktoré môžu priradiť pravdepodobnosť všetkým možným kombináciám medzier, zápasov a nesúladov na určenie najpravdepodobnejšieho MSA alebo množiny možných MSA. HMM môžu produkovať jediný výstup s najvyšším skóre, ale môžu tiež vytvoriť rodinu možných zarovnaní, ktoré potom môžu byť vyhodnotené z hľadiska biologického významu. Pretože HMM sú pravdepodobnostné, nevytvárajú rovnaké riešenie zakaždým, keď sú spustené na rovnakom súbore údajov; preto nie je možné zaručiť, že konvergujú k optimálnemu zarovnaniu. HMM môžu vytvárať globálne aj lokálne zarovnania. Hoci metódy založené na HMM boli vyvinuté relatívne nedávno, ponúkajú významné zlepšenie výpočtovej rýchlosti, najmä pre sekvencie, ktoré obsahujú prekrývajúce sa oblasti.<br/><br/>Typické metódy založené na HMM pracujú tak, že reprezentujú MSA ako formu riadeného acyklického grafu známeho ako graf čiastočného poradia, ktorý sa skladá z radu uzlov predstavujúcich možné položky v stĺpcoch MSA. V tomto znázornení stĺpec, ktorý je absolútne zachovaný (to znamená, že všetky sekvencie v MSA zdieľajú konkrétny znak na konkrétnej pozícii) je kódovaný ako jeden uzol s toľkými odchádzajúcimi pripojeniami, ako sú možné znaky v nasledujúcom stĺpci zarovnania. Z hľadiska typického skrytého Markovovho modelu sú pozorované stavy jednotlivé zarovnacie stĺpce a "skryté" stavy predstavujú predpokladanú pôvodnú sekvenciu, z ktorej sa predpokladá, že sekvencie v množine dotazov zostúpili. Efektívny vyhľadávací variant metódy dynamického programovania, známy ako Viterbiho algoritmus, sa vo všeobecnosti používa na postupné zosúladenie rastúcej MSA s ďalšou sekvenciou v dotaze nastavenom na vytvorenie novej MSA.[12] Toto je odlišné od progresívnych metód zosúladenia, pretože zosúladenie predchádzajúcich sekvencií sa aktualizuje pri každom pridaní novej sekvencie. Avšak, rovnako ako progresívne metódy, táto technika môže byť ovplyvnená poradím, v ktorom sú sekvencie v množine dotazov integrované do zarovnania, najmä ak sú sekvencie vzdialene súvisiace.<br/><br/>K dispozícii je niekoľko softvérových programov, v ktorých boli implementované varianty metód založených na HMM a ktoré sú známe svojou škálovateľnosťou a účinnosťou, hoci správne použitie metódy HMM je zložitejšie ako použitie bežnejších progresívnych metód. Najjednoduchšie je POA (Partial-Order Alignment)[13]; podobná, ale viac generalizovaná metóda je implementovaná v balíku SAM (Sequence Alignment and Modeling System).[12] SAM sa používa ako zdroj zarovnania pre predikciu štruktúry proteínov, aby sa podieľal na experimente predpovedania štruktúry CASP a vytvoril databázu predpovedaných proteínov v kvasinkách druhu S. cerevisiae. Metódy HMM môžu byť tiež použité pre vyhľadávanie databázy pomocou HMMer.<br/><br/>Genetické algoritmy a simulované žíhanie<br/><br/>Štandardné optimalizačné techniky v informatike - ktoré boli inšpirované, ale nie priamo reprodukovať, fyzikálne procesy - boli tiež použité v snahe efektívnejšie produkovať kvalitné MSA. Na tejto technike, genetické algoritmy, boli použité pre produkciu MSA v snahe široko simulovať predpokladaný evolučný proces, ktorý viedol k divergencii v súbore dotazov. Metóda funguje tak, že rozbije sériu možných MSA na fragmenty a opakovane preskupuje tieto fragmenty so zavedením medzier v rôznych pozíciách. Všeobecná objektívna funkcia je optimalizovaná počas simulácie, najčastejšie "súčet párov" maximalizačná funkcia zavedená v dynamických metódach MSA založených na programovaní. Technika pre proteínové sekvencie bola implementovaná v softvérovom programe SAGA (Sequence Alignment by Genetic Algorithm) a jej ekvivalent v RNA sa nazýva RAGA.<br/><br/>Technika simulovaného žíhania, ktorou je existujúce MSA vyrobené inou metódou rafinované radom preskupení navrhnutých tak, aby našli optimálnejšie oblasti zarovnania priestoru, ako je tá, ktorú už zaberá zarovnanie vstupov. Rovnako ako metóda genetického algoritmu, simulované žíhanie maximalizuje objektívnu funkciu, ako je funkcia sum-of-pairs. Simulované žíhanie používa metaforický "teplotný faktor", ktorý určuje rýchlosť, s akou prebiehajú preskupenia a pravdepodobnosť každej preskupenia; typické použitie strieda obdobia vysokých mier preskupenia s relatívne nízkou pravdepodobnosťou (preskúmanie vzdialenejších regiónov zarovnania priestoru) s obdobiami nižších sadzieb a vyšších pravdepodobností dôkladnejšie preskúmať miestne minimá v blízkosti novo "kolonizovaných" regiónov. Tento prístup bol implementovaný v programe MSASA (Multiple Sequence Alignment by Simulated Annealing).<br/><br/>Nález motívu<br/><br/>Zarovnanie siedmich Drosophilacaspasses farebných motívmi, ktoré identifikoval MEME. Keď sú pozície motívov a zarovnanie sekvencií generované nezávisle, často dobre korelujú, ale nie dokonale, ako v tomto príklade.<br/><br/>Motif finding, tiež známy ako profilová analýza, je metóda lokalizácie motívov sekvencií v globálnych MSA, ktorá je prostriedkom na vytvorenie lepšieho MSA a prostriedkom na vytvorenie hodnotiacej matice na použitie pri hľadaní iných sekvencií podobných motívov. Boli vyvinuté rôzne metódy izolácie motívov, ale všetky sú založené na identifikácii krátkych vysoko zachovaných vzorov v rámci väčšieho zarovnania a konštrukcii matrice podobnej substitučnej matrici, ktorá odráža aminokyselinové alebo nukleotidové zloženie každej pozície v predpokladanom motíve. Zarovnanie môže byť potom rafinované pomocou týchto matríc. V štandardnej analýze profilu matica obsahuje položky pre každý možný znak, ako aj položky pre medzery.[9] Alternatívne môžu štatistické algoritmy na hľadanie vzorov identifikovať motívy ako predchodcu MSA, a nie ako odvodenie. V mnohých prípadoch, keď súbor dotazov obsahuje len malý počet sekvencií alebo obsahuje len vysoko súvisiace sekvencie, pridajú sa pseudopočítania na normalizáciu rozdelenia, ktoré sa odráža v matici bodovania. Najmä to opravuje položky s nulovou pravdepodobnosťou v matici na hodnoty, ktoré sú malé, ale nenulové.<br/><br/>Analýza blokov je metóda nájdenia motívu, ktorá obmedzuje motívy na nezarovnané oblasti v zarovnaní. Bloky môžu byť generované z MSA alebo môžu byť extrahované z nezarovnaných sekvencií pomocou vopred vypočítanej sady spoločných motívov, ktoré boli predtým generované zo známych génových skupín.[18] Blokové bodovanie sa vo všeobecnosti spolieha skôr na rozstupy vysokofrekvenčných znakov než na výpočet explicitnej substitučnej matice.

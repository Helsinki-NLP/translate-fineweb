| One of the largest national parks in Indonesia, Kerinci Seblat protects almost 14,000 sq kilometres of tropical rain forests in central western Sumatra running down the Barisan mountain range and its foothills and covering parts of four provinces. Terrains varies from lowland forests up to the peak of mighty Mt Kerinci at 3,805 m.<br/><br/>The national park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Rainforest Heritage of Sumatra and one of the most important tiger reserves in the world.<br/><br/>The national park was formed from a collection of watershed protection forests (hutan lindung) and nature reserves and was established in 1982 although its boundaries were not legally formalised until the mid 1990s.<br/><br/>The park is dominated by the Barisan mountains. Scenery in many areas is very spectacular with active and dormant volcanoes including Mount Kerinci, at 3805m the highest mountain in Sumatra and Indonesia's highest active volcano. The national park forests protect the headwaters of some of Sumatra's most important rivers which flow from springs and peat swamps like Ladeh Panjang (Kerinci district) and Danau Kebut (Merangin district) high in the mountains, as well as many lakes and wetlands.<br/><br/>The Great Sumatra Fault runs through the centre of the national park and forms the densely populated Kerinci valley, which lies in the middle of and is surrounded by national park forests.<br/><br/>Kerinci Seblat is most famous amongst bird-watchers as the best place to see most of the highland Sumatran endemic bird species including the Schneider's Pitta, Salvadore's pheasant and Sumatran Cochoa, all presumed extinct for much of the 20th century before being rediscovered here. More than 375 species of bird have been recorded to date.<br/><br/>This is also the most important location in Sumatra for wild Sumatran Tiger and one of the 12 most important tiger reserves anywhere in the world although seeing wild tigers is unusual due both to the dense forest and the animals' shy nature. Other fauna includes elephants (best seen in national park forests in Bengkulu), clouded leopards, tapirs, sun bears and at least seven species of primate.<br/><br/>Forest edge farmers continue to report occasional sightings of the mysterious orang pendek, a large, bipedal cryptozoological primate resembling an orangutan (which are not recorded in Kerinci Seblat).<br/><br/>The national park also houses the biggest and the tallest flowers in the world, the monstrous, flesh red flower of the parasite Rafflesia arnoldi which can grow up to a metre in diameter, and is best searched for in the Bengkulu area of the park - ask for flowering information in Curup. In the southern part of Kerinci district hope to see the slightly smaller Rafflesia hasselti which is a vivid dark red in colour. The huge Amorphophallus titanum and Amorphophallus gigas are also present and can grow up to 4 metres in height. A remarkable flower at higher altitudes on Mt Kerinci and Mt Tujuh is the Javanese edelweiss Anaphalis javanica, which only grows on volcanoes. This shrub can reach more than two metres in height and is colored white-green because of its small hairs; the flowers are yellow with white. Numerous orchids are also found, most often flowering at the beginning of the rainy season in late September or October.<br/><br/>Rainfall is heaviest between October-December and from February-April while May-August is mainly dry but with some occasional rains, there are no major variations in seasonal temperature. Photographers may wish to avoid the period July-August as these dry months are often hazy. Because much of the park is above 700m in altitude, evenings and nights tend to be cool while in the high mountains temperatures may occasionally drop as low as 5C at night and so trekkers should be prepared for cool evenings<br/><br/>Despite the Jambi province having an area name Kerinci, the closest way to get there is actually from Padang. Most visitors arrive from Padang's Minangkabau International Airport which offers quickest access for the Kerinci area of the national park (seven hours). Jambi (capital of Jambi province) is 9 hours away from Kerinci. Bengkulu airport (domestic flights from Jakarta) is better for visits to North Bengkulu (elephants) and the Curup area of the national park (rafflesia).<br/><br/>You may want to call your hotel or homestay in advance to arrange for personal transport (you can hire a car, van, or bus) for your trip, however there are good 'Travel' minibus and Share Taxi services from Padang and Jambi and Bengkulu to district capitals which border the park. Padang to Kerinci (Sungaipenuh) or Bangko (Merangin district) is a six-seven hour bus drive, Jambi city to Bangko five hours, Bengkulu city to Curup three hours while it is four hours to the elephant sanctuary at Seblat from Bengkulu city.<br/><br/>You should bring a photocopy of your passport, which is to be given to the authorities to process your national park entry permit. Visitors planning to climb Mt Kerinci can get a photocopy made at the village of Kersik Tuo at the foot of the mountain. Photocopies of your passport and the visa section are also useful if staying in a village as your homestay must report visitors to the village headman or local police post<br/><br/>The price for entry to the national park as of February 2012 is 20,000 Rupiah per person. Buy tickets at the national park headquarters in Sungaipenuh, Kerinci, at Kersiktua, at Curup (near Bengkulu) or in Bangko if you are planning to visit Renah kemumu or the national park forests in Merangin district.<br/><br/>There are frequent travel minibus services between district capitals, usually leaving in the morning or evening. Angkot minibuses serve villages to and from district capitals. Roads in many areas are poorly maintained so travelling even a relatively short distance can take time, and especially so in the rainy season. It is possible to hire a car and driver in many of the district capitals - expect to pay about $40 a day plus petrol and driver's food.<br/><br/>You can also hire a self0drive motor bike or grab a ride on an ojek motorbike taxi - always negotiate a price before, ask help from your hotel if necessary.<br/><br/>Megaliths in the Kerinci valley and at remote and rarely visited Renah Kemumu village in Merangin district.<br/><br/>Lake Gunung Tujuh. But it only spews the horrid smell once in a while. (updated Feb 2017)<br/><br/>Mount Kerinci. The namesake mountain is the highest volcano in Indonesia at 3,805 meters (nearly 12,500 feet) above sea level. Climbing it takes at least 2 days and 1 night including the descent. Look at the<br/>differences to determine what makes one book a winner, while the other is<br/>only mediocre.<br/><br/>Don't be afraid tackle<br/>difficult subject matter. Young<br/>people today are very savvy about what's going on in the world. Middle-grade fiction can include content<br/>on lying, bullying, jealousy, sibling rivalry, divorce, peer pressure,<br/>drug abuse, domestic violence, and suicide. These are all subjects many kids have<br/>had to deal with either directly or with family members, friends, or<br/>classmates. Just be sure to deal<br/>with these subjects in an appropriate manner.<br/><br/>Do make your characters<br/>slightly older than your targeted audience. Kids like to read about other kids a<br/>year or two older than they are. When I taught a writing class to 4th, 5th,<br/>and 6th grade students, I was always surprised by how old the<br/>characters were in the stories they chose to write. Growing up intrigues this age group.<br/><br/>Don't preach to your readers<br/>or talk down to them. They aren't<br/>toddlers or preschoolers. These are kids who know a lot about the world<br/>they live in and are exposed to so much through television and the<br/>Internet. Treat them as they want to be treated.<br/><br/>Do create three-dimensional<br/>characters. Know what your<br/>character looks like: keep a descriptive page handy so your character<br/>doesn't have red hair in chapter one and brown in chapter five. Know what<br/>they're good at: keep a list so you'll know if you're character is a<br/>computer whiz or can hit a baseball to outer field. Know what flaws your<br/>character has: make a chart listing things like easily angered, a poor<br/>student, too thin or overweight, etc.<br/>Know what quirks your character has: list things like a stutter,<br/>eye rubbing if she's tired, allergies which make him sneeze, an eye<br/>twitch, etc.<br/><br/>Don't forget to use the five<br/>senses: sight, smell, hear, taste, and touch. Bring your story to life with vivid<br/>descriptions: enticing aromas or horrific odors, owls hooting or cars<br/>crashing, Mom's yummy spaghetti cooking or the moldy sandwich the homeless<br/>guy pulls from the trash, and the soft velvet fur of a puppy or the slimy<br/>trail of a slug.<br/><br/>Do inject humor into your<br/>story, even those dealing with difficult topics. For example in Ghost for Rent, while our<br/>characters are adjusting to the fact their parents are separating, my main<br/>character's brother teases her by putting fake spiders in her sandwich and<br/>fake worms down her back.<br/><br/>Don't leave your characters<br/>hanging. Move your story forward by<br/>creating obstacles for your characters to solve by themselves, without an<br/>adult's help, and be sure they solve those problems by the end of the<br/>story.<br/><br/>Do spend time with children<br/>in your targeted age group. Listen<br/>to their conversations. Observe their clothes and their behaviors. Without this first-hand information, it<br/>will be difficult for you to create a story and characters to which your<br/>readers will relate. Even if you don't have children in your immediate<br/>circle, volunteer at a school, community center, church, or scout group.<br/><br/>Don't ignore<br/>guidelines. After your story is<br/>completed do a search for publishers who might be interested in your<br/>work. Check out their catalogs to<br/>see what kinds of books they are publishing. Read their submission guidelines<br/>carefully. They are there for a<br/>reason. If they want Times New<br/>Roman, font size 12, be sure that's what you send to them. Edit your rough draft. Put it away. Edit it again. Ask unbiased people to read the<br/>manuscript and comment on it. Take<br/>their comments and make necessary adjustments. Let the manuscript sit for a week, then<br/>re-read it and make final edits. Be<br/>sure the manuscript is formatted to the specifications of the submissions'<br/>guidelines and send it off.<br/><br/>If<br/>you've followed my suggestions, you have a good chance of finding a publisher<br/>for your work, but remember, it takes patience, a well-written and grammatically<br/>correct manuscript, and above all, being in the right place, at the right time,<br/>with the right story. Laissez le manuscrit reposer pendant une semaine, puis relisez-le et effectuez les modifications finales. Assurez-vous que le manuscrit est formaté selon les spécifications des soumissions.<br/>Lignes directrices et l'envoyer.<br/><br/>Si vous avez suivi mes suggestions, vous avez de bonnes chances de trouver un éditeur pour votre travail, mais rappelez-vous, il faut de la patience, un manuscrit bien écrit et grammaticalement correct, et surtout, être au bon endroit, au bon moment, avec la bonne histoire.

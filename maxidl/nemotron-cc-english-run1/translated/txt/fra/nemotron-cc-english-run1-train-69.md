After filling each artificial flower with sugar water to simulate a real flower's nectar, they arranged equal numbers of curved and flat flowers on a square grid.<br/><br/>They then allowed hawk moths to fly freely around the artificial flowers for five minutes and compared how many of each flower shape the moths emptied.<br/><br/>Like a garden hose<br/><br/>"With their long proboscis and nocturnal habits, finding a flower's nectar source isn't easy for the fist-sized hawk moths we used in our study," Campos says.<br/><br/>"Imagine being given a garden hose that's almost twice your height in length.

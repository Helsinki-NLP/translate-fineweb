13% of those between 12 and 19.<br/><br/>All youth up to age 19 from low-income families are twice as likely to have untreated cavities compared with children from higher-income households.<br/><br/>Understandably, pain and other symptoms from cavities and poor oral health may lead to more school absences and lower grades, explains Brandy Lipton, an assistant professor in SDSU's School of Public Health.<br/><br/>"Research suggests that good oral health in children may improve school performance and have a major and lasting impact on overall health, medical expenses, and quality of life through adulthood," said Lipton.<br/><br/>Lipton drilled down further on the association between adult Medicaid dental coverage and oral health outcomes in children of low-income families in a comprehensive study published in Health Affairs, which describes a surprising finding: a lag time of one to two years from the onset of parental dental benefits to improved oral health care outcomes in their children.<br/><br/>"A key finding from this study is that adult dental coverage does improve oral health outcomes among children in low-income families, but it takes some time to increase dental care visits and improve oral health," explained Lipton.<br/><br/>"Non-cost barriers like oral health literacy, access to transportation, or taking time off from work for a dental visit could be contributing factors to the lag time," she said.<br/><br/>Analyzing 18 years of data across the U.S. that detailed thousands of kids' dental visits, untreated cavities, and oral health analysis of existing research, offering adult dental coverage through Medicaid may have long-lasting health impacts on children whose parents gain benefits," said Lipton.<br/><br/>Lipton's research
